Section 1 — Elasticity & Hooke's Law
🔩
Elasticity, Stress & Strain
How materials deform — and spring back
▾

Elasticity is the property of a material by virtue of which it tends to regain its original shape and size after the deforming force is removed. If it cannot, it is called plastic.

Hooke's Law & Key Definitions
Stress = Force / Area  ·  Strain = Change / Original
Hooke's Law: Within the elastic limit, Stress ∝ Strain → Stress / Strain = Modulus of Elasticity (constant)

Young's Modulus (Y): Longitudinal stress / longitudinal strain  ·  unit: N/m² (Pa)
Bulk Modulus (B): Volumetric stress / volumetric strain (liquids & solids)
Modulus of Rigidity (G): Shear stress / shear strain (solids only)
Key Definitions — Frequently Tested
  • Elastic limit — maximum stress beyond which a material does not return to its original shape (permanent deformation begins)
  • Yield point — stress at which material starts to deform plastically without further increase in load
  • Breaking stress (Ultimate stress) — maximum stress a material can withstand before breaking
  • Ductile materials — large plastic deformation before breaking (e.g., copper, mild steel)
  • Brittle materials — little or no plastic deformation before breaking (e.g., glass, cast iron)
  • Steel is more elastic than rubber — steel returns to shape with less deformation for the same stress
⚑ Classic Exam Trap
  • Steel is MORE elastic than rubber — NOT less. Elasticity = ability to resist deformation and return to original shape. Steel has a much higher Young's modulus and returns to shape more precisely. Rubber stretches a lot, which seems "elastic" in everyday language, but physically it is LESS elastic.
  • Stress is force per unit area (like pressure); Strain is dimensionless (a pure ratio)
Practical Example

Bridges and buildings use steel beams precisely because steel has a high Young's modulus — it does not deform significantly under large loads. Rubber bands, despite their large deformation, have low Young's modulus and are physically less elastic.

Section 2 — Pressure in Fluids
🌊
Pressure — Definition & Fluid Pressure
How pressure works in liquids and gases
▾

Pressure is defined as the force exerted per unit area. In a fluid (liquid or gas), pressure acts in all directions equally at a given depth.

Pressure Formulas
P = F/A   ·   P = ρgh
P = Pressure (Pa = N/m²)  ·  F = Force (N)  ·  A = Area (m²)
ρ = density of fluid (kg/m³)  ·  g = 9.8 m/s²  ·  h = depth (m)

Fluid pressure depends only on depth, not on the shape or volume of the container.
Key Properties of Fluid Pressure
  • Pressure at a point in a fluid acts equally in all directions (Pascal's property)
  • Pressure increases with depth — deeper → greater pressure
  • Pressure depends on density of the fluid — denser fluid → more pressure at same depth
  • Pressure is independent of the shape of the container (Hydrostatic paradox)
  • At the same horizontal level in a connected fluid, pressure is equal everywhere
  • SI unit: Pascal (Pa) = 1 N/m²  ·  Also: 1 atm = 101,325 Pa ≈ 10⁵ Pa
Hydrostatic Paradox

A narrow tall vase and a wide shallow bowl filled to the same height with water exert the same pressure at the bottom (P = ρgh — shape doesn't matter). But the total force on the wide bowl's base is greater because its area is larger. This is the "hydrostatic paradox."

⚑ Pressure vs Force — Exam Trap
  • Pressure = F/A — a sharp knife cuts more easily (same force, tiny area → huge pressure)
  • Camel walks on sand without sinking — large foot area → low pressure per unit area
  • A woman in high heels exerts more pressure than an elephant — her heel area is tiny
⚖️
Pascal's Law
Hydraulic machines — force multiplication principle
▾

Pascal's Law states that pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally and undiminished in all directions throughout the fluid.

Pascal's Law — Statement

"A change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted without diminishment to all portions of the fluid and to the walls of the container."

This means if you press on one part of an enclosed liquid, the pressure increases by the same amount everywhere in the liquid.

Hydraulic Machine (Pascal's Law Applied)
F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂  →  F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁)
A small force F₁ on a small piston (area A₁) creates pressure P = F₁/A₁.
This same pressure acts on a large piston (area A₂), producing a large force F₂.
Force is multiplied — but note: work input = work output (energy conservation). The large piston moves a shorter distance.
Applications of Pascal's Law — All Tested
  • Hydraulic lift / jack — used in service stations to lift cars and heavy vehicles
  • Hydraulic press — used in industries to compress, stamp, and shape metal
  • Hydraulic brakes — in vehicles; pressing brake pedal transmits pressure to all four wheels equally
  • Hydraulic crane — construction machinery
  • Dental chair, barber chair — height adjusted using hydraulic system
⚑ Exam Trap — Pascal's Law
  • Pascal's law multiplies force, NOT energy or work. Work done by both pistons is equal (energy is conserved). What you gain in force, you lose in distance.
  • The law applies only to enclosed, incompressible fluids (liquids, not gases, for practical machines)
🚢
Archimedes' Principle & Buoyancy
Why ships float and why objects feel lighter in water
▾

Archimedes' Principle: When a body is wholly or partially immersed in a fluid, it experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by it.

Buoyant Force
F_b = ρ_fluid × V_submerged × g
F_b = buoyant force (upthrust)  ·  ρ_fluid = density of fluid
V_submerged = volume of body submerged in fluid  ·  g = 9.8 m/s²

Law of Floatation: A body floats when the buoyant force = weight of the body.
This means: ρ_body × V_body × g = ρ_fluid × V_submerged × g
ConditionRelationshipResult
ρ_body < ρ_fluidBuoyant force > WeightBody floats (partially submerged)
ρ_body = ρ_fluidBuoyant force = WeightBody remains suspended anywhere in fluid
ρ_body > ρ_fluidBuoyant force < WeightBody sinks
Real-World Applications
  • Ships float despite being made of steel — the hollow interior means average density is less than water
  • Icebergs — approximately 1/9 of an iceberg is visible above water (density of ice ≈ 0.9 × density of water)
  • Submarines — use ballast tanks; fill with water to sink, expel water to rise
  • Hydrometer — measures density/relative density of liquids using Archimedes' principle
  • Hot air balloon — hot air inside is less dense; average density < surrounding air → buoyant force > weight → rises
  • Apparent weight in water = actual weight − buoyant force (objects feel lighter)
Relative Density (Specific Gravity)
  • Relative Density (RD) = Density of substance / Density of water = Weight in air / Loss of weight in water
  • Water has RD = 1  ·  Ice: 0.9  ·  Mercury: 13.6  ·  Aluminium: 2.7  ·  Iron: 7.8
  • Relative Density is a pure number — no units
⚑ Exam Traps — Archimedes
  • Buoyant force depends on the volume submerged and the fluid density — NOT on the weight or density of the object itself
  • An ice cube floating in a glass of water — when it melts, the water level stays the same (ice displaces exactly its own weight of water, and melted water has the same mass)
  • Dead Sea is extremely salty (dense) → humans float easily without effort
  • A body weighs least in mercury (densest common liquid) — largest buoyant force
Section 3 — Fluid Dynamics & Bernoulli's Theorem
✈️
Bernoulli's Theorem
Why faster-moving fluid has lower pressure — and how planes fly
▾

Bernoulli's Theorem states that for an ideal, incompressible fluid in steady flow, the total energy per unit volume (sum of pressure energy, kinetic energy, and potential energy) remains constant along a streamline.

Bernoulli's Equation
P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant
P = pressure  ·  ½ρv² = kinetic energy per unit volume  ·  ρgh = potential energy per unit volume

Key implication: Where velocity is high, pressure is low — and vice versa.
This is an expression of conservation of energy for fluid flow.
Applications of Bernoulli's Theorem — Most Tested
  • Aerofoil / Aeroplane wing — curved upper surface → air flows faster over the top → lower pressure above wing → net upward lift force (aerodynamic lift)
  • Venturimeter — device to measure flow speed of a fluid in a pipe; narrowed section increases velocity → decreased pressure (pressure difference measured)
  • Carburetor — in petrol engines; fast-moving air creates low pressure that draws in petrol vapour
  • Atomiser / Sprayer / Perfume spray — fast air over a tube creates low pressure → liquid drawn up and sprayed
  • Bunsen burner — fast gas flow draws in air for combustion
  • Curve ball in cricket/baseball — spinning ball creates unequal air speeds on two sides → pressure difference → ball curves (Magnus effect, related to Bernoulli)
  • Blowing between two sheets of paper — they come together (low pressure between them)
Think of it this way

Imagine water flowing through a garden hose that narrows at the nozzle. At the narrow part, water must flow faster (same amount must pass through a smaller area). That faster-moving water has lower pressure — which is why the spray at the nozzle is powerful but doesn't "push back" on the hose walls much.

⚑ Exam Traps — Bernoulli
  • Bernoulli's theorem is valid for ideal (non-viscous), incompressible, steady (streamline) flow only
  • Higher velocity → lower pressure (many students get this backwards)
  • Aeroplane lift is due to pressure difference (Bernoulli), NOT because air "pushes up" on the flat bottom
  • Continuity equation (A₁v₁ = A₂v₂) is related — narrower pipe → higher velocity
Section 4 — Surface Tension & Capillarity
💧
Surface Tension
Why water forms droplets and insects walk on water
▾

Surface tension is the tendency of the free surface of a liquid to contract to the minimum possible area, behaving like a stretched elastic membrane. It arises due to intermolecular attractive forces (cohesion) among liquid molecules.

Surface Tension Definition
T = F / L
T = surface tension (N/m)  ·  F = force acting along the surface  ·  L = length of the liquid surface
Also defined as: T = Surface energy / Area (J/m²)
Surface tension decreases with increasing temperature (except for molten copper and cadmium).
Factors Affecting Surface Tension
  • Temperature increases → surface tension decreases (molecules have more energy, cohesion weakens)
  • Adding soap / detergent → surface tension decreases significantly (surfactants disrupt cohesion)
  • Adding salt to water → surface tension slightly increases
  • Contamination / impurities → generally decreases surface tension
  • At critical temperature of a liquid → surface tension becomes zero
Phenomena Due to Surface Tension
  • Water droplets are spherical — sphere has minimum surface area for a given volume
  • Insects walk on water (e.g., water strider) — their weight is supported by surface tension
  • Needle floats on water if placed gently — steel is denser than water but surface tension supports it
  • Soap bubbles are spherical and last for a while
  • Camphor dances on water — camphor dissolves unevenly, reducing surface tension on one side, pulling the camphor
  • Oil spreads on water — oil has lower surface tension than water
  • Hot water washes better than cold — lower surface tension means better wetting
Why Soap Reduces Surface Tension

Soap molecules have a hydrophilic head (attracted to water) and a hydrophobic tail (repelled by water). They sit at the water surface with tails sticking out, disrupting the cohesive bonds between water molecules and dramatically lowering surface tension. This makes soapy water spread and penetrate fabric much better — which is why soap cleans clothes.

⚑ Exam Traps — Surface Tension
  • Surface tension decreases with temperature — hot water has lower surface tension than cold water
  • Soap decreases surface tension — that's why soap bubbles are possible (they need the surface to stretch) and why soap is a good cleaner
  • A soap bubble has two surfaces (inner + outer) — excess pressure inside = 4T/r. A liquid drop has one surface — excess pressure = 2T/r
🌿
Capillarity (Capillary Action)
How water rises in thin tubes — and in plants
▾

Capillarity (or capillary action) is the rise or fall of a liquid in a narrow tube (capillary) due to the combined effects of adhesion (liquid–solid attraction) and cohesion (liquid–liquid attraction) and surface tension.

Capillary Rise Formula
h = 2T cos θ / (ρgr)
h = height of rise  ·  T = surface tension  ·  θ = contact angle
ρ = density of liquid  ·  g = gravitational acceleration  ·  r = radius of capillary tube

h ∝ 1/r — narrower tube → greater capillary rise
Water in Glass Rises

Water wets glass (adhesion > cohesion). Contact angle < 90°. Meniscus is concave. Liquid rises in capillary.

Mercury in Glass Falls

Mercury does NOT wet glass (cohesion > adhesion). Contact angle > 90°. Meniscus is convex. Liquid falls in capillary.

Applications of Capillarity
  • Water transport in plants — water moves up from roots to leaves through narrow xylem tubes via capillary action
  • Blotting paper / sponge — soaks up water through tiny capillary spaces
  • Oil lamp / candle wick — oil / wax rises up the wick to the flame
  • Towel drying — absorbs water through capillary spaces in fabric fibres
  • Ink in fountain pen — capillary action draws ink to the nib
  • Soil moisture — water rises from deep soil to surface through capillary action in soil particles
⚑ Mercury Falls — Exam Trap

Mercury falls in a glass capillary (unlike water which rises) because mercury's cohesion is stronger than its adhesion to glass. The contact angle for mercury–glass is about 140°, so cos θ is negative → the height formula gives a negative value → liquid level is depressed below the outside level.

Section 5 — Viscosity
🫙
Viscosity & Stokes' Law
Internal friction of fluids — why honey flows slower than water
▾

Viscosity is the property of a fluid that opposes relative motion between its layers — it is the internal friction of a fluid. Honey is more viscous than water; water is more viscous than air.

Viscous Force (Newton's Law of Viscosity) & Stokes' Law
F = ηA(dv/dx)   ·   F_stokes = 6πηrv
η (eta) = coefficient of viscosity (Pa·s or Poise)  ·  1 Poise = 0.1 Pa·s
dv/dx = velocity gradient between layers
Stokes' Law: viscous drag on a sphere of radius r moving at speed v through fluid of viscosity η
Terminal velocity: when drag force + buoyancy = weight → v_t = 2r²(ρ−σ)g / 9η
Effect of Temperature on Viscosity
  • Liquids — viscosity DECREASES with temperature (honey flows more easily when warm)
  • Gases — viscosity INCREASES with temperature (gas molecules move faster, more intermolecular collisions)
  • This is the opposite behaviour — a key exam comparison
  • Unit of viscosity: Poise (CGS) or Pa·s (SI)  ·  1 Pa·s = 10 Poise
Terminal Velocity — Concept

When a raindrop (or any sphere) falls through air, it accelerates until the viscous drag + buoyancy = weight. At this point, net force = 0 and velocity becomes constant — called terminal velocity. Larger drops reach higher terminal velocities. This is why raindrops fall at a steady speed rather than accelerating forever.

⚑ Liquid vs Gas Viscosity with Temperature
  • Liquid viscosity decreases with temperature (cooking oil becomes runnier when heated)
  • Gas viscosity increases with temperature (opposite of liquids)
  • This distinction is a DIRECT exam question in CDS/NDA/AFCAT — do not confuse the two
Section 6 — Density, States of Matter & Thermal Expansion
🧊
Density, Specific Gravity & States of Matter
Key densities, anomalous expansion of water, and matter properties
▾
Density & Relative Density
ρ = m / V   ·   RD = ρ_substance / ρ_water
ρ = density (kg/m³)  ·  SI unit: kg/m³  ·  CGS unit: g/cm³
RD = Relative Density (no units)  ·  Water density = 1000 kg/m³ = 1 g/cm³
RD numerically equals density in g/cm³.
Important Densities — Frequently Tested
  • Water — 1000 kg/m³ (1 g/cm³)  ·  maximum density at 4°C
  • Ice — 900 kg/m³ (less than water → floats)
  • Mercury — 13,600 kg/m³ (13.6 g/cm³) — densest common liquid
  • Air — ≈ 1.2 kg/m³  ·  Steel — ≈ 7800 kg/m³  ·  Gold — ≈ 19,300 kg/m³
  • Osmium — densest known element ≈ 22,590 kg/m³
Anomalous Expansion of Water — Critical Concept

Water has a unique property: it expands on cooling below 4°C (instead of contracting like most substances). Water has its maximum density at 4°C. Below 4°C, it expands; at 0°C it freezes and ice forms with even lower density (floats on water).

Why this matters: In winter, lakes freeze from the top down, not the bottom up. The ice layer at the top insulates the water below, allowing aquatic life to survive. If water behaved normally (denser when colder), water would sink to the bottom and freeze from the bottom up — killing all aquatic life.

⚑ Anomalous Expansion — Exam Trap
  • Water is densest at 4°C, not 0°C. At 0°C it is ice with lower density.
  • Ice floats because it is less dense than liquid water — not because it is lighter in some other sense
  • Pipes burst in winter because water expands when it freezes (unusual for a solid)
🌡️
Thermal Expansion
How solids, liquids, and gases expand with heat
▾

Almost all materials expand on heating and contract on cooling. The degree of expansion differs between solids, liquids, and gases.

Expansion Formulas
ΔL = αLΔT  ·  ΔA = βAΔT  ·  ΔV = γVΔT
α = coefficient of linear expansion  ·  β = coefficient of areal expansion  ·  γ = coefficient of volumetric expansion
Relationship: β = 2α  ·  γ = 3α
For gases at constant pressure: V ∝ T (Charles' Law) — much larger expansion than solids/liquids
Applications of Thermal Expansion
  • Railway tracks — gaps left between sections to allow for expansion in summer (no gaps → tracks buckle)
  • Bimetallic strip — two metals bonded together; different expansion rates cause bending on heating → used in thermostats, fire alarms, circuit breakers
  • Pendulum clock runs slow in summer — rod expands, pendulum becomes longer, time period increases (T = 2π√(L/g))
  • Rivets — heated and inserted; as they cool and contract, they clamp parts together tightly
  • Glass jars — run hot water over a stuck metal lid; metal expands faster than glass → lid loosens
  • Invar — alloy with very low coefficient of expansion, used in precision instruments and watches
  • Tyres burst in summer — gas inside expands; if pressure is already high, tyre may burst (Charles' Law)
⚑ Pendulum Clock in Summer — Exam Trap
  • In summer, a pendulum clock runs slow (loses time) — the rod expands, making the pendulum longer, increasing time period
  • In winter, it runs fast (gains time) — rod contracts, shorter pendulum, shorter time period
  • γ = 3α (volumetric = 3 × linear) — a basic but frequently tested relationship
