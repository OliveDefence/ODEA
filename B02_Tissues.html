The Study of Tissues
From individual cells to organised groups — how the body builds its functional layers. Essential for CDS, NDA & AFCAT.
CDS NDA AFCAT Plant Tissues Animal Tissues

A tissue is a group of similar cells that work together to perform a specific function. Tissues are the next level of organisation above cells — cells → tissues → organs → organ systems → organism. The study of tissues is called Histology.

Section 1 — What is a Tissue?
🔬
Definition & Levels of Organisation
Where tissues fit in the hierarchy of life
▾

Tissues arise when cells of the same type group together and become specialised for a common task. This division of labour makes multicellular organisms far more efficient.

Think of it this way

If cells are soldiers, then a tissue is a regiment. All soldiers in the regiment are trained for the same job — together they're far more effective than operating alone.

Levels of Biological Organisation
  • Cell → smallest unit of life
  • Tissue → group of similar cells performing one function
  • Organ → group of tissues performing a related set of functions
  • Organ System → group of organs working together
  • Organism → complete, independent living being
Histology — The Science of Tissues

Histology (from Greek: histos = tissue, logos = study) is the branch of biology that studies the microscopic structure of tissues. It is also called microscopic anatomy. Marcello Malpighi is regarded as the father of histology.

Section 2 — Plant Tissues
🌿
Classification of Plant Tissues
Meristematic vs Permanent — the key division
▾

Plant tissues are broadly divided into two types based on their ability to divide:

Cell divisionActive — cells keep dividingNo division — cells are fixed
Cell wallThin, celluloseThin or thick depending on type
VacuoleSmall or absentLarge central vacuole
CytoplasmDense, no intercellular spacesMay have intercellular spaces

⚑ Exam Trap

Meristematic cells are NOT differentiated — they are undifferentiated (like stem cells). Once a meristematic cell matures and stops dividing, it becomes a permanent tissue cell. This distinction is frequently tested.

🌱
Meristematic Tissue — Types & Locations
Apical, Lateral & Intercalary
▾

Based on their position in the plant, meristematic tissues are classified into three types:

Apical Meristem Primary Growth

Found at the tips of roots and shoots. Responsible for increase in length of the plant.

Lateral Meristem Secondary Growth

Found along the sides (vascular & cork cambium). Responsible for increase in girth (width).

Intercalary Meristem Leaf base / nodes

Found at the base of leaves and internodes. Helps grasses and monocots grow after grazing or cutting.

Memory Trick — ALI
  • Apical = tip = length
  • Lateral = sides = girth
  • Intercalary = internodes = re-growth
🌾
Permanent Tissues — Simple Tissues
Parenchyma, Collenchyma & Sclerenchyma
▾

Simple permanent tissues are made of only one type of cell. There are three types:

Parenchyma

Parenchyma is the most common plant tissue. Cells are thin-walled, living, loosely packed with large intercellular spaces.

Functions & Key Points
  • Storage of food (starch, water)
  • When it contains chloroplasts → called Chlorenchyma (photosynthesis)
  • When it has large air cavities → called Aerenchyma (buoyancy in aquatic plants)
  • Provides turgidity and softness to the plant
Collenchyma

Collenchyma cells have unevenly thickened cell walls (at corners). Cells are living.

Functions & Key Points
  • Provides mechanical support with flexibility to young stems and leaves
  • Found in the leaf stalks (petioles) and young stems
  • Cells are elongated with irregular, corner-thickened walls
  • Living cells — can also do photosynthesis when containing chloroplasts
Sclerenchyma

Sclerenchyma cells have uniformly thick, lignified walls. Cells are dead at maturity.

Functions & Key Points
  • Provides rigidity and hardness — makes plant body hard and stiff
  • Found in seed coats, nutshells, coconut husk fibres
  • Two types: Fibres (elongated) and Sclereids / Stone cells (isodiametric)
  • Dead cells — no cytoplasm at maturity

Cell wallThin, celluloseUnevenly thick (corners)Uniformly thick, lignified
Living/DeadLivingLivingDead
Intercellular spacesLargeFewAbsent
FunctionStorage, photosynthesisFlexible supportRigid support
⚑ Top Exam Trap
  • Sclerenchyma = dead cells. Parenchyma and collenchyma = living cells. This is the most commonly tested fact.
  • Chlorenchyma is a modified parenchyma — NOT a separate tissue type.
  • Collenchyma is found in dicot stems and leaf petioles only — NOT in roots or monocot stems (which have sclerenchyma instead).
💧
Permanent Tissues — Complex Tissues
Xylem & Phloem — the Vascular System
▾

Complex permanent tissues are made of more than one type of cell working together. The two main types are Xylem and Phloem — together they form the vascular bundle of a plant.

Xylem — Water Conducting Tissue

Xylem conducts water and dissolved minerals upward from roots to leaves (upward, unidirectional transport). It is also called wood.

Components of Xylem (4 types)
  • Tracheids — elongated, dead cells with tapering ends; main water-conducting element in gymnosperms
  • Vessels (Xylem vessels) — wider, dead cells joined end-to-end forming tubes; main water-conducting element in angiosperms
  • Xylem Fibres — dead cells providing mechanical support
  • Xylem Parenchyma — only living cells in xylem; stores food, assists in lateral conduction
Phloem — Food Conducting Tissue

Phloem conducts food (sucrose) both up and down the plant (bidirectional transport). It is also called bast.

Components of Phloem (4 types)
  • Sieve Tubes — main food-conducting cells; living but no nucleus at maturity; have sieve plates at ends
  • Companion Cells — living cells that help sieve tubes function (sieve tubes lack nucleus, so companion cells assist)
  • Phloem Fibres — dead cells for support; commercial fibres like jute, flax come from here
  • Phloem Parenchyma — living cells; storage and lateral conduction
FeatureXylemPhloem
What it transportsWater + mineralsFood (sugars/sucrose)
DirectionUpward only (unidirectional)Both directions (bidirectional)
Main conducting cellsTracheids, Vessels (dead)Sieve tubes (living, no nucleus)
Living/dead cellsMostly dead (except xylem parenchyma)Mostly living (except phloem fibres)
Also calledWoodBast
⚑ Exam Traps — Xylem & Phloem
  • Xylem parenchyma = living. All other xylem cells = dead. Don't say "all xylem cells are dead."
  • Sieve tubes are living but have no nucleus at maturity — assisted by companion cells.
  • Xylem = unidirectional (up only). Phloem = bidirectional. This is very frequently tested.
  • Jute, hemp, flax, coir are all obtained from phloem fibres — frequently asked in exam context.
Section 3 — Animal Tissues
🏗️
Epithelial Tissue
The covering and lining tissue
▾

Epithelial tissue covers the external surface of the body and lines the internal cavities of organs. Cells are tightly packed with very little intercellular space, and rest on a basement membrane.

Think of it this way

Epithelial tissue is like the tiles on a floor — cells fit tightly together to form a continuous protective layer, preventing anything from getting through unless allowed.

Types of Epithelial Tissue — By Shape
  • Squamous (Pavement) — flat, scale-like cells. Found in: skin, walls of blood capillaries, lung alveoli. Function: diffusion, filtration.
  • Cuboidal — cube-shaped cells. Found in: kidney tubules, salivary glands. Function: secretion, absorption.
  • Columnar — tall, column-shaped cells. Found in: inner lining of intestine, stomach. Function: secretion, absorption.
  • Ciliated — columnar cells with cilia. Found in: trachea, fallopian tubes. Function: moves mucus/particles.
  • Glandular — specialised for secretion. Forms glands (sweat, saliva, etc.).
⚑ Exam Trap

Epithelial tissue is avascular (no blood vessels). It gets nutrients by diffusion from underlying connective tissue. This is a commonly tested fact.

🕸️
Connective Tissue
Binding, supporting and transporting tissue
▾

Connective tissue is the most abundant tissue in the body. It connects, supports, and binds other tissues. Unlike epithelial tissue, cells are scattered in a matrix (not packed tightly).

Blood Fluid connective

Plasma = matrix. Transports O₂, CO₂, nutrients, hormones. RBCs, WBCs, platelets are cells.

Bone Dense connective

Matrix of calcium phosphate + collagen fibres. Osteoblasts make bone, osteoclasts break it.

Cartilage Semi-rigid

Matrix = chondrin. Flexible support — found in ear pinna, tip of nose, between vertebrae.

Adipose Fat storage

Stores fat (energy), insulates the body, cushions organs. Found below skin, around kidneys.

Areolar Loose connective

Fills spaces between organs. Contains fibroblasts, mast cells. Found in skin, around blood vessels.

Tendons & Ligaments Dense fibrous

Tendons: connect muscle to bone (collagen). Ligaments: connect bone to bone (elastin + collagen).

Blood — A Special Connective Tissue

Blood is classified as fluid connective tissue. Its matrix is called plasma (liquid). The cells floating in plasma are RBCs (erythrocytes), WBCs (leukocytes) and Platelets (thrombocytes). This classification is frequently tested in MCQs.

⚑ Exam Traps — Connective Tissue
  • Tendons = muscle to bone. Ligaments = bone to bone. Getting these reversed is the #1 mistake.
  • Cartilage has no blood vessels — it is avascular (nutrients by diffusion).
  • Cartilage cells are called chondrocytes; bone cells are called osteocytes.

Muscular Tissue
Skeletal, Smooth & Cardiac
▾

Muscular tissue is specialised for contraction and movement. All muscle cells contain contractile proteins — actin and myosin. There are three types:

LocationAttached to skeletonWalls of hollow organs (intestine, blood vessels)Heart wall only
ControlVoluntary (under will)InvoluntaryInvoluntary
AppearanceStriated (striped)Non-striated (smooth)Striated + branched

⚑ Exam Traps — Muscle Tissue
  • Cardiac muscle is involuntary + striated — it shares features of both other types. Don't say it's non-striated.
  • Skeletal muscle fibres are multi-nucleated (syncytium) — each fibre has many nuclei.
  • Smooth muscle = spindle-shaped cells. Cardiac muscle = branched cells (intercalated discs connect them).
🧠
Nervous Tissue
The communication tissue of the body
▾

Nervous tissue is the most specialised tissue in the animal body. It receives stimuli and transmits signals (nerve impulses) throughout the body.

Key Facts About Nervous Tissue
  • The basic structural and functional unit is the Neuron (nerve cell)
  • A neuron has three parts: Cell body (Cyton/Soma), Dendrites (receive signals), Axon (transmit signals away)
  • The junction between two neurons is called a Synapse
  • Nerve impulse travels: dendrite → cell body → axon → synapse → next neuron
  • Myelin sheath — fatty covering on some axons; increases speed of impulse conduction; formed by Schwann cells
  • Supporting cells of the nervous system = Neuroglia (Glial cells) — outnumber neurons 10:1
Types of Neurons — by Function

Sensory (Afferent) neurons carry impulses from sense organs to the brain. Motor (Efferent) neurons carry impulses from the brain to muscles/glands. Relay (Interneurons) connect sensory and motor neurons inside the spinal cord and brain.

⚑ Exam Trap

Neurons are non-dividing cells — adult neurons do not regenerate (with very limited exceptions). Loss of neurons is permanent. This is frequently tested in context of brain injury or ageing questions.

  • Memory: ECMN → "Every Cell Makes Noise"
Plant Tissue — Master Summary
  • Meristematic — dividing, undifferentiated; Apical (length), Lateral (girth), Intercalary (nodes)
  • Parenchyma — thin wall, living, storage; chlorenchyma (photosynthesis), aerenchyma (buoyancy)
  • Collenchyma — uneven wall at corners, living, flexible support
  • Sclerenchyma — lignified, uniformly thick, dead, rigid support
  • Xylem — water upward; tracheids + vessels (dead) + xylem parenchyma (living)
  • Phloem — food both directions; sieve tubes (living, no nucleus) + companion cells + phloem fibres (dead)
Animal Tissue — Master Summary
  • Epithelial — covering/lining, tightly packed, avascular, on basement membrane
  • Squamous — flat; skin, alveoli, capillaries
  • Cuboidal — cube; kidney tubules, glands
  • Columnar — tall; intestine (absorption); ciliated → trachea, fallopian tube
  • Connective — scattered cells in matrix; blood (fluid), bone (Ca₃PO₄), cartilage (chondrin)
  • Tendon = muscle to bone · Ligament = bone to bone
Muscle Tissue Comparison
  • Skeletal — voluntary, striated, multi-nucleated, fatigues
  • Smooth — involuntary, non-striated, single nucleus, does not fatigue
  • Cardiac — involuntary, striated, branched, intercalated discs, never fatigues
  • All muscles have actin + myosin proteins
Nervous Tissue Key Points
  • Neuron = cell body (cyton) + dendrites (receive) + axon (transmit)
  • Synapse = gap between two neurons
  • Sensory = afferent (toward brain) · Motor = efferent (away from brain)
  • Myelin sheath — speeds impulse; made by Schwann cells
  • Neurons are non-dividing — permanent loss when damaged
  • Neuroglia = supporting cells of nervous system (not neurons)
High-Frequency Exam Facts
  • Histology = study of tissues; Father = Marcello Malpighi
  • Only living xylem cell = xylem parenchyma
  • Sieve tubes = living but no nucleus — companion cells assist
  • Xylem = unidirectional (up); Phloem = bidirectional
  • Blood = fluid connective tissue (matrix = plasma)
  • Cartilage = avascular; cells = chondrocytes; matrix = chondrin
  • Sclerenchyma, xylem vessels, tracheids = dead plant cells
  • Intercalary meristem → allows grasses to regrow after cutting
Commercial & Applied Tissue Facts
  • Jute, Flax, Hemp, Coir — obtained from phloem fibres (sclerenchyma)
  • Cotton — obtained from seed hair (epidermal cell extensions)
  • Coconut husk = sclerenchyma fibres
  • Aerenchyma — helps aquatic plants float (lotus, water hyacinth)
  • Bone marrow — produces blood cells (haematopoiesis)
  • Dendrites receive; axons transmit — never reverse these in MCQs
