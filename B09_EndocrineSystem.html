The Endocrine System
Endocrine glands, hormones, feedback mechanisms, pituitary axis, thyroid, adrenal, pancreas, gonads and hormonal disorders — focussed on CDS & NDA exam patterns.
CDS NDA AFCAT Hormones Glands Feedback

The endocrine system is the body's slow, long-range chemical signalling network. It consists of ductless glands that secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream. Hormones travel to target organs and produce effects. Unlike the nervous system (milliseconds), hormonal effects take seconds to hours but last much longer.

Properties of Hormones
  • Secreted in very small quantities but produce large effects
  • Act on specific target cells/organs that have complementary receptors
  • Are non-nutritive — they carry information, not energy
  • Chemical nature: (a) Protein/Peptide hormones — insulin, glucagon, ADH, oxytocin, FSH, LH, GH, PTH, calcitonin — water-soluble, bind surface receptors; (b) Steroid hormones — cortisol, aldosterone, testosterone, oestrogen, progesterone — lipid-soluble, enter cell and bind nuclear receptors; (c) Amino acid derivatives — adrenaline, noradrenaline (from tyrosine); thyroid hormones T₃ and T₄ (from tyrosine + iodine)
  • Hormones are inactivated in the liver and excreted by kidneys
Negative Feedback — The Master Control Principle

Most hormone secretion is controlled by negative feedback: when the level of a hormone (or its effect) rises sufficiently, it signals back to inhibit further secretion. Example: High blood glucose → insulin released → glucose falls → insulin secretion stops. This prevents over-secretion and maintains homeostasis. Positive feedback (rare) amplifies a response: e.g., oxytocin during labour — more contractions → more oxytocin → stronger contractions.

⚑ Mixed Glands — Exam Trap
  • Pancreas — both endocrine (islets of Langerhans → insulin, glucagon) and exocrine (acini → digestive enzymes). Most tested mixed gland.
  • Gonads (testes, ovaries) — both endocrine (testosterone, oestrogen) and exocrine (sperm, eggs)
  • Liver — mostly exocrine (bile) but also produces some hormones (IGF-1, thrombopoietin)
  • Hypothalamus — part of the nervous system BUT also produces hormones → it is the master link between nervous and endocrine systems
Section 2 — The Pituitary Gland: Master Gland
👑
Pituitary Gland — Anterior & Posterior
Hypophysis — controls most other endocrine glands
▾

The pituitary gland (hypophysis) is called the "master gland" because it secretes hormones that control other endocrine glands. It lies at the base of the brain in a bony cavity called the sella turcica and is connected to the hypothalamus by the infundibulum (pituitary stalk). It has two lobes with completely different functions.

Posterior Pituitary (Neurohypophysis) — stores and releases hypothalamic hormones
Posterior Pituitary Hormones — Made in Hypothalamus, Stored Here
  • ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone / Vasopressin) — acts on kidney collecting duct → water reabsorption → concentrated urine. Deficiency → Diabetes Insipidus (polyuria with dilute urine)
  • Oxytocin — (a) stimulates uterine contractions during labour (positive feedback); (b) milk ejection reflex (let-down) during breastfeeding. Called the "love hormone" — also involved in bonding and trust.
⚑ Pituitary Exam Traps
  • The posterior pituitary does NOT synthesise ADH or oxytocin — it only stores and releases them. These hormones are MADE by the hypothalamus (supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei).
  • Pituitary = "master gland" but the hypothalamus controls the pituitary → hypothalamus is the actual master. Hypothalamic releasing hormones (CRH, TRH, GnRH, GHRH) stimulate anterior pituitary; inhibiting hormones (somatostatin, dopamine) suppress it.
  • Gigantism vs Acromegaly: Both caused by excess GH. Gigantism = excess GH before puberty (growth plates open → tall stature). Acromegaly = excess GH after puberty (growth plates closed → enlarged hands, feet, jaw, forehead).
Section 3 — Thyroid & Parathyroid Glands
🦋
Thyroid Gland — Metabolism Regulator
T₃, T₄, calcitonin and iodine connection
▾

The thyroid gland is a butterfly-shaped gland located in the neck, anterior to the trachea. It is the largest purely endocrine gland in the body. It secretes three hormones: T₃ (triiodothyronine), T₄ (thyroxine/tetraiodothyronine) and calcitonin.

HormoneCellsKey ActionsRequires
T₃ & T₄Follicular cells↑ Basal metabolic rate (BMR); ↑ heat production; normal growth and development; essential for CNS maturation in foetus/infants; ↑ heart rateIodine (T₃=3 iodines; T₄=4 iodines); Tyrosine
CalcitoninParafollicular cells (C cells)↓ Blood calcium — inhibits osteoclasts (bone resorption), promotes Ca²⁺ deposition in bone—
Thyroid Disorders — Most Tested
  • Hypothyroidism (T₃/T₄ deficiency): low BMR, weight gain, lethargy, cold intolerance, constipation, bradycardia, dry skin, hair loss, myxoedema (non-pitting oedema) in adults. In infants: Cretinism — stunted growth, intellectual disability, protruding tongue, pot-bellied. Treated with levothyroxine.
  • Hyperthyroidism (T₃/T₄ excess): high BMR, weight loss, heat intolerance, sweating, tachycardia, anxiety, exophthalmos (bulging eyes). Graves' disease = commonest cause (autoimmune — antibodies stimulate TSH receptor). Treated with antithyroid drugs (carbimazole), radioiodine or surgery.
  • Goitre — enlarged thyroid gland. Simple/endemic goitre = iodine deficiency → low T₃/T₄ → high TSH → gland hypertrophies. Prevented by iodised salt.
  • Cretinism — hypothyroidism in infants/foetuses; iodine deficiency during pregnancy; most common preventable cause of intellectual disability worldwide.
Parathyroid Glands — Calcium Controllers

Four small parathyroid glands are embedded in the posterior surface of the thyroid. They secrete PTH (Parathyroid Hormone / Parathormone) — the main hormone for raising blood calcium: (1) Stimulates osteoclasts → bone resorption → Ca²⁺ into blood. (2) ↑ Ca²⁺ reabsorption in kidney DCT. (3) ↑ Vitamin D activation in kidney → ↑ Ca²⁺ absorption from intestine. Hyperparathyroidism → hypercalcaemia → kidney stones, bone loss ("bones, groans, moans, stones"). Hypoparathyroidism → hypocalcaemia → tetany (involuntary muscle spasms), laryngospasm — life-threatening.

⚑ Calcitonin vs PTH — Opposite Actions
  • PTH (parathyroid) = raises blood Ca²⁺ — mnemonic: PTH = Please Take it Higher
  • Calcitonin (thyroid C cells) = lowers blood Ca²⁺ — mnemonic: Calcitonin = Calcium Toned down
  • They are antagonistic — PTH is far more powerful physiologically; calcitonin is a minor regulator in adults but important during pregnancy and growth.
Section 4 — Adrenal Glands
⚡
Adrenal Glands — Cortex & Medulla
Two glands in one — stress hormones and mineralocorticoids
▾

The two adrenal (suprarenal) glands sit atop each kidney. Each has two distinct regions with completely different embryological origins, cell types and hormones — the outer cortex (80%) and inner medulla (20%).

Adrenal Medulla — Modified Sympathetic Ganglion
Adrenal Medulla Hormones — Catecholamines
  • Adrenaline (Epinephrine) — 80% of medullary secretion. "Fight-or-flight" hormone: ↑ heart rate, ↑ BP, ↑ blood glucose (glycogenolysis), dilates bronchi, dilates pupils, redirects blood to muscles. Secreted during acute stress.
  • Noradrenaline (Norepinephrine) — 20% of medullary secretion. Mainly causes vasoconstriction → ↑ BP. Also a CNS neurotransmitter.
  • Adrenal medulla is controlled by pre-ganglionic sympathetic neurons — it is essentially a modified sympathetic ganglion that secretes hormones instead of having a post-ganglionic nerve.
Adrenal Disorders — High CDS/NDA Frequency
  • Addison's Disease — adrenal cortex insufficiency (often autoimmune). Deficiency of both cortisol AND aldosterone. Features: hypoglycaemia, hypotension, hyperkalaemia, hyponatraemia, weight loss, fatigue, bronze skin hyperpigmentation (↑ ACTH/MSH). Life-threatening adrenal crisis with stress.
  • Cushing's Syndrome — excess cortisol (from adrenal tumour, ACTH-secreting pituitary tumour, or prolonged steroid use). Features: moon face (round puffy face), buffalo hump (fat on upper back), central obesity, thin skin, striae, hypertension, hyperglycaemia, osteoporosis, immunosuppression.
  • Conn's Syndrome (Primary Hyperaldosteronism) — excess aldosterone from adrenal adenoma. Features: hypertension, hypokalaemia (muscle weakness, cramps), metabolic alkalosis.
  • Phaeochromocytoma — tumour of adrenal medulla secreting excess adrenaline/noradrenaline. Features: episodic severe hypertension, headache, sweating, palpitations. Treated surgically.
Section 5 — Pancreas (Islets of Langerhans)
🩺
Pancreas — Insulin, Glucagon & Diabetes
Blood glucose regulation and most-tested endocrine topic
▾

The endocrine pancreas consists of ~1 million clusters of cells called Islets of Langerhans (discovered by Paul Langerhans, 1869). They comprise ~2% of pancreatic mass.

Cell TypeHormoneStimulus for SecretionAction
β (Beta) cells — 70%Insulin↑ Blood glucose (after meals)↓ Blood glucose: promotes glucose uptake into cells (muscle, liver, fat); promotes glycogen synthesis (glycogenesis); inhibits glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis; promotes fat and protein synthesis
α (Alpha) cells — 25%Glucagon↓ Blood glucose (fasting/starvation)↑ Blood glucose: promotes glycogen breakdown (glycogenolysis); promotes glucose synthesis from non-carb sources (gluconeogenesis); promotes fat breakdown (lipolysis)
δ (Delta) cells — 5%SomatostatinHigh glucose/amino acidsInhibits both insulin and glucagon secretion (paracrine); slows digestion
Think of it this way

Insulin is the key that unlocks cells to let glucose in — it lowers blood sugar. Glucagon is the alarm that raises blood sugar when it falls. They are antagonistic hormones working together to keep blood glucose in the normal range (70–100 mg/dL fasting).

Diabetes Mellitus — Type 1 vs Type 2
  • Type 1 DM (Insulin-Dependent / Juvenile): Autoimmune destruction of β cells → absolute insulin deficiency. Onset typically in childhood/adolescence. Must inject insulin to survive. Prone to diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).
  • Type 2 DM (Non-Insulin-Dependent / Adult-onset): Insulin resistance (cells don't respond to insulin) + relative insulin deficiency. Most common type (~90–95% of DM). Associated with obesity, sedentary lifestyle, family history. Treated initially with lifestyle changes, then oral hypoglycaemics (metformin), then insulin if needed.
  • Gestational Diabetes: Develops during pregnancy; usually resolves after delivery; ↑ risk of Type 2 DM later.
  • Key complications of DM: Diabetic neuropathy (nerve damage), nephropathy (kidney damage → most common cause of CKD), retinopathy (eye damage → leading cause of new blindness in adults), cardiovascular disease, foot ulcers.
  • Nobel Prize 1923: Frederick Banting & John Macleod — discovery of insulin.
⚑ Insulin Traps
  • Insulin is a protein hormone — it CANNOT be taken orally (would be digested by proteases). Must be injected. This is why Type 1 diabetics inject insulin.
  • β cells make insulin; α cells make glucagon — do not confuse these. β cells are the MAJORITY (70%).
  • Hypoglycaemia (low blood sugar) triggers glucagon release AND adrenaline release — both raise blood glucose. Symptoms of hypoglycaemia: sweating, trembling, confusion — due to adrenaline release.
Section 6 — Other Important Glands
🌐
Pineal, Thymus, Gonads & Other Hormones
Melatonin, sex hormones, local hormones
▾
Pineal Gland Brain — Epithalamus

Secretes Melatonin. Controls circadian rhythm (sleep-wake cycle) and seasonal biological rhythms. Secretion ↑ in darkness, ↓ in light. Called the "third eye." Regulates puberty onset.

Thymus Thorax

Secretes Thymosin, Thymopoietin. Site of T-lymphocyte maturation. Most active during childhood; involutes after puberty. Essential for immune system development.

Testes Scrotum

Testosterone from Leydig (interstitial) cells. Controls: male secondary sexual characteristics, spermatogenesis, libido, muscle mass, bone density. Inhibin from Sertoli cells inhibits FSH.

Ovaries Pelvis

Oestrogen (follicle cells) — female secondary sexual characteristics, uterine lining growth, bone density. Progesterone (corpus luteum) — maintains pregnancy, prepares uterus. Relaxin — relaxes pelvic ligaments during childbirth.

Placenta Uterus in pregnancy

Temporary endocrine organ. Secretes: hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin — basis of pregnancy test; maintains corpus luteum in 1st trimester), oestrogen, progesterone, hPL.

Kidney & Heart Organ hormones

Kidney: Renin (RAAS), Erythropoietin / EPO (stimulates RBC production in bone marrow). Heart atria: ANP (atrial natriuretic peptide — lowers BP by ↑ Na⁺ excretion).

Prostaglandins — Local Hormones
  • Prostaglandins are local hormones (also called tissue hormones or paracrine factors) — they act near their site of production, not via bloodstream
  • Derived from arachidonic acid (a fatty acid); found in almost all tissues
  • Functions: regulate inflammation, pain, fever; stimulate uterine contractions; regulate blood pressure; involved in blood clotting
  • NSAIDs (aspirin, ibuprofen) work by inhibiting COX enzymes that synthesise prostaglandins → reduce pain and inflammation

  • β cells (70%) → Insulin → ↓ glucose (after meals; anabolic)
  • α cells (25%) → Glucagon → ↑ glucose (fasting; catabolic)

  • Pineal — Melatonin → sleep-wake cycle (↑ in dark)
  • Thymus — Thymosin → T-cell maturation; most active in childhood
  • Testes — Testosterone (Leydig cells); Inhibin (Sertoli cells)
  • Ovaries — Oestrogen (follicle cells) + Progesterone (corpus luteum)
  • Kidney — EPO (↑ RBC production); Renin (RAAS)
  • hCG (placenta) — basis of pregnancy test; maintains corpus luteum
Feedback & Disorders Summary
  • Most hormones = negative feedback; Oxytocin (labour) = positive feedback
  • Steroid hormones — lipid-soluble; bind nuclear receptors (enter cell)
  • Protein hormones — water-soluble; bind surface receptors
  • Goitre — iodine deficiency → ↑ TSH → thyroid enlarges; prevented by iodised salt
  • ACTH/MSH excess (Addison's) → bronze skin pigmentation
  • Prostaglandins = local hormones; NSAIDs inhibit their synthesis
