The Digestive System
From mouth to absorption — organs, enzymes, secretions and the complete journey of food through the human body. Essential for CDS, NDA & AFCAT.
CDS NDA AFCAT Organs Enzymes Absorption

Digestion is the process of breaking down complex food molecules into simpler, absorbable forms. The human digestive system consists of the alimentary canal (a ~9-metre tube from mouth to anus) and associated accessory glands (salivary glands, liver, pancreas). Digestion is both mechanical (physical breaking) and chemical (enzymatic breakdown).

Section 1 — The Alimentary Canal: Journey of Food
🗺️
The Complete Digestive Pathway
From ingestion to egestion — step by step
▾

Food travels through a series of organs, each performing a specific role in digestion or absorption. The movement of food through the alimentary canal is driven by peristalsis — rhythmic, wave-like muscle contractions of the gut wall.

1
Mouth (Buccal Cavity)
Mechanical: teeth chew food (mastication), tongue mixes it. Chemical: Salivary amylase (ptyalin) begins starch → maltose digestion. Saliva also contains lysozyme (antibacterial) and mucin (lubrication). Food is shaped into a bolus.
2
Pharynx & Oesophagus
Bolus passes through the pharynx (throat) and down the oesophagus (food pipe) by peristalsis. No digestion occurs here. The epiglottis closes the trachea during swallowing to prevent choking.
3
Stomach
Mechanical: churning. Chemical: gastric juice contains HCl (kills bacteria, activates pepsinogen), pepsin (proteins → peptides), renin (milk protein casein in infants). Food becomes a semi-liquid chyme. HCl creates pH ~2.
4
Small Intestine (Duodenum → Jejunum → Ileum)
The primary site of digestion AND absorption. Receives secretions from the pancreas and liver (bile). Pancreatic juice neutralises acidic chyme. Intestinal juice (succus entericus) completes digestion. Absorption occurs in the ileum via villi and microvilli.
5
Large Intestine (Colon → Rectum → Anus)
No enzymatic digestion. Main function: absorption of water and minerals from undigested food. Remaining waste is compacted into faeces and stored in the rectum before egestion through the anus. Gut bacteria here produce some Vitamin K and B12.
Think of it this way

The alimentary canal is like an assembly line in reverse — instead of building something, it disassembles complex food molecules progressively. Each station (organ) has its own tools (enzymes) and does one specific job before passing the material forward.

Section 2 — Digestive Glands & Their Secretions
🫙
Salivary Glands
Three pairs — the first digestive secretion
▾

There are three pairs of salivary glands that secrete saliva into the mouth:

Parotid Largest

Largest salivary gland. Located in front of and below the ear. Site of mumps infection.

Submandibular Under jaw

Below the mandible (lower jaw). Produces mixed serous + mucous secretion.

Sublingual Smallest

Under the tongue. Smallest of the three. Primarily mucous secretion.

Composition & Function of Saliva
  • Salivary amylase (Ptyalin) — digests starch → maltose (begins carbohydrate digestion)
  • Mucin — lubricates food, helps form bolus
  • Lysozyme — antibacterial enzyme in saliva
  • pH of saliva: 6.8–7.0 (slightly acidic to neutral)
  • Saliva production: ~1–1.5 litres per day
⚑ Exam Trap

Salivary amylase only acts on starch — it does NOT digest proteins or fats. Also, its action stops in the stomach because the acidic pH (HCl) denatures the enzyme. Starch digestion is then completed later in the small intestine by pancreatic amylase.

🫀
Stomach — Gastric Glands
HCl, pepsin, renin and gastric juice
▾

The stomach wall contains gastric glands with three types of secretory cells:

Cell TypeSecretionFunction
Oxyntic / Parietal cellsHydrochloric acid (HCl)Creates acidic pH (~2); kills bacteria; activates pepsinogen → pepsin; denatures proteins
Chief / Peptic cells (Zymogen cells)Pepsinogen (inactive)Activated by HCl → Pepsin; digests proteins → peptides
Mucous neck cellsMucusProtects stomach lining from acid (prevents self-digestion)

Renin — Special Role in Infants
  • Renin is a proteolytic enzyme secreted by the stomach of infants (not adults)
  • It coagulates (curdles) milk protein casein into calcium paracaseinate
  • This slows the passage of milk through the stomach, allowing proper digestion
  • Not present in significant amounts in adult human stomach
⚑ Exam Trap
  • Pepsin is secreted as an inactive precursor (pepsinogen) — activated by HCl. This is to prevent the enzyme from digesting the stomach wall itself.
  • The stomach produces pepsin (not trypsin). Trypsin is from the pancreas. Getting these mixed up is the #1 mistake here.
  • HCl is produced by parietal (oxyntic) cells, NOT by chief cells.
🟤
Liver & Bile
The largest gland — bile production and liver functions
▾

The liver is the largest gland and the largest internal organ in the human body. It performs over 500 functions. In digestion, its key role is producing bile.

Bile — Key Facts

Bile is produced by the liver, stored in the gall bladder, and released into the duodenum. It is alkaline (pH ~7.7–8.0) and contains bile salts (sodium glycocholate and sodium taurocholate).

Functions of Bile
  • Emulsification of fats — breaks large fat globules into tiny droplets (increases surface area for lipase action). NOT actual digestion.
  • Neutralises acidic chyme from the stomach — makes duodenum slightly alkaline for pancreatic enzyme activity
  • Activates pancreatic lipase
  • Contains bile pigments (bilirubin, biliverdin) — breakdown products of haemoglobin; give faeces their colour
  • Bile has NO digestive enzymes
Other Major Functions of the Liver
  • Glycogen storage — stores excess glucose as glycogen; releases it when blood sugar falls
  • Detoxification — breaks down alcohol, drugs, ammonia (urea cycle)
  • Synthesis of plasma proteins — albumin, fibrinogen, prothrombin
  • Production of bile (~600–1200 mL/day)
  • Urea formation — converts toxic ammonia (from amino acid breakdown) into urea for excretion
  • Vitamin storage — stores vitamins A, D, B12
  • Heat production — metabolically very active; major heat source in the body
⚑ Exam Trap

Bile does NOT contain any digestive enzymes. It emulsifies fats (physical process) but does NOT chemically digest them — that is the job of lipase. This distinction is tested directly. Also: bile is produced by the liver, stored in the gall bladder — these are two different organs.

🔵
Pancreas — The Mixed Gland
Both exocrine (digestive) and endocrine (hormonal)
▾

The pancreas is a mixed gland — it has both exocrine functions (produces digestive enzymes) and endocrine functions (secretes hormones into blood).

FunctionCells ResponsibleSecretionRole
ExocrineAcinar cellsPancreatic juice (into duodenum)Digestion of carbohydrates, proteins, fats
EndocrineIslets of Langerhans — α cellsGlucagonRaises blood glucose (glycogenolysis)
EndocrineIslets of Langerhans — β cellsInsulinLowers blood glucose; promotes glycogen storage
Pancreatic Juice — Enzyme Contents
  • Pancreatic amylase — digests remaining starch → maltose
  • Trypsin (from trypsinogen) + Chymotrypsin — digest proteins → smaller peptides
  • Pancreatic lipase — digests fats (emulsified by bile) → fatty acids + glycerol
  • Nucleases (DNase, RNase) — digest nucleic acids
  • Sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃) — neutralises acidic chyme; makes pH alkaline (~8) for enzyme activity
⚑ Exam Trap
  • Trypsinogen (inactive) is secreted by the pancreas → activated to trypsin by enterokinase (secreted by intestinal lining). This cascade prevents self-digestion of the pancreas.
  • Insulin is secreted by β (beta) cells. Glucagon by α (alpha) cells. Never reverse these.
  • Deficiency of insulin → Diabetes mellitus. This is the most tested clinical link from the pancreas.

⚑ Exam Trap — Lactose Intolerance

Lactose intolerance occurs when lactase enzyme is absent or insufficient — lactose in milk cannot be digested, causing bloating, gas and diarrhoea. The problem is with the enzyme lactase, not with calcium or milk allergy. This is a frequently tested applied question.

🔬
Absorption — Villi & Microvilli
How digested nutrients enter the bloodstream
▾

Absorption of digested nutrients primarily occurs in the ileum (last part of the small intestine). The inner lining (mucosa) is highly folded and covered with villi and further with microvilli (forming the brush border), dramatically increasing surface area.

Increasing Surface Area — Three Levels

Circular folds (plicae circulares) → Villi (finger-like projections, ~0.5–1 mm tall) → Microvilli (brush border on each villus cell). Together, these increase the absorptive surface area from ~0.3 m² (flat tube) to approximately 250 m² — the size of a tennis court.

What is Absorbed — Where and How
  • Glucose & amino acids → absorbed into blood capillaries in villi → portal vein → liver
  • Fatty acids & glycerol → reassembled into triglycerides → enter lacteals (lymph vessels in villi) as chylomicrons → thoracic duct → blood
  • Water-soluble vitamins (B, C) → blood capillaries
  • Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) → lacteals (along with fat)
  • Water & minerals → mainly in large intestine (and partly small intestine)
⚑ Exam Trap

Fats are absorbed into lacteals (lymph), NOT directly into blood. Glucose and amino acids go into blood capillaries. This distinction is directly tested. The lymph eventually drains into blood via the thoracic duct — so fats do reach blood, but via lymph first.

Vestigial Organs in the Digestive System
  • Appendix (Vermiform appendix) — small finger-like projection from the caecum; considered vestigial in humans; plays a role in gut immunity (lymphoid tissue) but is not essential
  • Removal (appendectomy) causes no long-term digestive problems
Hormonal Control of Digestion

Gastrin (from stomach G-cells) → stimulates HCl secretion. Secretin (from duodenum) → stimulates pancreatic NaHCO₃ secretion to neutralise chyme. Cholecystokinin (CCK) (from duodenum) → stimulates gall bladder to release bile AND pancreas to release digestive enzymes. GIP (Gastric Inhibitory Peptide) → inhibits gastric secretion when food enters the small intestine.

Memory — Fat-Soluble Vitamins: ADEK
  • Fat-soluble: A, D, E, K — "A Dog Eats Kibble"
  • All others (B-complex, C) are water-soluble
  • Fat-soluble vitamins are absorbed along with fats via lacteals
  • Fat-soluble vitamins can accumulate (toxicity risk); water-soluble are excreted in urine (lower toxicity risk)
⚑ Top Vitamin Exam Traps
  • Scurvy = Vitamin C. Rickets = Vitamin D. Night blindness = Vitamin A. These three pairs are the most tested.
  • Vitamin D is synthesised in skin by sunlight (UV radiation converts 7-dehydrocholesterol). It is also called the "sunshine vitamin".
  • Vitamin K is essential for blood clotting (activates clotting factors). Produced by gut bacteria. Newborns get a Vitamin K injection at birth because they lack gut bacteria.
  • Vitamin B12 is NOT found in plant foods — vegans are at risk of pernicious anaemia.
Alimentary Canal — Organs & Roles
  • Mouth — mastication; salivary amylase digests starch; bolus formed
  • Oesophagus — peristalsis only; no digestion
  • Stomach — HCl + pepsin (protein → peptides); chyme formed; renin in infants
  • Small intestine — main site of digestion + absorption; villi + microvilli
  • Large intestine — water + mineral absorption; no enzymes; faeces formed
  • Peristalsis = wave-like muscle contractions propel food forward
Accessory Glands at a Glance
  • Salivary glands (3 pairs) — parotid (largest), submandibular, sublingual
  • Liver — largest gland; produces bile (stored in gall bladder)
  • Bile: emulsifies fats; neutralises chyme; NO digestive enzymes
  • Bile pigments (bilirubin/biliverdin) → faeces colour
  • Pancreas — mixed gland; amylase, trypsin, lipase, NaHCO₃
  • Trypsinogen → trypsin (by enterokinase); insulin (β cells), glucagon (α cells)
Enzyme Quick-Fire
  • Ptyalin — starch → maltose (mouth)
  • Pepsin — proteins → peptides (stomach); activated by HCl
  • Trypsin — proteins → peptides (pancreas/duodenum)
  • Lipase — fats → fatty acids + glycerol (pancreas)
  • Maltase → glucose · Sucrase → glucose+fructose · Lactase → glucose+galactose
  • Erepsin (peptidases) — peptides → amino acids (small intestine)
Absorption Routes
  • Glucose + amino acids → blood capillaries → portal vein → liver
  • Fatty acids + glycerol → lacteals (lymph) → thoracic duct → blood
  • Fat-soluble vitamins (A,D,E,K) → lacteals
  • Water-soluble vitamins (B,C) → blood capillaries
  • Water + minerals → large intestine → blood
  • Surface area of small intestine: ~250 m² (due to villi + microvilli)
Fat-Soluble Vitamins (ADEK)
  • Vitamin A — Night blindness, Xerophthalmia
  • Vitamin D — Rickets (child), Osteomalacia (adult); "sunshine vitamin"
  • Vitamin E — sterility, muscle weakness
  • Vitamin K — impaired clotting; produced by gut bacteria
Water-Soluble Vitamins + Key Disorders
  • B1 (Thiamine) — Beriberi
  • B3 (Niacin) — Pellagra (dermatitis, diarrhoea, dementia)
  • B12 (Cobalamin) — Pernicious anaemia; absent in plant food
  • Vitamin C — Scurvy (bleeding gums)
  • Diabetes mellitus — insulin deficiency (β cells of pancreas)
  • Jaundice — excess bilirubin; liver/bile duct disorder
