The Reproductive System
Male & female reproductive organs, gametogenesis, menstrual cycle, fertilisation, embryonic development, pregnancy and reproductive health — at CDS, NDA & AFCAT exam depth.
CDS NDA AFCAT Gametogenesis Fertilisation Menstrual Cycle

The reproductive system enables continuation of the species through production of gametes (sex cells), fertilisation and development of offspring. Humans reproduce sexually — a sperm (male gamete) fuses with an ovum (female gamete) to form a zygote, which develops into a new individual over approximately 9 months (38–40 weeks).

Section 1 — Male Reproductive System
♂️
Male Reproductive Organs & Functions
Testes, ducts, accessory glands and semen
▾

The primary male reproductive organs are the testes (singular: testis), located in the scrotum — outside the body cavity to maintain a temperature 2–3°C lower than body temperature, which is essential for spermatogenesis (sperm production).

Testes Primary gonad

Produce sperm in seminiferous tubules. Leydig (interstitial) cells between tubules secrete testosterone. Sertoli cells (inside tubules) nourish developing sperm and secrete inhibin (inhibits FSH).

Epididymis Sperm maturation

Coiled tube on posterior surface of testis. Site of sperm maturation and storage. Sperm gain motility here. Transit time: ~2–3 weeks. If not ejaculated, sperm are reabsorbed.

Vas Deferens Sperm duct

Muscular duct transporting sperm from epididymis to urethra. Cut in vasectomy (male sterilisation). Joins with seminal vesicle duct to form ejaculatory duct.

Seminal Vesicles Accessory gland

Contribute ~60% of semen volume. Secretion rich in fructose (energy for sperm), prostaglandins and fibrinogen. Secretion is slightly alkaline.

Prostate Gland Accessory gland

Surrounds urethra below bladder. Adds ~30% of semen volume. Secretes alkaline fluid (neutralises vaginal acidity), enzymes, zinc and PSA (Prostate Specific Antigen — marker for prostate cancer).

Bulbourethral Glands Cowper's glands

Small paired glands below prostate. Secrete pre-ejaculatory mucus — lubricates urethra and neutralises acidic urine residue before ejaculation.

Semen — Composition & Key Facts
  • Average volume per ejaculation: 2–5 mL
  • Sperm count: ~200–300 million sperm per ejaculation (minimum 15 million/mL for fertility)
  • Only ~1% of semen is sperm; the remaining 99% is seminal plasma (secretions from accessory glands)
  • Semen pH: 7.2–8.0 (slightly alkaline — protects sperm from vaginal acidity)
  • Main energy source for sperm: fructose (from seminal vesicles)
  • Semen clots after ejaculation (due to fibrinogen from seminal vesicles) then liquefies within 20 minutes (due to PSA from prostate)
⚑ Sperm Path — Exam Trap (SEVEN UP mnemonic)

Path of sperm from production to ejaculation: Seminiferous tubules → Epididymis → Vas deferens → Ejaculatory duct → (Nothing) → Urethra → Penis. Mnemonic: SEVEN UP (Seminiferous tubules → Epididymis → Vas deferens → Ejaculatory duct → urethra → Penis). Vasectomy cuts the vas deferens. Sperm are stored in the epididymis, NOT the testes.

Section 2 — Spermatogenesis
🔬
Spermatogenesis — Sperm Production
From spermatogonia to mature spermatozoa — meiosis in males
▾

Spermatogenesis is the production of spermatozoa (sperm) in the seminiferous tubules of the testes. It begins at puberty and continues throughout life. The entire process takes approximately 64–74 days.

1
Spermatogonium (2n) — Diploid stem cell
Mitotic divisions produce more spermatogonia (self-renewal) and primary spermatocytes. Present in outer layer of seminiferous tubule.
2
Primary Spermatocyte (2n) → Meiosis I
Undergoes Meiosis I (first reduction division) → two secondary spermatocytes (haploid, n). Crossing over occurs in prophase I — genetic recombination.
3
Secondary Spermatocyte (n) → Meiosis II
Undergoes Meiosis II (equational division) → four spermatids (n). Each primary spermatocyte produces 4 spermatids.
4
Spermatids → Spermatozoa (Spermiogenesis)
Spermiogenesis — spermatids differentiate into mature sperm (spermatozoa) with head (nucleus + acrosome), midpiece (mitochondria for energy) and tail (flagellum for motility). No cell division here — just morphological change.
Sperm Structure — Parts & Functions
  • Head: contains nucleus (haploid, 23 chromosomes) + acrosome (enzyme-filled cap derived from Golgi apparatus; contains hyaluronidase and acrosin to penetrate the zona pellucida of the egg)
  • Midpiece (neck): packed with mitochondria — provides ATP for flagellar movement (sperm use aerobic respiration using fructose from semen)
  • Tail (flagellum): provides motility; whip-like movement propels sperm
  • Temperature sensitivity: spermatogenesis requires 2–3°C below body temperature → that is why testes are in the scrotum. Undescended testis (cryptorchidism) → infertility
⚑ Spermatogenesis vs Oogenesis — Key Differences
  • Spermatogenesis: produces 4 functional sperm from 1 primary spermatocyte. Continuous from puberty onwards.
  • Oogenesis: produces 1 functional ovum + 2–3 polar bodies (non-functional). Begins before birth, paused at birth, resumes at puberty, one per month.
  • Sperm are the smallest human cells; ovum is the largest human cell.

  • Fertilisation normally occurs in the ampulla of the fallopian tube (outer third) — NOT in the uterus or ovary.
  • If a fertilised egg implants in the fallopian tube instead of the uterus → ectopic pregnancy — a surgical emergency.
  • Tubectomy (tubal ligation) — female sterilisation — cuts or ties the fallopian tubes, preventing eggs from reaching the uterus.
Section 4 — Oogenesis & The Menstrual Cycle
🥚
Oogenesis — Egg Production
From oogonia to mature ovum — meiosis in females
▾

Oogenesis is the production of ova (eggs) in the ovary. It is a lifelong process with very long pauses — most dramatically different from the continuous spermatogenesis in males.

1
Foetal life — Oogonia → Primary oocytes
In foetal ovaries, oogonia divide by mitosis then enter meiosis I, becoming primary oocytes. Meiosis I is arrested in prophase I. At birth, a girl has ~1–2 million primary oocytes (all she will ever have).
2
Puberty to menopause — Monthly resumption
Each month, FSH stimulates a few follicles. One dominant follicle matures (Graafian follicle). Just before ovulation, the primary oocyte completes Meiosis I → secondary oocyte (n) + first polar body.
3
Ovulation — Secondary oocyte released
The secondary oocyte (arrested in metaphase II) is released at ovulation. It is surrounded by zona pellucida and corona radiata cells. Meiosis II only completes IF fertilisation occurs.
4
Fertilisation → Mature ovum
Sperm entry triggers completion of Meiosis II → mature ovum (n) + second polar body. Total: 1 functional egg + 2–3 non-functional polar bodies from one primary oocyte.

Menstrual Cycle — 28-Day Hormonal Clock
Four phases, hormones and what happens at each stage
▾

The menstrual cycle is a monthly cycle of hormonal changes that prepares the female body for pregnancy. Average duration: 28 days (range 21–35 days). Day 1 = first day of menstruation.

PhaseDaysKey EventsDominant Hormones
Menstrual Phase1–5Shedding of endometrium (menstruation) — because no pregnancy occurred; progesterone & oestrogen fall↓ Progesterone, ↓ Oestrogen
Follicular Phase (Proliferative)1–13FSH stimulates follicle growth; follicle secretes oestrogen; endometrium thickens and proliferates (rebuilds)↑ FSH, ↑ Oestrogen
Ovulation~Day 14LH surge triggers release of secondary oocyte from Graafian follicle; most fertile periodLH surge (peak)
Luteal Phase (Secretory)15–28Empty follicle → corpus luteum (secretes progesterone + oestrogen); endometrium becomes secretory, ready for implantation. If no fertilisation → corpus luteum degenerates → progesterone falls → menstruation begins again↑ Progesterone, ↑ Oestrogen
Hormonal Roles — LH, FSH, Oestrogen, Progesterone
  • FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone) — anterior pituitary → stimulates follicle growth and oestrogen secretion
  • LH (Luteinising Hormone) — anterior pituitary → LH surge on day ~13 triggers ovulation; forms corpus luteum after ovulation
  • Oestrogen — ovarian follicle cells → rebuilds endometrium (proliferative phase); responsible for female secondary sexual characteristics; at high levels, triggers LH surge (positive feedback — one of few examples in the body)
  • Progesterone — corpus luteum → maintains endometrium in secretory state ready for implantation; if pregnancy occurs, progesterone remains high (from corpus luteum then placenta) to maintain pregnancy; inhibits further ovulation during pregnancy
⚑ Menstrual Cycle Exam Traps
  • Ovulation occurs on day ~14 of a 28-day cycle — triggered by the LH surge (NOT FSH). The safest and most fertile day is day 14.
  • The luteal phase is always ~14 days regardless of cycle length — it is the follicular phase that varies. A woman with a 35-day cycle ovulates on day ~21, not day 14.
  • High oestrogen just before ovulation triggers positive feedback on the pituitary → LH surge → ovulation. This is one of very few positive feedback examples in biology.
  • Corpus luteum = remains of the Graafian follicle after ovulation; secretes progesterone. If no pregnancy → degenerates → corpus albicans. If pregnancy → maintained by hCG from placenta.
  • Menarche = first menstrual period (onset of puberty, ~11–13 years). Menopause = cessation of menstruation (~45–55 years).
Section 5 — Fertilisation, Implantation & Early Development
🌱
Fertilisation & Implantation
Sperm meets egg — from zygote to blastocyst
▾

Fertilisation is the fusion of a sperm (n=23) with a secondary oocyte (n=23) to form a zygote (2n=46). It normally occurs in the ampulla of the fallopian tube within 12–24 hours of ovulation.

1
Acrosome Reaction
Sperm reaches corona radiata → acrosomal enzymes (hyaluronidase, acrosin) digest through corona radiata and zona pellucida → sperm head penetrates secondary oocyte membrane.
2
Cortical Reaction — Block to Polyspermy
First sperm entry → cortical granules fuse with egg membrane → zona reaction hardens zona pellucida → prevents additional sperm from entering (block to polyspermy). Essential — two sperm = lethal polyploidy.
3
Completion of Meiosis II & Zygote Formation
Sperm entry triggers secondary oocyte to complete Meiosis II → mature ovum + second polar body. Male and female pronuclei fuse → zygote (2n=46). Sex determined at fertilisation by sperm chromosome (X or Y).
4
Cleavage — Rapid Mitotic Divisions
Zygote undergoes rapid mitotic divisions (cleavage) without growth → 2-cell → 4-cell → 8-cell → morula (solid ball of 16–32 cells, day 3–4) while travelling down fallopian tube.
5
Blastocyst Formation & Implantation
Morula → blastocyst (hollow ball; inner cell mass = embryo; trophoblast = placenta precursor) by day 5. Blastocyst reaches uterus and implants into endometrium on day 6–10 → implantation. Trophoblast secretes hCG — basis of pregnancy test.
Sex Determination
  • All eggs carry X chromosome (females are XX)
  • Sperm carry either X or Y chromosome (males are XY)
  • If X-sperm fertilises egg → XX = female
  • If Y-sperm fertilises egg → XY = male
  • Sex is determined at fertilisation by the father's sperm — the mother has no role in determining sex chromosomally
  • Y chromosome carries the SRY gene (Sex-determining Region of Y) which triggers testis development
⚑ Key Developmental Landmarks
  • Zygote → day 0 (fertilisation)
  • Morula → day 3–4 (solid 16–32 cells)
  • Blastocyst → day 5–6 (hollow; implants day 6–10)
  • Gastrulation → week 3 (3 germ layers form: ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm)
  • Embryo → weeks 1–8 (organogenesis — major organ formation)
  • Foetus → week 9 to birth (growth and maturation)
  • Normal gestation: 40 weeks (280 days) from last menstrual period; ~38 weeks from fertilisation
Section 6 — Placenta, Pregnancy & Parturition
🤱
Placenta, Foetal Development & Birth
The organ of exchange, pregnancy hormones and parturition
▾

The placenta is a temporary disc-shaped organ formed from maternal endometrium and foetal trophoblast cells. It is the lifeline between mother and foetus, fully formed by ~week 12.

Placenta — Structure & Functions

Functions of the Placenta: (1) Exchange — O₂ and nutrients pass from mother to foetus; CO₂ and waste pass from foetus to mother via diffusion (maternal and foetal blood do NOT mix — separated by placental membrane). (2) Endocrine — secretes hCG (maintains corpus luteum in 1st trimester), oestrogen, progesterone (maintains pregnancy, prevents ovulation), hPL (human placental lactogen). (3) Barrier — prevents some pathogens and large molecules from reaching foetus (but does NOT block all — rubella virus, HIV, some drugs cross the placenta). (4) Immunological — IgG antibodies from mother cross placenta → passive immunity to newborn.

Hormones of Pregnancy
  • hCG (human Chorionic Gonadotropin) — secreted by trophoblast from ~day 8 post-fertilisation; maintains corpus luteum → progesterone production continues; peaks at ~10 weeks; basis of PREGNANCY TEST (detected in blood/urine). hCG is why "morning sickness" correlates with high hCG levels.
  • Progesterone — maintains endometrium; prevents uterine contractions; suppresses maternal immune response (prevents rejection of foetus); inhibits new ovulation
  • Oestrogen — promotes uterine growth, breast development; increases sensitivity to oxytocin at term
  • Relaxin (from corpus luteum/placenta) — relaxes pelvic ligaments and cervix for delivery
  • Oxytocin (from posterior pituitary) — stimulates uterine contractions during labour (positive feedback)
Parturition (Childbirth) — Key Sequence
  • Near term: progesterone falls, oestrogen rises → uterus becomes sensitive to oxytocin
  • Labour begins: oxytocin triggers contractions → positive feedback loop amplifies contractions
  • Three stages: (1) Dilation — cervix opens to 10 cm; (2) Expulsion — baby delivered; (3) Placental stage — placenta expelled (afterbirth)
  • Colostrum — first milk secreted in first few days; rich in IgA antibodies, protein and Vitamin A; provides passive immunity to newborn
⚑ Foetal & Maternal Blood Do NOT Mix

A common exam trap: maternal and foetal blood circulations are SEPARATE — they do not mix in the placenta. Exchange of gases, nutrients and waste occurs by diffusion across the thin placental membrane (syncytiotrophoblast). This separation is important: it prevents maternal immune cells from attacking the foetus, and allows foetuses with different blood groups from the mother to survive.

Assisted Reproductive Technologies (ART)
  • IVF (In Vitro Fertilisation) — eggs retrieved from ovary, fertilised with sperm in laboratory → resulting embryo transferred to uterus. Often called "test-tube baby." First IVF baby: Louise Brown (UK, 1978). First IVF baby in India: Kanupriya (Durga) (1978, same year).
  • ICSI (Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection) — single sperm injected directly into egg; used when sperm count or motility is very low
  • AI (Artificial Insemination) — sperm introduced into female reproductive tract without sexual intercourse; used in livestock and human infertility
  • GIFT (Gamete Intrafallopian Transfer) — eggs and sperm transferred together into fallopian tube for fertilisation to occur naturally in the tube
  • Surrogacy — another woman carries the pregnancy; used when the genetic mother cannot carry the foetus
Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs) — Exam Points
  • HIV/AIDS — Human Immunodeficiency Virus; destroys CD4+ T-helper cells; spread by sexual contact, blood, mother to child. No cure; managed with antiretroviral therapy (ART). ELISA test detects HIV antibodies.
  • Gonorrhoea — caused by Neisseria gonorrhoeae (bacterium); affects urethra, cervix; treated with antibiotics
  • Syphilis — caused by Treponema pallidum (bacterium); three stages; can cross placenta (congenital syphilis); treated with penicillin
  • Chlamydia — caused by Chlamydia trachomatis; most common bacterial STI; often asymptomatic; can cause pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) and infertility
  • HPV (Human Papillomavirus) — causes genital warts; high-risk strains cause cervical cancer. Prevented by HPV vaccine (Gardasil). Pap smear detects precancerous changes.
  • Herpes — caused by Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV-2 mainly genital); lifelong infection; managed with antivirals

Three Germ Layers — Derivatives (Gastrulation, Week 3)
  • Ectoderm (outer) — gives rise to: nervous system (brain, spinal cord), skin (epidermis), sense organs, hair, nails, lens of eye, tooth enamel. Mnemonic: Ecto = Exterior
  • Mesoderm (middle) — gives rise to: muscles, skeleton, circulatory system (heart, blood vessels), kidneys, gonads, connective tissue, dermis of skin. Mnemonic: Meso = Middle/Muscular
  • Endoderm (inner) — gives rise to: lining of digestive tract, respiratory tract, liver, pancreas, thyroid, bladder, urethra. Mnemonic: Endo = Inside/Inner lining
Infertility — Causes & Key Terms
  • Male infertility: azoospermia (no sperm), oligospermia (low count <15 million/mL), asthenospermia (poor motility), teratospermia (abnormal morphology), cryptorchidism (undescended testes)
  • Female infertility: anovulation (PCOS most common cause), blocked fallopian tubes (PID/chlamydia), endometriosis, uterine fibroids, premature ovarian failure
  • PCOD/PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome) — most common cause of female infertility; multiple cysts in ovaries; high androgens; irregular periods; excess hair growth; managed with hormones and lifestyle changes
Male System — Key Facts
  • Testes in scrotum (2–3°C cooler) → essential for spermatogenesis
  • Sperm path: Seminiferous tubules → Epididymis → Vas deferens → Ejaculatory duct → Urethra → Penis (SEVEN UP)
  • Sertoli cells — nourish sperm · Leydig cells — secrete testosterone
  • Seminal vesicles — fructose (sperm energy, 60% volume)
  • Prostate — alkaline fluid, PSA (prostate cancer marker, 30% volume)
  • Vasectomy = cut vas deferens; does NOT affect testosterone/libido
Spermatogenesis vs Oogenesis
  • Spermatogenesis: 1 primary spermatocyte → 4 functional sperm; continuous from puberty
  • Oogenesis: 1 primary oocyte → 1 functional ovum + 2–3 polar bodies
  • Sperm = smallest human cell · Ovum = largest human cell
  • Oogenesis arrested in prophase I at birth; resumes monthly at puberty
  • Secondary oocyte released at ovulation (arrested in metaphase II)
  • Meiosis II completes only on fertilisation by sperm
Menstrual Cycle — 28 Days
  • Day 1–5: Menstruation (↓ progesterone, ↓ oestrogen)
  • Day 1–13: Follicular phase — FSH → follicle growth → ↑ oestrogen → endometrium thickens
  • Day ~14: LH surge → Ovulation (most fertile day)
  • Day 15–28: Luteal phase — corpus luteum → ↑ progesterone → endometrium secretory
  • Luteal phase always ~14 days; follicular phase varies with cycle length
  • No pregnancy → corpus luteum degenerates → menstruation; Pregnancy → hCG maintains corpus luteum
Fertilisation & Development
  • Fertilisation site: ampulla of fallopian tube (NOT uterus)
  • Ectopic pregnancy: implantation outside uterus (usually tube) → emergency
  • Sex determined by sperm: X-sperm → girl (XX); Y-sperm → boy (XY)
  • Zygote → Morula (day 3–4) → Blastocyst (day 5) → Implantation (day 6–10)
  • hCG from trophoblast = basis of pregnancy test; maintains corpus luteum
  • Gestation: 40 weeks from LMP; ~38 weeks from fertilisation
Germ Layers & Placenta
  • Ectoderm — skin, nervous system, sense organs, hair, nails
  • Mesoderm — muscles, skeleton, heart, kidneys, gonads
  • Endoderm — gut lining, respiratory tract, liver, pancreas, thyroid
  • Placenta: maternal + foetal blood do NOT mix; exchange by diffusion
  • Maternal IgG crosses placenta → passive immunity to newborn
  • Colostrum: first milk; rich in IgA antibodies + Vitamin A
Contraception & ART
  • Condom = only method that protects against both pregnancy AND STIs
  • OCP = synthetic oestrogen + progesterone → inhibit FSH/LH → no ovulation
  • Copper-T: Cu²⁺ ions toxic to sperm; lasts 5–10 years
  • IVF = fertilisation in lab; first test-tube baby: Louise Brown (1978, UK)
  • First IVF baby in India: Kanupriya (Durga), 1978
  • HPV vaccine prevents cervical cancer; Pap smear = cervical cancer screening
