Section 1 — Temperature & Scales
🌡️
Temperature Scales & Conversions
Celsius, Fahrenheit, Kelvin — and how they relate
▾

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the molecules of a substance. Heat is energy in transit due to a temperature difference. The three common scales are Celsius, Fahrenheit, and Kelvin.

Temperature Conversion Formulas
°F = (9/5 × °C) + 32   ·   K = °C + 273.15
Celsius → Fahrenheit: multiply by 9/5 then add 32
Fahrenheit → Celsius: subtract 32 then multiply by 5/9
Celsius → Kelvin: add 273 (or 273.15 precisely)
Absolute zero: 0 K = −273.15°C — theoretically the lowest possible temperature
Fixed Points & Key Values — Frequently Tested
  • Freezing point of water: 0°C = 32°F = 273 K
  • Boiling point of water: 100°C = 212°F = 373 K
  • Normal body temperature: 37°C = 98.6°F = 310 K
  • Absolute zero: −273.15°C = 0 K (molecules have zero kinetic energy — theoretically)
  • Celsius and Fahrenheit are equal at: −40° (−40°C = −40°F)
  • Triple point of water: 273.16 K = 0.01°C (water exists as solid, liquid, and gas simultaneously)

⚑ Exam Traps — Temperature Scales
  • Kelvin scale has no negative values — it starts at absolute zero (0 K)
  • −40°C = −40°F — the only temperature where both scales read the same; this is directly asked
  • Temperature difference of 1°C = temperature difference of 1 K (the step size is the same; only the zero points differ)
  • Kelvin is the SI unit of temperature — NOT Celsius
Section 2 — Specific Heat & Latent Heat
🔥
Specific Heat Capacity
Why water heats and cools slowly — and why it matters
▾

Specific heat capacity (c) is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1°C (or 1 K). It tells us how "heat-resistant" a material is.

Specific Heat Formula
Q = mcΔT
Q = heat absorbed or released (J)  ·  m = mass (kg)
c = specific heat capacity (J/kg·K)  ·  ΔT = temperature change (K or °C)
Key Specific Heat Values — Exam Essentials
  • Water: c = 4200 J/kg·K — the highest among common substances
  • Ice: c = 2100 J/kg·K (half that of liquid water)
  • Steam: c = 2010 J/kg·K
  • Copper: c ≈ 390 J/kg·K  ·  Iron: c ≈ 450 J/kg·K
  • Lead: c ≈ 128 J/kg·K (lowest among common metals)
  • Hydrogen: c ≈ 14,300 J/kg·K — highest specific heat of any gas
Why Water's High Specific Heat Matters
  • Coastal climates are milder than inland areas — the sea absorbs and releases heat slowly, moderating temperature extremes
  • Oceans act as heat reservoirs — regulating Earth's climate and temperature
  • Hot water bottles — water retains heat for a long time, staying warm for hours
  • Car radiators use water as coolant — it absorbs large amounts of heat from the engine
  • Human body — high water content (≈60%) helps maintain stable body temperature
⚑ Exam Trap

Water has the highest specific heat among common liquids. This is why sea breeze and land breeze occur — land (low specific heat) heats and cools faster than the sea (high specific heat). During the day, land heats up faster → hot air rises → sea breeze blows inland. At night, the reverse happens.

💧
Latent Heat
The hidden heat that changes state without changing temperature
▾

Latent heat is the heat absorbed or released by a substance during a change of state (solid ↔ liquid ↔ gas) at constant temperature. The temperature does not change while the state is changing.

Latent Heat Formula
Q = mL
Q = heat (J)  ·  m = mass (kg)  ·  L = specific latent heat (J/kg)

Latent heat of fusion (L_f): heat for solid → liquid at melting point
Latent heat of vaporisation (L_v): heat for liquid → gas at boiling point
Key Latent Heat Values for Water
  • Latent heat of fusion of ice: L_f = 3.36 × 10⁵ J/kg (334 kJ/kg)
  • Latent heat of vaporisation of water: L_v = 22.6 × 10⁵ J/kg (2260 kJ/kg)
  • L_v is about 7 times larger than L_f — steam burns are far more severe than boiling water burns
  • During a change of state, temperature remains constant — the heat goes into breaking/forming intermolecular bonds
Phenomena Explained by Latent Heat
  • Burns from steam are more severe than boiling water — steam gives up L_v (2260 kJ/kg) on condensing, in addition to cooling
  • Ice at 0°C cools drinks better than water at 0°C — ice must absorb L_f to melt, extracting much more heat
  • Sweating cools the body — sweat evaporates using L_v drawn from body heat
  • Spirit/alcohol evaporates quickly — feels cold on skin as it absorbs L_v
  • Desert coolers work — water evaporation absorbs latent heat, cooling air
Why steam burns are worse

When 1 kg of steam at 100°C touches your skin, it first condenses to water (releasing 2260 kJ of latent heat) — then the resulting boiling water cools from 100°C. A burn from water at 100°C only delivers the cooling heat. Steam delivers both — making steam burns far more dangerous than boiling water burns at the same temperature.

Section 3 — Gas Laws
🫧
Gas Laws — Boyle, Charles & Ideal Gas
How pressure, volume, and temperature of gases relate
▾
Boyle's Law Isothermal

At constant temperature: P ∝ 1/V → PV = constant. If pressure doubles, volume halves.

Charles' Law Isobaric

At constant pressure: V ∝ T (in Kelvin) → V/T = constant. Hot gas expands; cool gas contracts.

Gay-Lussac's Law Isochoric

At constant volume: P ∝ T (in Kelvin) → P/T = constant. Heating a sealed container increases pressure.

Ideal Gas Law Combined

PV = nRT or PV/T = constant. Combines all three laws. R = 8.314 J/mol·K (universal gas constant).

All Gas Laws Together
PV = nRT   →   P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂
Always use T in Kelvin (never Celsius) in gas law calculations
n = number of moles  ·  R = 8.314 J/mol·K

STP (Standard Temperature and Pressure): 0°C (273 K) and 1 atm
At STP, 1 mole of ideal gas occupies 22.4 litres
Real-World Applications of Gas Laws
  • Tyre bursts in summer — gas inside expands with temperature (Charles' Law); if pressure exceeds safe limit, tyre bursts
  • Pressure cooker — sealed vessel; heating increases pressure (Gay-Lussac's Law) → higher boiling point → food cooks faster
  • Bicycle pump — compressing gas increases pressure (Boyle's Law)
  • Weather balloon — expands at high altitude (lower atmospheric pressure) and eventually bursts
  • Hot air balloon — heated air expands, density decreases, balloon rises (Charles' Law)
  • Deep-sea diving — decompression sickness (bends) occurs when dissolved nitrogen comes out of blood rapidly as pressure decreases
⚑ Always Use Kelvin — Critical Trap
  • ALL gas law calculations must use temperature in Kelvin, not Celsius. 0°C ≠ 0 K. 0°C = 273 K.
  • Using Celsius in gas law problems is the single most common error in numerical questions
  • Boyle's Law: Temperature must be constant (isothermal process)
  • Charles' Law: Pressure must be constant (isobaric process)
Section 4 — Laws of Thermodynamics
⚖️
Zeroth, First & Second Laws
The fundamental laws that govern all energy transformations
▾
The Laws of Thermodynamics
  • Zeroth Law: If body A is in thermal equilibrium with body B, and body B is in thermal equilibrium with body C, then A and C are also in thermal equilibrium. This is the basis of temperature measurement (thermometers).
  • First Law (Conservation of Energy): The heat supplied to a system equals the increase in internal energy plus the work done by the system. Q = ΔU + W. Energy cannot be created or destroyed — only converted.
  • Second Law: Heat naturally flows from a hotter body to a colder body — never spontaneously the reverse. No heat engine can be 100% efficient. Entropy of an isolated system always increases.
  • Third Law: The entropy of a perfect crystal at absolute zero (0 K) is zero. Absolute zero can never be fully reached.
First Law of Thermodynamics
Q = ΔU + W
Q = heat added to system  ·  ΔU = change in internal energy  ·  W = work done BY the system

Isothermal process: T constant → ΔU = 0 → Q = W (all heat converted to work)
Adiabatic process: Q = 0 → ΔU = −W (work done at expense of internal energy)
Isochoric process: V constant → W = 0 → Q = ΔU (all heat increases internal energy)
Isobaric process: P constant → all three terms non-zero
ProcessConstantKey ResultExample
IsothermalTemperature (T)ΔU = 0; Q = WSlow compression of gas in contact with reservoir
AdiabaticHeat (Q = 0)ΔU = −W; temperature changesRapid compression; diesel engine compression
IsochoricVolume (V)W = 0; Q = ΔUHeating gas in a rigid sealed container
IsobaricPressure (P)All terms non-zeroGas heated in a cylinder with a free piston
Entropy — Second Law Implication

Entropy is a measure of disorder or randomness of a system. The second law says entropy always increases in an isolated system (or stays constant in reversible processes). This is why:
• Ice melts when placed in warm room (disorder increases)
• A dropped glass shatters — but shards never spontaneously reassemble
• Heat flows hot → cold — never cold → hot spontaneously

⚑ Exam Traps — Thermodynamic Laws
  • First law → perpetual motion machine of the first kind (creates energy from nothing) is impossible
  • Second law → perpetual motion machine of the second kind (100% efficient heat engine) is impossible
  • Refrigerator does NOT violate second law — it uses external work to transfer heat from cold to hot
  • Adiabatic process: no heat exchange, NOT no temperature change — temperature CAN change
⚙️
Heat Engines & Refrigerators
Carnot efficiency, heat pumps, and thermodynamic cycles
▾

A heat engine converts heat energy into mechanical work by operating between a hot source (T₁) and a cold sink (T₂). No real engine can be 100% efficient.

Carnot Efficiency — Maximum Possible
η = 1 − T₂/T₁ = (T₁ − T₂)/T₁
η = efficiency (between 0 and 1)  ·  T₁ = temperature of hot source (in Kelvin)  ·  T₂ = temperature of cold sink (in Kelvin)

Carnot efficiency is the maximum possible efficiency for any heat engine operating between T₁ and T₂. Real engines are always less efficient due to friction, heat losses, etc.
Heat Engine Work Output

Absorbs Q₁ from hot source, does work W, rejects Q₂ to cold sink. η = W/Q₁ = 1 − Q₂/Q₁. Examples: steam turbine, petrol engine, diesel engine.

Refrigerator Reverse Engine

Uses work W to transfer heat from cold reservoir (inside fridge) to hot reservoir (room). COP = Q₂/W. Does NOT violate 2nd law (external work is supplied).

⚑ Carnot Trap — Always Use Kelvin
  • Carnot formula: must use temperatures in Kelvin, not Celsius
  • Efficiency can only be 100% if T₂ = 0 K (absolute zero) — unachievable in practice
  • To increase efficiency: increase T₁ (hot source) OR decrease T₂ (cold sink) — or both
Section 5 — Heat Transfer
🌡️
Conduction, Convection & Radiation
The three modes by which heat travels
▾
ModeMechanismMedium needed?Examples
ConductionTransfer of heat through direct molecular vibration — no bulk movement of matterYes (solid preferred)Metal rod heated at one end; iron feels hotter than wood at same temp
ConvectionTransfer by bulk movement of fluid (liquid/gas) — hot fluid rises, cool fluid sinksYes (fluid)Boiling water, sea breeze, room heater, atmospheric circulation
RadiationTransfer by electromagnetic waves (infrared) — no medium neededNo (works in vacuum)Sun's heat reaching Earth, thermal camera, microwave oven
Thermal Conductivity — Key Comparisons
  • Best conductors: Silver > Copper > Gold > Aluminium (metals are good conductors due to free electrons)
  • Poor conductors (insulators): Wood, glass, rubber, plastic, air, wool, cotton
  • Metals feel colder than wood at the same temperature — metals conduct heat away from your hand faster
  • Newton's Law of Cooling: Rate of heat loss is proportional to the excess temperature of the body over its surroundings (valid for small temperature differences)
Radiation Laws — Stefan-Boltzmann & Wien's Law
  • Stefan-Boltzmann Law: Power radiated ∝ T⁴ (E = σT⁴). A body at higher temperature radiates enormously more energy — doubling absolute temperature → 16× radiation
  • Wien's Displacement Law: λ_max × T = constant (2.9 × 10⁻³ m·K). Hotter objects radiate at shorter wavelengths — a very hot star is blue-white; a cooler one is red
  • Black body: A perfect absorber AND emitter of radiation (absorptivity = emissivity = 1). The Sun is approximately a black body.
  • Good absorbers are good emitters (Kirchhoff's Law) — and poor absorbers (shiny/white surfaces) are poor emitters
Practical Applications of Heat Transfer
  • Thermos flask — silvered walls (reduces radiation); vacuum layer (eliminates conduction and convection)
  • Woollen clothes keep us warm — wool traps air (poor conductor), reduces convection and conduction
  • Two thin blankets warmer than one thick — air trapped between them acts as insulator
  • Double-glazed windows — air gap between panes reduces heat transfer
  • Dark/black surfaces absorb more radiation and emit more radiation
  • White/shiny surfaces reflect radiation — used in summer clothing, thermos flask silver lining
  • Sea breeze by day — land heats faster (convection); sea breeze blows from sea to land
  • Land breeze at night — land cools faster; breeze blows from land to sea
⚑ Key Traps — Heat Transfer
  • Radiation is the only mode that works in vacuum (how the Sun heats Earth)
  • Metals feel colder than wood at room temperature because of conduction, not actual temperature difference
  • A black body is a perfect absorber AND emitter — not just absorber. Black clothes are warmer in winter AND cooler in summer? No — they are always warmer because they also emit more. Wear white in summer (reflects more).
  • Newton's Law of Cooling is valid only for small temperature differences
Section 6 — Change of State & Humidity
❄️
Change of State & Effect of Pressure
Melting, boiling, regelation, and pressure cookers
▾

When pressure is applied, the melting and boiling points of substances change. This has important practical consequences.

Effect of Pressure on Melting & Boiling Points

Boiling point increases with pressure — higher pressure → higher boiling point. This is the principle of the pressure cooker: the sealed vessel raises steam pressure, raising the boiling point of water above 100°C, so food cooks faster.

Melting point of ice decreases with pressure (unusual property of water/ice — most substances show the opposite). Higher pressure → lower melting point of ice.

This leads to regelation: when a wire under pressure passes through a block of ice, the ice under the wire melts due to increased pressure; the water refreezes above the wire as pressure returns to normal — the wire passes through without cutting the ice in two.

Dew Point & Humidity — Exam Concepts
  • Dew point — the temperature at which air becomes saturated with water vapour and dew begins to form
  • Relative Humidity (RH) = (actual vapour pressure / saturation vapour pressure) × 100%
  • RH = 100% → air is fully saturated → condensation/dew/fog forms
  • Fog — occurs when RH reaches 100% near the ground; tiny water droplets suspended in air
  • Hygrometer — instrument used to measure humidity
  • Psychrometer (wet and dry bulb hygrometer) — most common type; uses difference between wet-bulb and dry-bulb temperatures
Pressure Cooker Principle

Inside a sealed pressure cooker, steam pressure builds up above atmospheric. At 2 atm, water boils at about 120°C instead of 100°C. Food cooks faster at higher temperatures. The steam valve prevents pressure from exceeding safe limits (safety valve is critical).

Why Mercury Is Used in Thermometers
  • Uniform expansion — mercury expands uniformly with temperature over a wide range
  • High boiling point — 357°C (doesn't boil at high temps)
  • Low freezing point — −39°C (remains liquid in cold conditions)
  • Does not wet glass — forms a clear meniscus (easy to read)
  • Opaque — visible in glass tube
  • Good conductor of heat — quickly reaches thermal equilibrium
Heat-Related Phenomena — All Frequently Tested
  • Greenhouse effect — glass/CO₂ is transparent to visible light but opaque to infrared (heat radiation). Sunlight enters, warms Earth, but heat (IR) cannot escape. Earth's average temperature rises.
  • Global warming — enhanced greenhouse effect due to excess CO₂, methane from human activities
  • Cooking in earthen pots — earthen pots are porous; water seeps out and evaporates, cooling the contents (latent heat)
  • Wearing black in winter — black absorbs more radiation, keeping you warmer
  • Ice factory / Refrigerator — based on the vapour compression cycle (reverse Carnot cycle)
  • Heat island effect — cities are warmer than surrounding rural areas due to concrete, asphalt, and reduced vegetation absorbing and retaining heat
