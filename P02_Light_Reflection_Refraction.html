Section 1 — Reflection of Light
🔁
Laws of Reflection
The two fundamental rules that govern all mirrors
▾

When light bounces off a surface, it obeys two laws — always, for any type of surface (plane or curved).

The Two Laws of Reflection

Law 1: The angle of incidence (∠i) = angle of reflection (∠r). Both are measured from the normal to the surface at the point of incidence.

Law 2: The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal — all lie in the same plane.

Key Definitions — Always Tested
  • Normal — an imaginary line perpendicular (90°) to the reflecting surface at the point of incidence
  • Angle of incidence (∠i) — angle between incident ray and the normal (NOT the surface)
  • Angle of reflection (∠r) — angle between reflected ray and the normal
  • Glancing angle — angle between incident ray and the surface itself = (90° − ∠i)
⚑ Exam Trap
  • Angles are always measured from the normal, NOT from the surface. Many students confuse glancing angle with angle of incidence.
  • If a ray falls normally (perpendicularly) on a mirror, it reflects straight back — ∠i = ∠r = 0°
  • Laws of reflection hold for all types of surfaces — plane, concave, convex, rough, smooth
Practical Example

If a plane mirror is rotated by angle θ, the reflected ray rotates by 2θ. This principle is used in galvanometers and periscopes.

🪞
Plane Mirror — Image Properties
Virtual, erect, laterally inverted — and always the same size
▾

A plane mirror is a flat, polished reflecting surface. The image it forms has specific properties that are directly tested in every exam.

Properties of Image in a Plane Mirror
  • Image is Virtual — cannot be projected on a screen; formed behind the mirror
  • Image is Erect — upright, same orientation as the object
  • Image is Laterally Inverted — left and right are swapped (your right hand appears as left hand in mirror)
  • Image size = Object size — magnification = 1
  • Image distance behind mirror = Object distance in front of mirror
  • For a full-length image of a person, mirror height = half the person's height
Number of Images in Two Mirrors — Formula
  • Two mirrors at angle θ: number of images = (360°/θ) − 1 (when 360/θ is even)
  • Mirrors at 90° → 3 images · At 60° → 5 images · At 45° → 7 images
  • Parallel mirrors (θ = 0°) → infinite images (used in barber shops)
⚑ Lateral Inversion vs Inversion

Lateral inversion means left ↔ right swap only. The image is NOT upside down. The word "AMBULANCE" is written mirror-reversed on ambulances so drivers can read it correctly in their rear-view mirrors.

🔭
Spherical Mirrors — Concave & Convex
Terminology, sign convention, mirror formula
▾

Spherical mirrors are sections of a hollow sphere. The concave mirror (reflecting surface is the inner side) converges light; the convex mirror (outer side) diverges light.

Key Terms — Learn All of These
  • Pole (P) — midpoint of the mirror surface
  • Centre of Curvature (C) — centre of the sphere of which the mirror is a part
  • Radius of Curvature (R) — distance from P to C
  • Principal Focus (F) — point where parallel rays meet (concave) or appear to diverge from (convex) after reflection
  • Focal Length (f) — distance from P to F  ·  f = R/2
  • Principal Axis — straight line through P and C
Mirror Formula & Magnification
1/f = 1/v + 1/u
f = focal length  ·  v = image distance  ·  u = object distance

Magnification: m = −v/u = h_i / h_o
m > 0 → erect image  ·  m < 0 → inverted image  ·  |m| > 1 → magnified
New Cartesian Sign Convention

All distances are measured from the pole (P).
• Distances in the direction of incident light (usually left to right) are positive.
• Distances opposite to incident light are negative.
• Object is always placed to the left of mirror, so u is always negative.
• For concave mirror: f is negative. For convex mirror: f is positive.

Concave Mirror Converging

Always forms real + inverted image (except when object is between F and P → virtual + erect). Used: shaving mirrors, dentist mirrors, solar furnaces, torch reflectors, satellite dishes.

Convex Mirror Diverging

Always forms a virtual, erect, diminished image — regardless of object position. Wider field of view. Used: rear-view mirrors in vehicles, security mirrors in shops, road bends.

⚑ Top Exam Traps — Spherical Mirrors
  • Convex mirror always gives virtual, erect, diminished image — no exceptions
  • Concave mirror gives virtual image only when object is between F and P
  • Focal length = R/2, NOT R. Confusing f and R is the #1 calculation error
  • Rear-view mirror in vehicles = convex (wider field of view)
  • Dentist's mirror = concave (magnified image of teeth)
Section 2 — Refraction of Light
📐
Laws of Refraction & Snell's Law
Why light bends when it changes medium
▾

Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one medium to another due to a change in its speed. Light slows down in denser media and bends toward the normal; it speeds up in rarer media and bends away.

Laws of Refraction

Law 1: The incident ray, refracted ray, and normal all lie in the same plane.

Law 2 (Snell's Law): n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂ — the ratio of sines of the angles of incidence and refraction is constant for a given pair of media.

Snell's Law
n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂
n = refractive index of the medium  ·  θ₁ = angle of incidence  ·  θ₂ = angle of refraction

Also: n = c / v  ·  c = speed of light in vacuum = 3 × 10⁸ m/s  ·  v = speed in medium
Higher n → denser medium → light travels slower
Refractive Indices — Frequently Tested Values
  • Vacuum / Air — n = 1.0 (light travels fastest here)
  • Water — n ≈ 1.33
  • Glass — n ≈ 1.5
  • Diamond — n ≈ 2.42 (highest among common materials — explains its brilliance)
  • Speed of light in vacuum — c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
Think of it this way

Imagine a car driving from a smooth road (fast) into mud (slow). The front wheel hitting the mud first slows down, causing the car to turn. That's exactly what happens to light — the part of the wavefront entering the denser medium slows and the beam bends toward the normal.

Everyday Refraction Phenomena
  • A pencil partially in water appears bent at the surface
  • A pool of water looks shallower than it actually is (apparent depth)
  • Stars appear to twinkle (atmospheric refraction)
  • The sun is visible slightly before it actually rises above the horizon (atmospheric refraction)
  • A coin in a glass of water appears raised
⚑ Exam Trap — What Changes, What Doesn't
  • During refraction, frequency does NOT change. Speed and wavelength change.
  • When light goes from rarer → denser medium: it bends toward the normal (angle decreases)
  • When light goes from denser → rarer medium: it bends away from the normal (angle increases)
  • A ray falling perpendicular to the surface (θ = 0°) does NOT bend — it passes straight through
💎
Total Internal Reflection (TIR)
Critical angle, optical fibres, mirage, diamond brilliance
▾

When light travels from a denser to a rarer medium and the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle, no refraction occurs — all light is reflected back. This is Total Internal Reflection.

Conditions for TIR

1. Light must travel from a denser medium to a rarer medium (e.g., glass to air, water to air).
2. Angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle (C).

Critical Angle Formula
sin C = 1/n  (or n = 1/sin C)
C = critical angle  ·  n = refractive index of the denser medium (w.r.t. air)
Higher n → smaller critical angle → TIR occurs more easily
Critical Angles — Exam Values
  • Water–Air: Critical angle ≈ 48.8°
  • Glass–Air: Critical angle ≈ 42°
  • Diamond–Air: Critical angle ≈ 24.4° (very small — almost all light reflects internally, giving brilliant sparkle)
Applications of TIR — All Frequently Tested
  • Optical Fibre — light trapped inside glass fibre by repeated TIR; used in internet cables, endoscopes, medical imaging
  • Diamond brilliance — very small critical angle (≈24°) causes most light to undergo TIR and emerge from the top, creating sparkle
  • Mirage — hot air near the ground is less dense; light from sky bends away from normal and undergoes TIR, creating an illusion of water on hot roads
  • Periscope & binoculars — use totally reflecting prisms (more efficient than mirrors)
  • Sparkling of glass prism — same principle as diamond
⚑ Exam Traps — TIR
  • TIR is possible only from denser to rarer medium — NEVER from rarer to denser
  • At exactly the critical angle, the refracted ray travels along the surface (θ_refraction = 90°)
  • Optical fibres work on TIR, not reflection from a silvered surface
  • Mirage is caused by TIR (atmospheric refraction + TIR), NOT simple reflection
Section 3 — Lenses
🔬
Convex & Concave Lenses
Lens formula, power, magnification, and image tables
▾

A convex (converging) lens is thicker at the centre; it converges parallel rays to a real focus. A concave (diverging) lens is thinner at the centre; it diverges rays and the focus is virtual.

Lens Formula & Power
1/f = 1/v − 1/u
Same variables as mirror formula but note the sign difference (minus, not plus).

Power of a lens: P = 1/f (in metres)  ·  Unit = Dioptre (D)
Convex lens: f positive → P positive
Concave lens: f negative → P negative

Combined lenses: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ ...

Convex Lens Converging

Used for: magnifying glass, camera, projector, telescope eyepiece, microscope, correction of hypermetropia (long-sightedness).

Concave Lens Diverging

Always gives virtual, erect, diminished image. Used for: correction of myopia (short-sightedness), peepholes in doors (spy-holes).

⚑ Critical Traps — Lenses
  • Lens formula uses 1/v − 1/u (minus sign), mirror formula uses 1/v + 1/u — extremely common error
  • Power is in Dioptres, NOT Watts. A lens of focal length 25 cm = 0.25 m → P = 1/0.25 = +4 D
  • Concave lens always forms virtual, erect, diminished image — no exception
  • Myopia corrected by concave lens; Hypermetropia by convex lens
Section 4 — Dispersion & Optical Phenomena
🌈
Dispersion of Light & Rainbow
Why a prism splits white light — and how rainbows form
▾

Dispersion is the splitting of white light into its constituent colours (spectrum) when it passes through a prism. It happens because different colours travel at different speeds in glass and are refracted by different amounts.

VIBGYOR — Spectrum Order
  • Order from top to bottom through a prism: V-I-B-G-Y-O-R (Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red)
  • Violet is deviated MOST (highest n, shortest wavelength, highest frequency)
  • Red is deviated LEAST (lowest n, longest wavelength, lowest frequency)
  • In a rainbow: Red is on the outer edge, Violet on the inner edge
  • Speed in glass: Red is fastest, Violet is slowest (opposite of frequency)
Rainbow Formation

A rainbow forms due to dispersion + total internal reflection + refraction inside raindrops. Sunlight enters a raindrop, disperses (splits into colours), undergoes TIR at the back of the drop, and exits at different angles. The observer sees different colours from different droplets simultaneously.

Memory Trick

"Roy G Biv" backwards = VIBGYOR. Red has the longest wavelength (≈700 nm), Violet the shortest (≈400 nm). Red bends least — think "Red = Relaxed = barely bends."

⚑ Exam Traps — Dispersion
  • In a rainbow, red is outside, violet is inside (opposite to prism orientation — confuses many)
  • The colour that undergoes maximum deviation in a prism = Violet
  • The colour with maximum speed in glass = Red
  • White light = mixture of all visible colours. Sunlight = white light.
  • Scattering (not dispersion): sky is blue because air scatters shorter wavelengths (blue) more — Rayleigh scattering

Human Eye & Defects of Vision
Myopia, hypermetropia, presbyopia — causes and corrections
▾

The human eye works like a convex lens camera. The crystalline lens focuses light on the retina. Defects occur when this focusing goes wrong.

DefectAlso CalledProblemImage FormsCorrected By
MyopiaShort-sightednessCan't see distant objects clearlyIn front of retinaConcave lens
HypermetropiaLong-sightednessCan't see near objects clearlyBehind retinaConvex lens
PresbyopiaOld-age sightCan't see near OR far (loss of accommodation)—Bifocal lens
Astigmatism—Unequal curvature of cornea; blurred vision—Cylindrical lens
Key Eye Facts — Frequently Asked
  • Persistence of vision — the eye retains an image for about 1/16th of a second; basis of cinema and animation
  • Near point of a normal eye = 25 cm
  • Far point of a normal eye = infinity
  • Power of accommodation — ability of the eye lens to change its focal length (curvature) to focus objects at varying distances
  • Blind spot — point on retina where optic nerve connects; no photoreceptors here; no image formed
  • Yellow spot (Fovea) — point of sharpest vision on retina

Atmospheric Optical Phenomena
Twinkling of stars, blue sky, red sunset — all explained
▾
PhenomenonCauseKey Detail
Twinkling of starsAtmospheric refraction (turbulent air layers)Stars are point sources — small changes in refraction cause apparent flickering. Planets do NOT twinkle (they are extended sources).
Blue skyRayleigh scattering by air moleculesBlue light (short wavelength) is scattered ~10× more than red. Sky appears blue to us.
Red sunrise/sunsetScattering — blue scattered away, red reaches eyeSun near horizon: light travels through more atmosphere, blue scattered away; only red/orange remain.
MirageTIR in hot air layers near groundHot road air is less dense (rarer). Light undergoes TIR, creating inverted image that looks like water.
Sun visible before sunriseAtmospheric refractionAtmosphere bends light, making sun appear above horizon before it actually is. Day is longer than night due to this.
LoomingAtmospheric refraction (cold air near sea)Cold dense air at sea level — objects below horizon appear raised. Opposite of mirage.
⚑ Stars vs Planets — Twinkling

Stars twinkle because they are so far away they act as point sources. Planets are close enough to appear as extended discs — the multiple beams average out, so planets do NOT twinkle. This is a CDS/NDA favourite.

Optical Instruments — Applications
Which mirror/lens goes where — a must-memorise list
▾
Mirror & Lens Applications — Learn Each One
  • Rear-view mirror (vehicles) → Convex mirror (wider field of view, virtual image)
  • Dentist / ENT doctor mirror → Concave mirror (magnified, virtual, erect image)
  • Shaving / makeup mirror → Concave mirror (magnified image when close)
  • Torch / headlight / searchlight reflector → Concave mirror (placed at F, produces parallel beam)
  • Solar furnace / solar cooker → Large concave mirror (converges sun's rays to F)
  • Shops / banks security mirror → Convex mirror (wide-angle view)
  • Periscope → Two plane mirrors at 45° (submarines, tanks)
  • Telescope (Astronomical) → Concave objective + convex eyepiece
  • Compound microscope → Two convex lenses (objective + eyepiece)
  • Spectacles for myopia → Concave lens
  • Spectacles for hypermetropia → Convex lens
  • Camera → Convex lens (real, inverted, diminished image on film/sensor)
  • Magnifying glass → Convex lens (object inside F → virtual, erect, magnified)
  • Projector / slide projector → Convex lens (real, inverted, magnified image on screen)

Master Formula Sheet — Light

  • Mirror Formula: 1/f = 1/v + 1/u  ·  f = R/2
  • Mirror Magnification: m = −v/u
  • Snell's Law: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂

  • Critical Angle: sin C = 1/n
  • Lens Formula: 1/f = 1/v − 1/u
  • Lens Power: P = 1/f(m)  ·  Unit = Dioptre (D)
  • Combined Power: P = P₁ + P₂ + ...
  • Near point of normal eye: 25 cm
Mirror vs Lens — Key Differences
Mirror vs Lens Formula Comparison
  • Mirror: 1/f = 1/v + 1/u  ·  Concave f is negative
  • Lens: 1/f = 1/v − 1/u  ·  Convex f is positive
  • Mirror sign: All real distances negative (object left of mirror)
  • Lens sign: Object always left → u negative; real image → v positive
  • Concave mirror ↔ Convex lens — both are converging; similar image tables
  • Convex mirror ↔ Concave lens — both are diverging; always virtual image
Always Virtual Image — Both Situations
When is the image ALWAYS virtual?
  • Plane mirror — always virtual, erect, same size
  • Convex mirror — always virtual, erect, diminished
  • Concave lens — always virtual, erect, diminished
  • Concave mirror (object between F and P) → virtual, erect, magnified
  • Convex lens (object between F and O) → virtual, erect, magnified
