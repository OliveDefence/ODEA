The Respiratory System
Breathing mechanics, gas exchange, transport of O₂ & CO₂, respiratory volumes and disorders — at CDS, NDA & AFCAT exam depth.
CDS NDA AFCAT Airways Gas Exchange Lung Volumes

Respiration in physiology refers to the entire process of gas exchange between an organism and its environment — taking in O₂ and releasing CO₂. The respiratory system provides the anatomical machinery for this exchange. It includes the airway (nose → lungs) and the breathing mechanism (diaphragm and chest muscles). This is distinct from cellular respiration (energy release in cells — covered in B03).

Section 1 — Organs of the Respiratory System
🫁
The Airway — Path of Air
From nasal cavity to alveoli
▾

Air travels through a series of progressively narrowing passages before reaching the alveoli where gas exchange occurs. Each region has a specific structural and protective role.

1
Nasal Cavity
Air enters through the nostrils (external nares). Nasal hair and mucus filter dust and pathogens. Nasal conchae warm and humidify incoming air. The olfactory epithelium in the roof provides the sense of smell.
2
Pharynx & Larynx
Pharynx (throat) — common passage for air and food. Epiglottis covers the larynx during swallowing to prevent food entering the airway. Larynx (voice box) contains the vocal cords; generates sound by vibration of air.
3
Trachea (Windpipe)
~12 cm long tube reinforced by C-shaped cartilage rings (incomplete — open posteriorly) to keep the airway open. Lined by ciliated epithelium with goblet cells producing mucus — the "mucociliary escalator" traps and sweeps debris upward.
4
Bronchi & Bronchioles
Trachea divides at the carina into the right and left primary bronchi (right bronchus is shorter, wider, and more vertical — more common site of inhaled foreign body lodgement). Bronchi further divide into secondary → tertiary bronchi → bronchioles (no cartilage, smooth muscle walls).
5
Alveoli — Site of Gas Exchange
Tiny air sacs (~300 million in each lung). Walls are extremely thin (one cell thick — simple squamous epithelium). Surrounded by a rich capillary network. Surfactant (secreted by Type II pneumocytes) prevents alveolar collapse. Total surface area: ~70 m² in adults.

⚑ Exam Traps — Airways
  • The right bronchus is shorter, wider and more vertical — inhaled foreign objects (coins, peanuts) lodge more commonly in the right bronchus.
  • Bronchioles have no cartilage — they are held open only by smooth muscle tone and lung elastic recoil. This is why asthma (bronchospasm) causes such severe narrowing.
  • The trachea has C-shaped (not complete O-shaped) cartilage rings — the opening is posterior, allowing the oesophagus to expand during swallowing.

Pleura — The Double Membrane Around Lungs

Each lung is covered by two layers of pleura: the visceral pleura (inner — directly covers the lung) and the parietal pleura (outer — lines the chest wall). Between them is the pleural cavity containing a thin film of pleural fluid that lubricates lung movement and creates surface tension that keeps the lung expanded. Pleuritis (pleurisy) = inflammation of the pleura, causing painful breathing.

⚑ Exam Trap

Pneumothorax — if air enters the pleural cavity (e.g., due to a stab wound or spontaneous rupture), the negative pressure is lost and the lung collapses. The pleural fluid's surface tension is what normally holds the lungs expanded against the chest wall.

Section 2 — Mechanism of Breathing
💨
Inspiration & Expiration
How pressure changes drive airflow
▾

Breathing is driven by pressure differences between the lungs and atmosphere, created by changes in chest volume (Boyle's Law: P ∝ 1/V). The diaphragm is the primary muscle of respiration.

PhaseInspiration (Inhalation)Expiration (Exhalation)
DiaphragmContracts & flattens (moves down)Relaxes — domes upward
External intercostalsContract — ribs move up & outRelax
Chest volumeIncreasesDecreases
Lung pressureDecreases below atmospheric → air flows inIncreases above atmospheric → air flows out
Energy requirementActive (requires muscle contraction)Passive (elastic recoil of lungs — no muscles needed normally)
Think of it this way

The lung is like a bellows. When you pull the handles apart (chest expands), pressure inside drops and air rushes in. When you push them together (chest contracts), air is forced out.

Key Breathing Facts
  • Normal breathing rate at rest: 12–20 breaths/minute
  • Tidal volume (normal breath): 500 mL
  • Breathing is controlled by the respiratory centre in the medulla oblongata (brainstem)
  • Primary stimulus for breathing: rising CO₂ levels (not falling O₂) detected by chemoreceptors
  • Forced expiration — uses internal intercostals and abdominal muscles (active)
⚑ Exam Trap

Normal expiration is passive — it requires no muscular effort; it is driven by the elastic recoil of lung tissue. Only forced expiration (e.g., blowing out a candle, coughing) requires active muscular contraction. This is frequently tested.

Memory Aid — Volumes & Capacities
  • Tidal Volume (TV) = 500 mL — the baseline number everything builds on
  • Vital Capacity (VC) = TV + IRV + ERV ≈ 4.6 L — what spirometry measures; decreases in lung disease
  • Total Lung Capacity (TLC) = VC + RV ≈ 5.8 L — maximum possible air in lungs
  • Residual Volume (RV) — cannot be measured by spirometry alone (lungs never fully empty)
  • Trained athletes have higher VC than untrained individuals
⚑ Exam Trap

Residual Volume (RV) cannot be measured by a simple spirometer — it requires special techniques (gas dilution, body plethysmography). This is because you can never exhale all the air from your lungs. Only capacities that include RV (TLC, FRC) also cannot be measured by spirometry alone.

Section 4 — Gas Exchange & Transport
🔄
Gas Exchange — Alveoli & Tissues
How O₂ and CO₂ cross membranes by diffusion
▾

Gas exchange occurs by simple diffusion along partial pressure gradients. Gases move from high partial pressure → low partial pressure.

LocationO₂ MovementCO₂ Movement
Alveoli ↔ Blood (External respiration)pO₂ in alveoli (104 mmHg) > pO₂ in deoxygenated blood (40 mmHg) → O₂ diffuses into bloodpCO₂ in blood (45 mmHg) > pCO₂ in alveoli (40 mmHg) → CO₂ diffuses into alveoli
Blood ↔ Tissues (Internal respiration)pO₂ in blood (95 mmHg) > pO₂ in tissues (40 mmHg) → O₂ diffuses into tissuespCO₂ in tissues (45 mmHg) > pCO₂ in blood (40 mmHg) → CO₂ diffuses into blood
Factors Favouring Efficient Gas Exchange at Alveoli

① Large surface area (~70 m² — 300 million alveoli). ② Thin walls (~0.2 µm — simple squamous epithelium + capillary endothelium). ③ Rich blood supply — dense capillary network. ④ Steep partial pressure gradient maintained by constant ventilation and blood flow. ⑤ Moist surface — gases dissolve in moisture first.

🩸
Transport of O₂ & CO₂ in Blood
Haemoglobin, oxyhaemoglobin and the chloride shift
▾
Oxygen Transport — 3 Forms
  • As oxyhaemoglobin (HbO₂) — ~97% of O₂; Hb binds O₂ in lungs (high pO₂), releases it in tissues (low pO₂). Each Hb molecule carries 4 O₂ molecules.
  • Dissolved in plasma — ~3% only (O₂ is poorly soluble in water)
  • 1 gram of Hb carries ~1.34 mL of O₂ (O₂-carrying capacity)
Carbon Dioxide Transport — 3 Forms
  • As bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻) — ~70% of CO₂ (most important). CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ (catalysed by carbonic anhydrase in RBCs). HCO₃⁻ moves into plasma (chloride shift balances charge).
  • Bound to haemoglobin as carbaminohaemoglobin — ~23% of CO₂ binds to the protein part of Hb (NOT to haem — different from O₂ binding).
  • Dissolved in plasma — ~7% (CO₂ more soluble than O₂)
Haemoglobin — Key Facts
  • Hb is a conjugated protein with 4 subunits — each subunit has a haem group containing iron (Fe²⁺)
  • Normal Hb level: males ~14–16 g/dL; females ~12–14 g/dL
  • Bohr Effect: increased CO₂ / decreased pH → decreased Hb affinity for O₂ → more O₂ released to active tissues
  • CO (carbon monoxide) binds Hb with 200× more affinity than O₂ → forms carboxyhaemoglobin → blocks O₂ transport → CO poisoning
  • Foetal Hb (HbF) has higher affinity for O₂ than adult Hb (HbA) — allows foetus to extract O₂ from maternal blood
⚑ Exam Traps — Gas Transport
  • CO₂ binds to the protein (globin) part of Hb, NOT to the haem group. O₂ binds to the haem (iron) part. These are different binding sites.
  • The Bohr Effect is about O₂ release — high CO₂ / low pH pushes O₂ OFF haemoglobin. Very commonly tested.
  • Carbonic anhydrase is the enzyme that catalyses CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃. Acetazolamide (a drug) inhibits it — used for altitude sickness.
  • Most CO₂ is transported as bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) — NOT as carbaminohaemoglobin.
Section 5 — Regulation of Breathing
🧠
Neural & Chemical Control of Breathing
Medulla, chemoreceptors and the CO₂ drive
▾

Breathing is an involuntary rhythmic process controlled by the respiratory centre in the brainstem.

Medulla Oblongata Primary centre

Houses DRG (Dorsal Respiratory Group — controls inspiration) and VRG (Ventral — controls forced expiration). Sets the basic rhythm.

Pons Modifier

Contains pneumotaxic centre (limits inspiration duration) and apneustic centre (prolongs inspiration). Fine-tunes rhythm.

Central Chemoreceptors Medulla

Detect rising CO₂ and H⁺ in cerebrospinal fluid. Most potent stimulus for increasing breathing rate.

Peripheral Chemoreceptors Carotid & Aortic bodies

Detect falling O₂ (hypoxia), rising CO₂, and rising H⁺ in blood. Secondary stimulus; primary for hypoxic drive at high altitude.

Primary Stimulus for Breathing — CO₂, NOT O₂

The primary chemical stimulus for normal breathing is an increase in blood CO₂ (hypercapnia), not a decrease in O₂. CO₂ crosses the blood-brain barrier and lowers pH in the CSF, directly stimulating central chemoreceptors. A decrease in O₂ (hypoxia) only becomes a significant drive at very low O₂ levels (pO₂ < 60 mmHg) — important in patients with COPD.

⚑ Exam Trap — CO₂ vs O₂ Drive

In healthy individuals, CO₂ is the primary respiratory stimulus. In patients with chronic lung disease (COPD) who retain CO₂, chemoreceptors become desensitised to CO₂ and they shift to an "hypoxic drive" (low O₂ stimulates breathing). Giving such patients high-flow O₂ without monitoring can paradoxically suppress their breathing — a classic clinical MCQ.

High-Yield Disease Facts
  • TB causative organism: Mycobacterium tuberculosis — also called Koch's bacillus (Robert Koch, 1882)
  • BCG vaccine (Bacille Calmette-Guérin) — protects against TB; given at birth in India

Section 7 — Special Topics for Exams
⭐
High-Altitude Physiology & Other Key Facts
Acclimatisation, diving, artificial respiration
▾
High Altitude — Acclimatisation Response
  • At high altitude, atmospheric pO₂ falls → hypoxia
  • Immediate: ↑ breathing rate (hyperventilation)
  • Short term: ↑ heart rate, ↑ cardiac output
  • Long term (days–weeks): ↑ RBC production (erythropoiesis) due to erythropoietin (EPO) secretion by kidneys → ↑ Hb → more O₂ carrying capacity
  • Altitude sickness symptoms: headache, nausea, fatigue at >2500 m
Other Frequently Tested Facts
  • Respiratory quotient (RQ): ratio of CO₂ produced to O₂ consumed. Carbohydrates: RQ = 1.0 · Fats: RQ ≈ 0.7 · Proteins: RQ ≈ 0.8 · Mixed diet: RQ ≈ 0.85
  • Surfactant: produced by Type II alveolar cells (pneumocytes); reduces surface tension; prevents alveolar collapse. Absent in premature infants → Infant Respiratory Distress Syndrome (IRDS)
  • Diaphragm: the most important muscle of breathing; innervated by the phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5)
  • Yawning: reflex to increase O₂ and expel excess CO₂ when shallow breathing causes CO₂ build-up
  • Hiccup: involuntary diaphragm spasm followed by sudden closure of glottis
Composition of Inhaled vs Exhaled Air

Inhaled air: O₂ 21%, CO₂ 0.04%, N₂ 78%. Exhaled air: O₂ ~16%, CO₂ ~4%, N₂ ~78%. The body consumes ~5% of O₂ per breath and produces ~4% CO₂. Nitrogen is physiologically inert — same percentage in and out.
