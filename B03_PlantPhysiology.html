Plant Physiology
How plants breathe, feed, move water, grow and respond — the complete functional biology of the plant body. Essential for CDS, NDA & AFCAT.
CDS NDA AFCAT Photosynthesis Transport Hormones

Plant Physiology is the study of the functional processes that occur within plants — how they make food, absorb and transport water and minerals, exchange gases, grow and respond to their environment. These processes are governed by physics, chemistry, and biology all at once.

Section 1 — Photosynthesis
☀️
Photosynthesis — Overview & Equation
How plants convert sunlight into food
▾

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants (and some algae/bacteria) convert light energy into chemical energy stored as glucose. It takes place in the chloroplasts, specifically in the thylakoid membranes and stroma.

Overall Equation

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Light Energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ (Glucose) + 6O₂

Key Facts — Frequently Tested
  • Raw materials: Carbon dioxide (CO₂) from air + Water (H₂O) from soil
  • Site: Chloroplast — the green pigment chlorophyll absorbs light
  • Chlorophyll absorbs mainly red and blue light; reflects green (that's why leaves look green)
  • O₂ released is a by-product — it comes from the splitting of water (photolysis)
  • Carbon is fixed from CO₂ into organic compounds (sugars)
Think of it this way

A plant is like a solar-powered factory. Sunlight is the electricity, CO₂ and water are the raw materials, and glucose is the finished product stored in the warehouse.

⚑ Exam Trap

The oxygen released in photosynthesis comes from water (H₂O), NOT from CO₂. This is confirmed by isotope labelling experiments (using ¹⁸O). This is one of the most frequently tested misconceptions.

Light Reactions vs Dark Reactions
The two stages of photosynthesis
▾

Photosynthesis occurs in two main stages:

FeatureLight Reactions (Photo-phase)Dark Reactions (Thermo-phase / Calvin Cycle)
Requires light?Yes — directly needs lightDoes not directly need light (but depends on products of light reactions)
LocationThylakoid membranes (grana)Stroma of chloroplast
What happensWater is split (photolysis) → O₂ released, ATP and NADPH formedCO₂ is fixed into glucose using ATP and NADPH
ProductsATP, NADPH, O₂Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), ADP, NADP⁺
Key cycle/processPhotosystems I and II (Z-scheme)Calvin Cycle (C3 pathway); discovered by Melvin Calvin
C3 vs C4 Plants — Exam Favourite
  • C3 plants — first product of CO₂ fixation is a 3-carbon compound (3-PGA). Most common. Examples: wheat, rice, potato, most trees
  • C4 plants — first product is a 4-carbon compound (OAA). Adapted to hot, dry conditions; more efficient. Examples: maize (corn), sugarcane, sorghum, millets
  • CAM plants (Crassulacean Acid Metabolism) — fix CO₂ at night (stomata open at night to reduce water loss). Examples: cacti, pineapple, succulents
⚑ Exam Traps
  • "Dark reactions" does NOT mean they happen only at night — they happen in the light too, just don't directly need light.
  • Maize and sugarcane are C4 plants — NOT C3. Rice and wheat are C3. This specific comparison is tested.
  • The Calvin cycle was discovered by Melvin Calvin — he received the Nobel Prize for this.
🌿
Factors Affecting Photosynthesis
Blackman's Law of Limiting Factors
▾

The rate of photosynthesis is controlled by several environmental and internal factors. Blackman's Law of Limiting Factors (1905) states that when a process is conditioned by several factors, the rate is limited by the factor that is at its minimum.

Light Intensity Key factor

Rate increases with light intensity up to a light saturation point, after which it plateaus.

CO₂ Concentration Most limiting

CO₂ is the most common limiting factor under natural conditions. Increasing CO₂ boosts rate.

Temperature Optimum 25–35°C

Affects enzyme-driven dark reactions. Optimum around 25–35°C; too high denatures enzymes.

Water Indirect

Severe water stress closes stomata (to prevent water loss), limiting CO₂ entry and thus photosynthesis.

Compensation Point

The compensation point is the light intensity at which the rate of photosynthesis exactly equals the rate of respiration. Below this, the plant consumes more O₂ than it produces. Above it, net O₂ is released to the environment.

Section 2 — Respiration in Plants
🔥
Cellular Respiration
How plants release energy from glucose
▾

Respiration is the process by which living cells break down glucose to release energy in the form of ATP. Plants (and ALL living organisms) respire. Unlike photosynthesis, respiration occurs in ALL cells, ALL the time (day and night).

Overall Equation (Aerobic Respiration)

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy (38 ATP)

FeatureAerobic RespirationAnaerobic Respiration
Oxygen needed?YesNo
LocationCytoplasm (glycolysis) + MitochondriaCytoplasm only
ATP yield36–38 ATP per glucose2 ATP per glucose
End productsCO₂ + H₂OCO₂ + Ethanol (in plants/yeast) OR Lactic acid (in animals)
ExamplesNormal plant and animal cellsYeast (fermentation), germinating seeds, waterlogged roots
Stages of Aerobic Respiration
  • Glycolysis — in cytoplasm; glucose (6C) → 2 pyruvate (3C) + 2 ATP + 2 NADH
  • Krebs Cycle (TCA Cycle) — in mitochondrial matrix; pyruvate fully oxidised; produces NADH, FADH₂, CO₂, 2 ATP
  • Electron Transport Chain (ETC) — in inner mitochondrial membrane; uses NADH and FADH₂ to produce bulk of ATP (34 ATP); O₂ is the final electron acceptor → forms H₂O
⚑ Exam Trap

Plants carry out both photosynthesis AND respiration. During the day, both occur simultaneously. At night, only respiration occurs. The net exchange depends on which process dominates — at the compensation point, they are equal.

Section 3 — Transport in Plants
💧
Osmosis, Diffusion & Active Transport
How water and substances move in and out of cells
▾
ProcessDefinitionEnergy needed?Example in plants
DiffusionMovement of molecules from high to low concentrationNo (passive)CO₂ enters leaf through stomata; O₂ exits
OsmosisDiffusion of water through a semi-permeable membrane from high water potential to lowNo (passive)Water absorption by root hair cells from soil
Active TransportMovement from low to high concentration against the gradientYes (ATP)Mineral ion absorption by root cells
Osmosis — Key Terms

Turgid: cell full of water, firm (ideal state for most plant cells). Flaccid: cell has lost water, limp. Plasmolysis: cell placed in hypertonic solution → cell membrane pulls away from cell wall (can kill cells). Turgor pressure: pressure of cell contents against cell wall — gives rigidity to herbaceous plants (wilting = loss of turgor).

⚑ Exam Trap

Osmosis is always about water movement only, not solutes. The direction is: water moves from hypotonic (dilute, high water potential) to hypertonic (concentrated, low water potential) solution. Plant cells in pure water become turgid (not bursting, because cell wall prevents lysis).

🌊
Ascent of Sap — Water Transport in Xylem
How water rises against gravity to the top of tall trees
▾

Water absorbed by roots must travel up through xylem to leaves — sometimes 100+ metres in tall trees. This upward movement of water is called Ascent of Sap.

The Cohesion-Tension Theory (Dixon & Joly) — Most Accepted
  • Transpiration from leaves creates a pull (tension/negative pressure) at the top
  • Cohesion — water molecules stick to each other (H-bonds), forming an unbroken water column
  • Adhesion — water molecules stick to xylem walls, preventing column collapse
  • This creates a continuous water column pulled upward by transpiration pull
  • Also called TACT mechanism: Transpiration pull + Adhesion + Cohesion + Tension
Other Forces Contributing to Ascent of Sap
  • Root pressure — osmotic pressure generated in roots pushes water upward; effective only in short plants or at night
  • Capillary action — minor contribution due to narrow xylem vessels
🌬️
Transpiration
The loss of water vapour from plant surfaces
▾

Transpiration is the evaporation of water from the aerial parts of a plant, mainly through stomata. It is sometimes called the "necessary evil" — unavoidable but also essential for water transport.

Types of Transpiration
  • Stomatal transpiration — through stomata; accounts for 80–90% of total transpiration
  • Cuticular transpiration — through the waxy cuticle on leaf surface; 5–10%
  • Lenticular transpiration — through lenticels on stems; negligible
Factors Affecting Transpiration Rate
  • Temperature ↑ → transpiration rate ↑ (more evaporation)
  • Humidity ↑ → transpiration rate ↓ (less gradient for evaporation)
  • Wind speed ↑ → transpiration rate ↑ (removes humid air from leaf surface)
  • Light ↑ → stomata open → transpiration ↑
  • Water stress → stomata close → transpiration ↓
Stomata — Guard Cells & Opening Mechanism

Stomata are pores in the leaf epidermis flanked by two guard cells. When guard cells are turgid (full of water), they bow outward → stomata open. When flaccid, they collapse → stomata close. Light triggers K⁺ ion uptake into guard cells, increasing osmotic pressure, drawing water in → turgidity → opening.

⚑ Exam Trap

Transpiration serves a cooling function (like sweating in animals) AND drives the ascent of sap. However, it also causes water loss which is detrimental in dry conditions. ABA (Abscisic acid) is the hormone that causes stomata to CLOSE under water stress — frequently tested.

⚑ Exam Traps — Mineral Nutrition
  • Magnesium is the central element of chlorophyll — NOT iron. Iron helps in chlorophyll SYNTHESIS but is not part of the chlorophyll molecule.
  • Nitrogen deficiency shows in older (lower) leaves first because N is mobile and moves from old to new leaves.
  • Calcium and boron deficiencies appear in young/growing parts first — they are immobile elements.
🔬
Nitrogen Fixation & Nitrogen Cycle
How atmospheric N₂ becomes usable for plants
▾

Plants cannot use atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) directly. It must be fixed — converted to ammonia (NH₃) or nitrates (NO₃⁻) — before plants can absorb it.

Nitrogen Fixation — Types & Key Organisms
  • Biological N-fixation — most important; by bacteria using enzyme nitrogenase
  • Rhizobium — lives in root nodules of legumes (pea, bean, soybean); symbiotic relationship
  • Azotobacter — free-living in soil (aerobic)
  • Clostridium — free-living in soil (anaerobic)
  • Nostoc, Anabaena — cyanobacteria; fix N₂ in paddy fields and aquatic ecosystems
  • Frankia — fixes N₂ in non-legume plants (e.g., Alnus)
Nitrogen Cycle — Key Steps
  • Nitrogen Fixation: N₂ → NH₃ (by bacteria or lightning)
  • Nitrification: NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (by Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter bacteria)
  • Assimilation: plants absorb NO₃⁻, incorporate into proteins
  • Ammonification: dead organic matter → NH₃ (by decomposers)
  • Denitrification: NO₃⁻ → N₂ back to atmosphere (by Pseudomonas, Thiobacillus denitrificans)
⚑ Exam Trap

Nitrification (NH₃ → NO₃⁻) and Denitrification (NO₃⁻ → N₂) are opposite processes. Confusing these is extremely common. Nitrification = good for plants (makes usable nitrates). Denitrification = reduces soil fertility.

Section 5 — Plant Growth Regulators (Hormones)
🧪
Plant Hormones — Overview
The 5 major classes and their roles
▾

Plant Growth Regulators (PGRs) or plant hormones are small organic molecules that coordinate plant growth and development. They act at very low concentrations and may be promoters or inhibitors of growth.

HormoneNatureKey FunctionsExam-Important Detail
Auxins (IAA)PromoterCell elongation, phototropism, apical dominance, root initiationProduced at shoot tip; high concentration inhibits lateral bud growth (apical dominance)
Gibberellins (GA)PromoterStem elongation, seed germination, breaking dormancy, fruit developmentCause bolting (sudden stem elongation) in rosette plants; discovered in Gibberella fujikuroi (foolish seedling disease of rice)
CytokininsPromoterCell division (cytokinesis), delay leaf senescence, promote lateral bud growthAntagonistic to auxins for lateral bud growth; delay ageing
EthylenePromoter/InhibitorFruit ripening, abscission (leaf/fruit fall), promotes senescenceOnly gaseous plant hormone; responsible for "one rotten apple spoils the barrel"
ABA (Abscisic Acid)InhibitorStomatal closure under stress, seed dormancy, inhibits growthCalled the "stress hormone" or "dormin"; causes wilting
Memory Tricks — Plant Hormones
  • Auxin — Apical dominance; Allows elongation
  • Gibberellin — Growth in height; Germination
  • Cytokinin — Cell division; Counters ageing
  • Ethylene — Early ripening; Exit (abscission)
  • ABA — Always Blocks Action (inhibitor, closes stomata)
⚑ Exam Traps — Hormones
  • Ethylene is a gas — the only gaseous plant hormone. All others are not gaseous.
  • ABA causes stomata to CLOSE — not open. Never reverse this.
  • Gibberellins discovered in a fungus (Gibberella) that caused "bakanae" (foolish seedling disease) in rice — plants grew abnormally tall.
  • Auxin at HIGH concentration inhibits lateral roots and shoots but promotes root initiation at low concentration.
Section 6 — Plant Movements & Responses
🌻
Tropisms & Nastic Movements
How plants move and respond to stimuli
▾

Plants cannot run from danger, but they grow and respond directionally to stimuli. These movements are called tropic movements (tropisms) — growth movements controlled by the direction of the stimulus.

Phototropism Light

Shoots grow toward light (positive). Roots grow away (negative). Controlled by auxin.

Geotropism Gravity

Roots grow downward = positive geotropism. Shoots grow upward = negative geotropism.

Thigmotropism Touch

Climbing plants wrap around supports. Tendrils of pea plants are the classic example.

Hydrotropism Water

Roots grow toward water — positive hydrotropism. Helps roots find water in soil.

Chemotropism Chemical

Growth toward or away from a chemical. Example: pollen tube grows toward the ovule.

Nastic Movements Non-directional

Response NOT related to direction of stimulus. Example: Mimosa pudica (touch-me-not) — leaves fold inward when touched.

Photoperiodism & Vernalisation

Photoperiodism: the response of plants to the relative duration of light and dark periods — controls flowering. Long-day plants (spinach, radish) flower with long days. Short-day plants (rice, soybean, chrysanthemum) flower with short days. Day-neutral plants (tomato, sunflower) flower regardless. Vernalisation: exposure to prolonged cold temperature promotes flowering (e.g., wheat, rye).

Photosynthesis — Must-Know Facts
  • Equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • O₂ released comes from water (H₂O), not CO₂
  • Light reactions → thylakoid; Dark reactions (Calvin) → stroma
  • C3 plants: rice, wheat, potato · C4: maize, sugarcane, sorghum
  • CAM plants open stomata at night (cacti, pineapple)
  • Chlorophyll absorbs red + blue light; reflects green
  • Blackman's Law: rate limited by the factor at minimum
Respiration — Key Points
  • Aerobic: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 38 ATP
  • Anaerobic: glucose → CO₂ + ethanol (plants/yeast) + 2 ATP
  • Glycolysis → cytoplasm · Krebs cycle → mitochondrial matrix
  • ETC → inner mitochondrial membrane; O₂ = final electron acceptor
  • Plants respire ALL the time (day + night)
  • Compensation point = photosynthesis rate = respiration rate
Water Transport & Transpiration
  • Osmosis = water movement through semi-permeable membrane
  • Active transport needs ATP; moves against concentration gradient
  • Ascent of sap: Cohesion-Tension theory (Dixon & Joly) — TACT
  • Root pressure = minor force; effective in short plants / at night
  • 80–90% transpiration through stomata
  • Guard cells turgid → stomata open · flaccid → closed
  • ABA causes stomata closure under drought stress
Plant Hormones — Quick Table
  • Auxin (IAA) — elongation, apical dominance, phototropism
  • Gibberellin — height, germination, bolting; from fungus Gibberella
  • Cytokinin — cell division, delays senescence, lateral buds
  • Ethylene — fruit ripening, abscission; only gaseous hormone
  • ABA — stress hormone; closes stomata; seed dormancy
Nitrogen & Mineral Nutrition
  • Mg = central atom of chlorophyll · Fe = needed for chlorophyll synthesis
  • N deficiency → chlorosis (older leaves first — N is mobile)
  • Ca, B deficiency → young/growing parts first (immobile)
  • Rhizobium — symbiotic in legume root nodules
  • Azotobacter — free-living, aerobic · Clostridium — anaerobic
  • Nitrification (NH₃→NO₃⁻): Nitrosomonas + Nitrobacter
  • Denitrification (NO₃⁻→N₂): Pseudomonas, Thiobacillus
Tropisms & Responses
  • Phototropism — light; shoot = positive; controlled by auxin
  • Geotropism — gravity; root = positive (downward)
  • Thigmotropism — touch; climbing plants, tendrils
  • Nastic — non-directional; Mimosa (touch-me-not) folds
  • Long-day plants: spinach, radish · Short-day: rice, soybean
