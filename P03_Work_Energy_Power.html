Section 1 — Work
🔩
Definition of Work
What physicists actually mean by "work"
▾

In physics, work is done when a force causes a displacement in the direction of the force. Simply applying force without movement does no work.

Formula
W = F · d · cos θ
W = Work done (Joules, J)  ·  F = Force (Newtons, N)  ·  d = Displacement (metres, m)  ·  θ = Angle between force and displacement
Key Conditions — Frequently Tested
  • θ = 0° → W = Fd (maximum work; force and displacement in the same direction)
  • θ = 90° → W = 0 (force perpendicular to motion — no work done)
  • θ = 180° → W = −Fd (negative work; force opposes motion)
Examples from Daily Life
  • Pushing a box across the floor — positive work
  • Carrying a bag horizontally — zero work (force is vertical, motion is horizontal)
  • Brakes stopping a moving car — negative work (friction opposes motion)
  • A planet orbiting the Sun in a circular orbit — zero work by gravity (centripetal force ⊥ velocity)
⚑ Exam Traps
  • A coolie carrying luggage on his head while walking on a level road does zero work — the upward force is perpendicular to displacement
  • Work is a scalar quantity — it has magnitude only, no direction
  • SI unit of work = Joule (J) = 1 N·m. CGS unit = erg. 1 J = 10⁷ erg
📐
Work Done Against Gravity
Lifting objects and potential energy connection
▾

When you lift an object of mass m to height h, you do work against gravity.

Formula
W = mgh
m = mass (kg)  ·  g = 9.8 m/s² (≈ 10 m/s² in exams)  ·  h = height (m)
Important Point

Work done against gravity depends only on the vertical height, not on the path taken. Whether you climb stairs or a ramp to reach the same height, the work done against gravity is identical. This is a key property of conservative forces.

Section 2 — Energy
⚡
Kinetic Energy (KE)
Energy due to motion
▾

Kinetic energy is the energy possessed by a body due to its motion. Any moving body has KE — and to stop it, you must do work against it.

Formula
KE = ½ mv²
m = mass (kg)  ·  v = velocity (m/s)  ·  KE is in Joules (J)
Must-Know Relationships
  • KE ∝ v² — if speed doubles, KE becomes 4 times
  • KE ∝ m — if mass doubles, KE doubles (at same speed)
  • KE is always positive (v² is always ≥ 0)
  • KE and momentum (p) relationship: KE = p² / 2m
Numerical Shortcut

If a body's speed increases by 20%, its KE increases by: (1.2)² − 1 = 1.44 − 1 = 44%. This "percentage increase in KE" type question is common.

⚑ Exam Trap — KE vs Momentum
  • Two bodies with same momentum: lighter body has higher KE
  • Two bodies with same KE: heavier body has higher momentum
  • A body at rest has zero KE but can have potential energy
🏔️
Potential Energy (PE)
Energy due to position or configuration
▾

Potential energy is energy stored in a body due to its position (gravitational PE) or configuration (elastic PE like a compressed spring).

Gravitational PE
PE = mgh
PE is always measured relative to a chosen reference level (usually the ground). It can be negative if the object is below the reference level.
Elastic (Spring) PE
PE = ½ kx²
k = spring constant (N/m)  ·  x = compression or extension from natural length
Types of Potential Energy
  • Gravitational PE — due to height above ground (e.g., water in a dam, a raised hammer)
  • Elastic PE — due to deformation (e.g., stretched rubber band, compressed spring)
  • Chemical PE — stored in chemical bonds (e.g., food, fuel, battery)
  • Electrostatic PE — due to position of charges
  • Nuclear PE — stored in nucleus (released in nuclear reactions)
🔄
Work-Energy Theorem
The fundamental link between work and kinetic energy
▾

The Work-Energy Theorem states that the net work done on a body equals the change in its kinetic energy.

Work-Energy Theorem
W_net = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mu²
v = final velocity  ·  u = initial velocity
This holds true even when multiple forces act simultaneously — only the net work matters.
Think of it this way

Imagine pushing a car from rest. Every joule of net work you do goes directly into increasing the car's kinetic energy. If friction also acts, it does negative work and reduces the net change in KE.

⚑ Common Mistake
  • The theorem uses net work — not work done by individual forces
  • If KE is constant (body moves at constant velocity), net work = zero
Section 3 — Conservation of Energy
⚖️
Law of Conservation of Energy
Energy can neither be created nor destroyed
▾

Energy cannot be created or destroyed — it can only be converted from one form to another. The total mechanical energy (KE + PE) of a system remains constant if only conservative forces act on it.

For a Freely Falling Body

At the top: PE is maximum, KE = 0
At the bottom: KE is maximum, PE = 0
At any intermediate height: PE + KE = constant = mgh (where h = initial height)

Classic Exam Example — Simple Pendulum

At the highest point of its swing → max PE, zero KE. At the lowest point → max KE, zero PE. Total mechanical energy is constant throughout (ignoring air resistance).

Energy Conversions — Frequently Asked
  • Hydroelectric dam → PE of water → KE of turbine → Electrical energy
  • Burning fuel → Chemical PE → Heat + Light energy
  • Solar panel → Light (radiant) energy → Electrical energy
  • Microphone → Sound energy → Electrical energy
  • Electric motor → Electrical energy → Mechanical energy
  • Loudspeaker → Electrical energy → Sound energy
⚑ Conservative vs Non-Conservative Forces
  • Conservative forces (gravity, spring) — work done is path-independent; mechanical energy is conserved
  • Non-conservative forces (friction, air resistance) — work done is path-dependent; they convert mechanical energy to heat
  • In the presence of friction, total mechanical energy decreases (converts to heat). The total energy of the universe is still conserved.
Section 4 — Power
💡
Power — Definition & Formulas
Rate of doing work
▾

Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. Two machines doing the same work differ in power if one does it faster.

Formulas for Power
P = W/t  =  F · v
P = Power (Watts, W)  ·  W = Work (J)  ·  t = time (s)
F·v form: used when force and velocity are given directly (e.g., engine problems)
UnitValueUsed for
Watt (W)1 J/sSI unit of power
Kilowatt (kW)1000 WAppliances, engines
Horsepower (hp)746 WMotors, vehicles
kWh3.6 × 10⁶ JUnit of energy (not power!), used for electricity billing
⚑ Critical Trap — kWh
  • kWh (kilowatt-hour) is a unit of ENERGY, not power. It is the commercial unit of electrical energy.
  • 1 kWh = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
  • 1 horsepower = 746 W (not 750 W — the exact value is tested)
Exam-Style Problem

A car engine exerts a force of 2000 N and moves at 20 m/s. Its power output = F × v = 2000 × 20 = 40,000 W = 40 kW.

Section 5 — Collisions & Practical Concepts
💥
Elastic vs Inelastic Collisions
What is conserved in each type
▾

In all collisions, momentum is always conserved. Whether kinetic energy is also conserved distinguishes elastic from inelastic collisions.

PropertyElastic CollisionPerfectly Inelastic
MomentumConserved ✓Conserved ✓
Kinetic EnergyConserved ✓Not conserved ✗
Objects afterSeparateStick together
e (restitution)e = 1e = 0
ExampleBilliard balls, gas moleculesClay lumps, bullet embedding in block
Coefficient of Restitution (e)

e = (relative velocity of separation) / (relative velocity of approach)
For elastic: e = 1  ·  For perfectly inelastic: e = 0  ·  For real collisions: 0 < e < 1

⚑ Exam Traps
  • In any collision, total energy is always conserved (some converts to heat/sound in inelastic), but kinetic energy may not be
  • A "perfectly elastic" collision is an ideal concept — rarely achieved in practice (gas molecules approximate it)
  • Explosion is the reverse of perfectly inelastic collision — KE increases, momentum is conserved
🏋️
Efficiency & Machines
No machine is 100% efficient
▾

In any real machine, some input energy is always lost to friction and heat. Efficiency tells us how much of the input energy is usefully converted.

Efficiency Formula
η = (Useful Output Energy / Input Energy) × 100%
Or equivalently: η = (Output Power / Input Power) × 100%
η < 100% for all real machines.
Key Points on Efficiency
  • An ideal machine has efficiency = 100% (theoretical only — friction makes this impossible)
  • Efficiency has no unit — it is a pure ratio (or percentage)
  • Lost energy typically appears as heat due to friction
All Formulas
Work, Energy & Power — Master Formula Sheet
  • Work: W = Fd cos θ  ·  Unit: Joule (J)
  • Work against gravity: W = mgh
  • Kinetic Energy: KE = ½mv²  ·  Unit: Joule (J)
  • Gravitational PE: PE = mgh
  • Elastic PE: PE = ½kx²
  • Work-Energy Theorem: W_net = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mu²
  • Conservation (no friction): KE + PE = constant
  • Power: P = W/t = Fv  ·  Unit: Watt (W)
  • Efficiency: η = (Output / Input) × 100%
  • KE–Momentum: KE = p²/2m
Units & Conversions
Units — Frequently Tested
  • Work & Energy: Joule (J) = 1 N·m (SI) · CGS = erg · 1 J = 10⁷ erg
  • Power: Watt (W) = 1 J/s · 1 kW = 1000 W
  • Horsepower: 1 hp = 746 W (exact — don't write 750)
  • kWh: Commercial unit of energy = 3.6 × 10⁶ J (used in electricity billing)
  • Electron-volt (eV): 1 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J (used in atomic physics)
Zero Work — Exam Favourites
When is work done = zero?
  • Force ⊥ displacement (θ = 90°) — e.g., coolie carrying load, centripetal force
  • No displacement — pushing a wall; holding a heavy bag stationary
  • No force — freely sliding on a frictionless surface (after the push)
  • Satellite in circular orbit — gravity is always perpendicular to velocity → zero work by gravity
Quick Comparisons
KE vs Momentum Relationships
  • Same momentum → lighter body has MORE KE
  • Same KE → heavier body has MORE momentum
  • Speed doubles → KE becomes 4×
  • Speed triples → KE becomes 9×
  • KE halved → speed becomes 1/√2 times
Collision Snapshot
  • Elastic: momentum ✓ · KE ✓ · e = 1
  • Inelastic: momentum ✓ · KE ✗ · e = 0
  • Explosion: momentum ✓ · KE increases · e > 1
  • Real collision: 0 < e < 1
Energy Forms — Conversion Table
Device → Energy Conversion
  • Electric bulb: Electrical → Light + Heat
  • Electric motor: Electrical → Mechanical
  • Dynamo/Generator: Mechanical → Electrical
  • Microphone: Sound → Electrical
  • Loudspeaker: Electrical → Sound
  • Solar cell: Light → Electrical
  • Battery: Chemical → Electrical
  • Hydroelectric dam: PE of water → Electrical
  • Candle: Chemical → Heat + Light
  • Seismograph: Mechanical (seismic) → Electrical
Exam Traps — Never Forget
Top Traps for CDS / NDA / AFCAT
  • kWh is a unit of energy, NOT power
  • 1 hp = 746 W exactly — not 750
  • Work is a scalar quantity (no direction)
  • A coolie carrying load horizontally does zero work
  • Gravity does zero work on a satellite in circular orbit
  • In inelastic collision, momentum conserved but KE is not
  • Conservative force = work independent of path (gravity, spring)
  • Path taken doesn't affect work done against gravity (only height matters)
