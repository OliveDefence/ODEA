⚡ CDS 🛡 NDA ✈ AFCAT Physics · P04

Gravitation is among the most reliably tested topics in CDS, NDA, and AFCAT. Questions span Newton's law of gravitation, variation of g, Kepler's laws, satellite motion, escape velocity, and tides. Many questions are conceptual — mastering the key relationships and conditions is more important than rote calculation.

Section 1 — Newton's Law of Gravitation
🍎
Universal Law of Gravitation
Every mass attracts every other mass in the universe
▾

Newton's Universal Law of Gravitation states that every object in the universe attracts every other object with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

Newton's Law of Gravitation
F = G · m₁m₂ / r²
F = gravitational force (N)  ·  G = Universal Gravitational Constant = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²
m₁, m₂ = masses of the two objects (kg)  ·  r = distance between their centres (m)

G is the same everywhere in the universe — hence called "universal."
Properties of Gravitational Force — All Tested
  • Gravitational force is always attractive — never repulsive
  • It is a long-range force — acts across any distance, even through vacuum
  • It acts along the line joining the centres of the two bodies
  • It follows the inverse-square law — double the distance → force becomes ¼
  • It is the weakest of all fundamental forces in nature
  • G was first measured experimentally by Henry Cavendish (1798)
⚑ Exam Traps
  • G (universal gravitational constant) and g (acceleration due to gravity) are completely different. G is constant everywhere; g varies with location.
  • F ∝ 1/r² — if distance is halved, force becomes 4 times (not 2 times)
  • Gravitational force is independent of the medium between the bodies — it acts through vacuum, air, and even solid rock
Section 2 — Acceleration Due to Gravity (g)
🌍
Value and Variation of g
Why g changes with altitude, depth, latitude, and shape of Earth
▾

The acceleration due to gravity (g) is the acceleration experienced by any freely falling body near Earth's surface due to Earth's gravitational pull. On Earth's surface, g ≈ 9.8 m/s² (taken as 10 m/s² in exams).

Formula for g at Earth's Surface
g = GM / R²
G = gravitational constant  ·  M = mass of Earth  ·  R = radius of Earth
Note: g does NOT depend on the mass of the falling object — all objects fall with the same acceleration in vacuum.

Key Values — Memorise
  • g on Earth's surface ≈ 9.8 m/s² (use 10 m/s² in calculations)
  • g at poles > g at equator — the most commonly tested comparison
  • g at centre of Earth = 0
  • g on Moon ≈ 1.67 m/s² ≈ g_Earth / 6
  • g decreases with both increasing altitude AND increasing depth
⚑ Classic Exam Traps — Variation of g
  • g decreases with depth AND altitude — NOT just altitude. At the centre of Earth, g = 0
  • A person weighs MORE at poles than at equator — because g is greater at poles
  • g does NOT depend on mass of the object — a feather and iron ball fall at the same rate in vacuum (Galileo's discovery)
  • Weight (W = mg) changes with location; mass does not change

Weightlessness — What It Actually Means

An astronaut in an orbiting spacecraft is not beyond gravity — gravity is very much present (it provides the centripetal force for the orbit). The astronaut feels weightless because both the astronaut and the spacecraft are in free fall together — the normal reaction force from the floor becomes zero. This state is called apparent weightlessness or microgravity.

Other Situations of Apparent Weightlessness
  • A person in a freely falling lift (lift in free fall) — feels weightless
  • A person at the centre of the Earth — g = 0, true weightlessness
  • A satellite in orbit — apparent weightlessness (free fall around Earth)
⚑ Exam Trap — Astronaut in Space

Astronauts in the ISS (orbiting at ~400 km) are NOT free from gravity. Earth's gravity at that altitude is still about 89% of surface value. They float because they are in continuous free fall around Earth. The orbit IS the free fall — it just keeps missing the Earth.

Section 3 — Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion
🪐
Kepler's Three Laws
The laws that describe how every planet orbits the Sun
▾

Johannes Kepler (early 17th century) formulated three laws describing planetary motion based on Tycho Brahe's observations. These laws apply to all orbiting bodies — planets, moons, and artificial satellites.

Kepler's Three Laws
  • Law of Orbits (First Law) — Every planet moves in an elliptical orbit with the Sun at one of the two foci. (Not at the centre — at a focus.)
  • Law of Areas (Second Law) — The line joining a planet to the Sun sweeps equal areas in equal intervals of time. This means a planet moves faster when closer to the Sun (perihelion) and slower when farther (aphelion).
  • Law of Periods (Third Law) — The square of the orbital period (T²) is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis (r³): T² ∝ r³. Planets farther from the Sun take longer to orbit.
Kepler's Third Law
T² ∝ r³   or   T²/r³ = constant
T = orbital period  ·  r = semi-major axis (average orbital radius)
For two planets: T₁²/T₂² = r₁³/r₂³
A planet twice as far from the Sun takes 2^(3/2) ≈ 2.83 times longer to orbit.
Kepler's Second Law in Action
  • Earth is closest to the Sun (perihelion) in early January — moves fastest
  • Earth is farthest from the Sun (aphelion) in early July — moves slowest
  • This is why seasons are NOT caused by Earth's distance from the Sun (it's the axial tilt)
⚑ Exam Traps — Kepler's Laws
  • Sun is at a focus of the ellipse — NOT at the centre
  • Kepler's 2nd law is equivalent to conservation of angular momentum
  • Kepler's laws apply to satellites too — not just planets
  • T² ∝ r³ — if orbital radius doubles, period increases by factor 2^(3/2) ≈ 2.83, NOT just 2
Section 4 — Satellites & Orbital Motion
🛰️
Orbital Velocity & Geostationary Satellites
How satellites stay in orbit — and the special case of GEO orbit
▾

A satellite orbits Earth because its orbital velocity is exactly right — gravity provides the centripetal force needed to keep it in a circular path. Too slow and it falls; too fast and it escapes.

Orbital Velocity
v₀ = √(GM/r) = √(gR²/r)
r = orbital radius (R + h)  ·  R = Earth's radius  ·  h = height above surface
For a near-Earth orbit (h ≈ 0): v₀ = √(gR) ≈ 7.9 km/s ≈ 8 km/s
Orbital velocity decreases as orbital height increases.
Geostationary Satellite — Key Facts
  • Orbits in the equatorial plane (above the equator)
  • Orbital period = 24 hours (same as Earth's rotation period)
  • Orbital height ≈ 36,000 km above Earth's surface
  • Appears stationary relative to a point on Earth — hence "geostationary"
  • Orbital direction: west to east (same as Earth's rotation)
  • Used for: TV broadcasting, weather forecasting, communication
  • Also called Clarke orbit (after Arthur C. Clarke who proposed it)
Near-Earth Orbit Low

Height: 200–2000 km. Period: ~90 min. Speed: ~7.9 km/s. Used for: ISS, reconnaissance satellites, weather satellites.

Geostationary Orbit GEO

Height: ~36,000 km. Period: exactly 24 hrs. Speed: ~3.1 km/s. Used for: TV, GPS (MEO), communication. Always above equator.

⚑ Exam Traps — Satellites
  • Orbital velocity and orbital period are independent of the satellite's mass
  • A geostationary satellite is only possible above the equator — not above poles or other latitudes
  • Geostationary orbit height ≈ 36,000 km — this exact value is tested
  • In orbit: kinetic energy = ½ × (−total mechanical energy); potential energy is negative
  • When a satellite is given extra velocity (boosted), it moves to a higher orbit with lower speed (paradox — more energy = lower speed in orbit)
Section 5 — Escape Velocity
🚀
Escape Velocity
The minimum speed to break free from a planet's gravity
▾

Escape velocity is the minimum speed needed for an object to escape a planet's gravitational field without further propulsion — i.e., the object will never come back.

Escape Velocity
v_e = √(2GM/R) = √(2gR)
For Earth: v_e ≈ 11.2 km/s (approximately 11 km/s)
For Moon: v_e ≈ 2.4 km/s (that's why Moon has no atmosphere — gas molecules can escape)

Relationship: v_e = √2 × v₀ (escape velocity = √2 times orbital velocity for the same body)
Escape Velocity — Key Points & Comparisons
  • Earth: 11.2 km/s (≈ 11 km/s) — standard exam value
  • Moon: ≈ 2.4 km/s — low escape velocity → no atmosphere retained
  • Jupiter: much higher — massive gravity, retains all gases including hydrogen
  • Escape velocity does NOT depend on the mass or direction of the projected object
  • Black holes: escape velocity exceeds speed of light — that's why even light cannot escape
Why Moon has no atmosphere

The Moon's escape velocity is only 2.4 km/s. Gas molecules in an atmosphere move at speeds comparable to this (especially light gases like hydrogen and helium). Over billions of years, most gas molecules have escaped the Moon's gravity. This is why the Moon is an airless, silent world.

⚑ Exam Traps — Escape Velocity
  • v_e = √2 × v₀ — escape velocity is √2 (≈1.414) times the orbital velocity, NOT 2 times
  • Escape velocity does not depend on the direction of projection — same value whether thrown up, sideways, or at an angle
  • Escape velocity depends on the mass and radius of the planet — NOT on the mass of the escaping object
Section 6 — Tides, Gravitational Potential & More
🌊
Tides
Why the Moon causes tides — and why there are two tides a day
▾

Tides are the periodic rise and fall of sea levels caused primarily by the gravitational pull of the Moon (and to a lesser extent, the Sun) on Earth's oceans.

Why Two High Tides Per Day?

There is a high tide on the side of Earth facing the Moon (direct pull) AND another high tide on the opposite side (the water there is pulled less than the solid Earth — it bulges outward due to the differential gravitational effect). As Earth rotates, any coastal point passes through both bulges in ~24 hours, giving two high tides and two low tides per day.

Spring Tides vs Neap Tides
  • Spring tides — Sun, Earth, and Moon are aligned (Full Moon or New Moon). Gravitational effects of Sun and Moon add together → very high high tides and very low low tides (greatest tidal range)
  • Neap tides — Sun, Earth, and Moon form a right angle (Quarter Moon phases). Sun and Moon partially cancel each other's effect → moderate tidal range (smaller than usual)
Moon vs Sun — Tidal Influence
  • Moon is the primary cause of tides despite being far smaller than the Sun
  • Tidal force depends on the gradient of gravity (difference across Earth's diameter) — the Moon is much closer, so its tidal effect is about 2.2 times that of the Sun
  • The Sun's tidal force is significant but smaller than the Moon's
⚑ Exam Traps — Tides
  • Spring tides occur during New Moon AND Full Moon — not just full moon
  • Neap tides occur during the first and third quarter moon phases
  • Moon is the primary driver of tides despite the Sun being far more massive — because tidal force ∝ 1/r³ and the Moon is much closer
⚡
Gravitational Potential Energy & Potential
Energy stored in the gravitational field
▾

Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE) is the energy possessed by a body due to its position in a gravitational field. For a body near Earth's surface it is mgh; in general (at distance r from Earth's centre) it is more precisely expressed using the full formula.

Gravitational PE & Potential
U = −GMm/r   ·   V = −GM/r
U = gravitational PE (always negative — bound state)
V = gravitational potential (PE per unit mass) — also always negative
Near Earth's surface: U ≈ mgh (taking surface as reference where U = 0)
Negative sign: reference is at infinity where U = 0; coming closer to Earth decreases U (makes it more negative).
Energy of a Satellite in Orbit
  • KE = GMm / 2r (positive)
  • PE = −GMm / r (negative, twice the magnitude of KE)
  • Total mechanical energy = −GMm / 2r (negative — bound system)
  • To move a satellite to a higher orbit: supply energy (total energy increases, becomes less negative)

Solar System — Key Facts for Exams
Planets, their properties, and what gets tested
▾
Mercury Closest to Sun

Smallest planet. No atmosphere (low escape velocity). Extreme temperature swings. Fastest orbital speed.

Venus Hottest

Hottest planet (not Mercury) due to strong greenhouse effect. Rotates retrograde (east to west). No moon.

Mars Red Planet

Two moons: Phobos & Deimos. Olympus Mons — tallest volcano. Thin CO₂ atmosphere.

Jupiter Largest

Largest planet. Great Red Spot = giant storm. 95 known moons including Ganymede (largest moon in Solar System).

Saturn Ringed

Most prominent ring system. Least dense planet (would float on water). 146 moons; Titan has a thick atmosphere.

Neptune Farthest

Farthest planet (since Pluto is a dwarf planet). Strongest winds in Solar System. Has a moon Triton with retrograde orbit.

Solar System Quick Facts — Frequently Asked
  • Largest planet: Jupiter  ·  Smallest planet: Mercury
  • Hottest planet: Venus (greenhouse effect) — NOT Mercury
  • Closest to Sun: Mercury  ·  Farthest: Neptune
  • Planet with most moons: Saturn (146) — recently surpassed Jupiter
  • Planet with no moon: Mercury and Venus
  • Pluto: Reclassified as a dwarf planet by IAU in 2006
  • Brightest planet in night sky: Venus
  • Red planet: Mars (iron oxide / rust on surface)
  • Blue planet: Earth (water + atmosphere)
⚑ Most Commonly Confused Facts
  • Hottest planet = Venus, NOT Mercury — the Sun's closest neighbour has a thin atmosphere; Venus has a thick CO₂ blanket that traps heat
  • Pluto is a dwarf planet — there are now only 8 planets in the Solar System
  • Largest moon in Solar System = Ganymede (Jupiter's moon), NOT Earth's Moon
