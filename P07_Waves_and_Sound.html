Section 1 — Nature & Types of Waves
〰️
Wave Basics — What is a Wave?
Propagation of energy without propagation of matter
▾

A wave is a disturbance that travels through a medium (or vacuum), transferring energy from one point to another without any net transfer of matter. The particles of the medium oscillate about their mean positions but do not travel with the wave.

Fundamental Wave Equation & Key Terms
v = fλ   ·   T = 1/f
v = speed of wave (m/s)  ·  f = frequency (Hz)  ·  λ = wavelength (m)
T = time period (s) = time for one complete oscillation = 1/f

Amplitude (A) — maximum displacement from mean position; determines intensity/loudness
Frequency (f) — number of complete oscillations per second (Hz); determines pitch
Wavelength (λ) — distance between two consecutive crests (or troughs) in a wave
PropertyTransverse WaveLongitudinal Wave
Particle oscillationPerpendicular to wave directionParallel to wave direction
FeaturesCrests and troughsCompressions and rarefactions
Medium needed?Solid or surface of liquid (not gases)Solid, liquid, or gas
ExamplesLight waves, water surface waves, string wavesSound waves, seismic P-waves, spring waves
Key Wave Properties — Tested Directly
  • Sound is a longitudinal, mechanical wave — needs a medium; cannot travel in vacuum
  • Light is a transverse, electromagnetic wave — does NOT need a medium; travels in vacuum
  • Frequency does not change when a wave passes from one medium to another — wavelength and speed change, not frequency
  • Speed of wave depends on the medium, not on the frequency or amplitude
  • A wave carries energy, not matter — the medium particles only oscillate in place
⚑ Exam Trap — Transverse vs Longitudinal
  • Sound is longitudinal — NOT transverse. This is directly asked.
  • Light is transverse — this is why it can be polarised (longitudinal waves cannot be polarised)
  • When a wave crosses from one medium to another, frequency stays constant; speed and wavelength change
Section 2 — Sound Waves
🔊
Nature of Sound & Speed in Different Media
Why sound travels faster in solids than in air
▾

Sound is a mechanical, longitudinal wave produced by the vibration of a body. It needs a material medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to travel — it cannot propagate through vacuum.

Speed of Sound — Key Values for Exams
  • Speed of sound in air at 0°C ≈ 332 m/s  ·  at 25°C ≈ 346 m/s
  • Speed in water ≈ 1500 m/s (about 4–5× faster than in air)
  • Speed in steel/iron ≈ 5000–5100 m/s (about 15× faster than in air)
  • Order: Solids > Liquids > Gases — sound travels fastest in solids
  • Speed of sound in air increases with temperature: ~0.61 m/s per °C rise
  • Speed of light = 3 × 10⁸ m/s — about 900,000× faster than sound in air
Why Speed Varies Across Media

Sound speed depends on the elasticity (restoring force) and density of the medium. v = √(E/ρ). Solids have much higher elasticity (and despite higher density, the elasticity dominates) → fastest speed. In gases, both elasticity and density are low but elasticity wins out for order-of-magnitude differences.

Factors Affecting Speed of Sound in Air
  • Temperature ↑ → speed ↑ (speed ∝ √T, T in Kelvin). Hot air: molecules move faster, transmit disturbance more quickly.
  • Humidity ↑ → speed ↑ (moist air is less dense than dry air — water vapour is lighter than N₂/O₂ — so sound travels slightly faster in humid air)
  • Pressure — at constant temperature, changing pressure does NOT change speed of sound in an ideal gas (density and elasticity change proportionally)
  • Wind — increases speed downwind, decreases speed upwind (apparent speed only)
  • Frequency and amplitude do NOT affect the speed of sound
⚑ Classic Exam Traps — Speed of Sound
  • Sound travels faster in humid air than dry air — many students think humid air would slow sound because it "feels heavier." In fact, water vapour (mol. mass 18) is lighter than N₂ (28) and O₂ (32), so humid air is less dense.
  • Changing air pressure alone (at constant temperature) does NOT change speed of sound
  • Sound does NOT travel in vacuum — demonstrated by bell-in-vacuum-jar experiment
  • Speed of sound in air ≈ 332 m/s at 0°C — this exact value is tested

Characteristics of Sound — Pitch, Loudness & Quality
What makes sounds high, loud, or musical
▾
CharacteristicDetermined byUnitDescription
PitchFrequencyHzHigh frequency → high pitch (shrill sound like a whistle). Low frequency → low pitch (like a drum). Pitch is the sensation of frequency.
LoudnessAmplitude (intensity)Decibel (dB)Greater amplitude → more energy → louder sound. Loudness is the sensation of intensity.
Quality / TimbreWaveform (harmonics present)—Why a piano and violin playing the same note sound different — overtones/harmonics differ.
Sound Intensity — Decibel Scale (Key Values)
  • Threshold of hearing = 0 dB (faintest audible sound)
  • Whisper ≈ 30 dB  ·  Normal conversation ≈ 60 dB
  • Busy street / traffic ≈ 80 dB  ·  Rock concert / jet engine ≈ 120–130 dB
  • Threshold of pain ≈ 120–130 dB
  • Every 10 dB increase = 10× increase in intensity (logarithmic scale)
  • Prolonged exposure above 85 dB causes hearing damage
Intensity & Decibel
β (dB) = 10 log₁₀ (I / I₀)
β = sound level in decibels  ·  I = intensity of sound (W/m²)  ·  I₀ = reference intensity = 10⁻¹² W/m² (threshold of hearing)
Intensity I ∝ A² (proportional to square of amplitude)  ·  I ∝ 1/r² (inverse square law from a point source)
Pitch vs Loudness — Quick Memory Aid

Think of tuning a guitar: turning the peg tighter increases the frequency → higher pitch (string vibrates faster). Plucking harder increases the amplitude → louder sound (string swings farther). The pitch and loudness are completely independent — you can have a high-pitched soft sound or a low-pitched loud one.

Section 3 — Reflection, Echo & Resonance
🏔️
Echo, Reverberation & SONAR
How reflected sound is used in nature and technology
▾

When sound strikes a hard surface and bounces back, it is called reflection of sound. The reflected sound heard distinctly after the original sound has stopped is an echo.

Conditions for Echo

For an echo to be heard distinctly, the reflecting surface must be at least 17 metres away from the observer (in air at normal temperature). This is because the human ear can distinguish two sounds only if they are separated by at least 0.1 second, and at ~340 m/s, sound travels 34 m in 0.1 s — so the surface must be at least 34/2 = 17 m away (sound must travel there and back).

Echo Distance & SONAR Formula
d = v × t / 2
d = distance to the reflecting surface  ·  v = speed of sound  ·  t = total time for echo to return
Divide by 2 because sound travels to the surface AND back.

SONAR (Sound Navigation And Ranging) uses this principle underwater to measure ocean depth, detect submarines, and locate fish.
Echo vs Reverberation — Key Distinction
  • Echo — reflected sound heard as a distinct, separate repetition of the original sound; reflecting surface is far away (≥17 m)
  • Reverberation — when reflected sound reaches the ear within 0.1 s of the original, the two sounds merge and the original sound appears to persist and "linger." Occurs in closed halls, rooms, and auditoriums.
  • In auditoriums: some reverberation is desirable for musical richness; too much makes speech unclear. Controlled by soft furnishings, curtains, and acoustic tiles (absorb sound).
Applications of Sound Reflection
  • SONAR — depth sounding, submarine detection, fish location, underwater mapping
  • Echolocation in bats — bats emit ultrasonic pulses and use the echo to navigate and hunt insects in complete darkness
  • Ultrasound scanning (medical) — sound echoes from internal organs to create images (sonography)
  • Megaphone / Loudspeaker horn — shaped to direct sound reflection forward
  • Stethoscope — uses multiple reflection of sound to amplify heartbeat/lung sounds
⚑ Echo Trap — Minimum Distance
  • Minimum distance for echo = 17 m (commonly tested as 17 m, sometimes stated as the wall must be at least 17 m away)
  • SONAR distance formula: always divide total time by 2 (time for one-way trip = t/2)
  • Reverberation is NOT the same as echo — reverberation is prolongation, echo is a distinct repetition

Resonance & Musical Instruments
Why a glass shatters at the right frequency — and how music works
▾

Resonance occurs when an object is made to vibrate at its natural frequency by an external vibration of the same frequency, resulting in maximum amplitude of vibration and maximum energy transfer.

Resonance — Definition and Conditions

Every object has a natural (fundamental) frequency at which it vibrates most readily. When a driving force vibrates at this same frequency, the object absorbs energy most efficiently — resonance occurs. The amplitude builds up dramatically. This can be constructive (musical instruments, radio tuning) or destructive (Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse).

String Instruments Strings

Violin, guitar, sitar. Pitch ∝ 1/length. Shorter string → higher frequency. Also depends on tension and mass per unit length.

Wind Instruments Air Column

Flute, harmonium, organ pipe. Sound produced by vibrating air column. Open pipe vs closed pipe give different harmonics.

Percussion Membrane

Drum, tabla, mridangam. Vibrating membrane produces sound. Tightening membrane → higher pitch (higher frequency).

Radio Tuning Electronic

Tuning a radio to a station = adjusting the circuit's resonant frequency to match the broadcast frequency. Resonance maximises signal reception.

Real-World Examples of Resonance
  • Shattering a glass with voice — an opera singer can shatter a wine glass by singing at its natural frequency; amplitude builds up until glass breaks
  • Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940) — wind caused oscillations at the bridge's natural frequency → resonance → bridge collapsed dramatically
  • Soldiers break step on bridges — to avoid resonance between marching frequency and the bridge's natural frequency
  • Microwave oven — microwaves at 2.45 GHz match the resonant frequency of water molecules; water absorbs energy → food heats
  • MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging) — uses nuclear magnetic resonance of hydrogen nuclei
⚑ Resonance Exam Trap

Soldiers are ordered to break step (march out of rhythm) when crossing a bridge because if their marching frequency matches the bridge's natural frequency, resonance could build up large oscillations and damage the bridge — exactly like the Tacoma Narrows collapse.

Section 4 — Doppler Effect
🚑
Doppler Effect
Why an ambulance siren changes pitch as it passes you
▾

The Doppler Effect is the apparent change in the frequency (and therefore pitch) of a wave when the source and observer are in relative motion towards or away from each other.

Doppler Effect — Key Rules

Source approaching observer: Sound waves are compressed → apparent frequency increases → higher pitch heard

Source moving away from observer: Sound waves are stretched → apparent frequency decreases → lower pitch heard

The actual frequency emitted by the source does NOT change — only the apparent frequency heard by the observer changes.

Doppler Formula (Sound)
f' = f × (v ± v_o) / (v ∓ v_s)
f' = apparent frequency  ·  f = actual frequency  ·  v = speed of sound
v_o = speed of observer  ·  v_s = speed of source
+v_o when observer moves toward source  ·  −v_o when moving away
−v_s when source moves toward observer  ·  +v_s when source moves away
Applications of Doppler Effect
  • Ambulance / police siren — siren pitch rises as it approaches, drops as it passes and recedes
  • Radar speed guns (traffic police) — radar waves reflected from a moving car undergo Doppler shift; frequency change gives speed
  • Doppler radar — used in weather forecasting; detects movement of rain and wind in storms
  • Astronomical redshift / blueshift — galaxies moving away from Earth show redshift (lower frequency); approaching galaxies show blueshift. Basis for expanding universe theory.
  • Medical Doppler ultrasound — measures blood flow speed using Doppler shift of ultrasound echoes from moving red blood cells
  • RADAR in aircraft — Doppler navigation systems measure aircraft speed relative to the ground
⚑ Doppler Effect Exam Traps
  • Doppler effect changes apparent frequency — the actual frequency emitted by the source is unchanged
  • When source approaches: apparent frequency increases (higher pitch) — NOT lower
  • Doppler effect applies to ALL waves — sound, light, radio waves, not just sound
  • Redshift of stars/galaxies = Doppler effect of light; used to prove universe is expanding
  • When both source and observer move at the same speed in the same direction → no Doppler effect (no relative motion)

Applications of Ultrasound — All Exam-Tested
  • Medical ultrasonography (sonography) — imaging internal organs, foetus during pregnancy, detecting tumours
  • Echocardiography — imaging the heart using ultrasound
  • SONAR — detecting submarines, measuring ocean depth, locating fish shoals, underwater mapping
  • Ultrasonic cleaning — delicate instruments (spectacle lenses, jewellery, circuit boards) cleaned using high-frequency vibrations in liquid
  • Non-destructive testing (NDT) — detecting cracks and flaws inside metal components without damaging them

  • Dolphins — use ultrasound for echolocation and communication under water
  • Kidney stone treatment (lithotripsy) — focused ultrasound pulses break up kidney stones non-invasively
Infrasound in Nature
  • Elephants communicate using infrasound (below 20 Hz) over distances of several kilometres
  • Before earthquakes and tsunamis, animals often flee — they may detect infrasonic waves that precede the main seismic shock
  • Whales communicate using very low-frequency sounds that travel thousands of kilometres through oceans
⚑ Frequency Range Traps
  • Human hearing range: 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz — both limits are tested
  • Ultrasound is >20,000 Hz (20 kHz) — NOT just "high frequency sound." The cutoff value matters.
  • Bats use ultrasound, not infrasound, for echolocation
  • Elephants use infrasound (<20 Hz) for long-distance communication
  • Dogs can hear up to about 40,000–65,000 Hz (ultrasonic range) — that's why dog whistles work at frequencies humans can't hear
Section 6 — Superposition, Beats & Standing Waves
🎸
Superposition, Beats & Standing Waves
When two waves meet — interference, beats, and nodes
▾

When two waves meet at a point, the resulting displacement is the algebraic sum of their individual displacements — this is the Principle of Superposition.

Beats

When two sound waves of slightly different frequencies (f₁ and f₂) travel in the same direction, they alternately reinforce and cancel each other. The listener hears a periodic variation in loudness — called beats. Beat frequency = |f₁ − f₂|. Musicians use beats to tune instruments: when two notes are in tune (same frequency), beats disappear.

Beats & Standing Waves
Beat frequency = |f₁ − f₂|
A standing (stationary) wave is formed when two identical waves travel in opposite directions and superpose.
Nodes — points of zero displacement (destructive interference always)
Antinodes — points of maximum displacement (constructive interference always)
Distance between adjacent nodes = λ/2  ·  Distance between adjacent antinode and node = λ/4
Constructive Interference Reinforce

Waves in phase: crest meets crest, trough meets trough. Resultant amplitude = sum of amplitudes → louder sound / brighter light.

Destructive Interference Cancel

Waves out of phase: crest meets trough. Resultant amplitude = difference of amplitudes. Can produce silence (noise-cancelling headphones).

Practical Applications
  • Noise-cancelling headphones — microphone picks up ambient noise, electronics generate a wave with the exact opposite phase → destructive interference → silence
  • Tuning musical instruments — a musician listens for beats; when beats fade away, the two strings are perfectly in tune
  • Guitar/sitar strings — standing waves form on plucked strings; the pattern of nodes and antinodes determines the frequency (note) produced
  • Organ pipes — standing waves in air columns; open and closed ends determine harmonics
Section 7 — Mach Number, Sonic Boom & Musical Notes
✈️
Mach Number, Sonic Boom & Musical Scale
Supersonic flight, shock waves, and the science of music
▾
Mach Number
Mach number = Speed of object / Speed of sound
Mach 1 = exactly the speed of sound in air ≈ 332–346 m/s ≈ 1200 km/h
Subsonic: Mach < 1  ·  Transonic: Mach ≈ 1  ·  Supersonic: Mach > 1  ·  Hypersonic: Mach ≥ 5
Named after Austrian physicist Ernst Mach
Sonic Boom
  • When an aircraft exceeds the speed of sound (Mach > 1), it outruns the sound waves it produces
  • Sound waves pile up into a shock wave (a cone-shaped pressure wave)
  • When this shock wave sweeps past an observer on the ground, they hear a sudden loud boom — the sonic boom
  • A sonic boom is NOT a one-time event when the aircraft "breaks the sound barrier" — it is continuously produced as long as the aircraft is supersonic
  • Glass can shatter and buildings can be damaged by intense sonic booms
Musical Notes & Sound — CDS/NDA Facts
  • Musical notes in Indian classical music: Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni Sa (7 svaras)
  • Western scale: Do Re Mi Fa Sol La Ti Do (7 notes)
  • Octave — the note that has exactly double the frequency of the base note (e.g., upper Sa has twice the frequency of lower Sa)
  • Noise = irregular, aperiodic vibrations  ·  Music = regular, periodic vibrations with a pleasing quality
  • Reverberation time — time for sound level to drop by 60 dB after source stops; ideal for concert halls ≈ 1–2 seconds
  • Decibel (dB) — named after Alexander Graham Bell
⚑ Sonic Boom Trap

Many people think the sonic boom happens only once — when the aircraft "breaks the sound barrier." This is wrong. The sonic boom is a continuous phenomenon that occurs throughout supersonic flight. Anyone the shock wave cone sweeps over hears the boom.
