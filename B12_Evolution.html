Evolution — Origin & Diversity of Life
Origin of life, theories of evolution, Darwin's natural selection, evidence for evolution, human evolution, Hardy-Weinberg law and speciation — at CDS, NDA & AFCAT exam depth.
CDS NDA AFCAT Darwin Natural Selection Origin of Life

Evolution is the change in the inherited characteristics of biological populations over successive generations. It explains the unity and diversity of life — why all organisms share fundamental biochemistry (common descent) yet are spectacularly diverse in form and function. The theory of evolution by natural selection, proposed by Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace (1858), is the cornerstone of modern biology.

Theories on Origin of Life — Historical Progression
  • Spontaneous Generation (Abiogenesis) — belief that life arises spontaneously from non-living matter (e.g., maggots from meat). Disproved by Francesco Redi (1668 — flies on covered meat) and conclusively by Louis Pasteur (1861 — swan-neck flask experiment).
  • Biogenesis — life arises only from pre-existing life (Pasteur). But this doesn't explain the very FIRST life.
  • Panspermia — life arrived on Earth from outer space (spores/microbes on meteorites). Proposed by Arrhenius. Does not explain origin of life — just shifts the question elsewhere.
  • Oparin-Haldane Hypothesis (Chemical Evolution / Abiogenesis) — A.I. Oparin (1924) and J.B.S. Haldane (1929) proposed that life originated from simple organic molecules in the early Earth's primitive ocean ("primordial soup") — rich in H₂, CH₄, NH₃, H₂O, NO atmosphere of free O₂. UV radiation and lightning provided energy for chemical reactions. Complex organic molecules formed → coacervates (protein clusters) → proto-cells → first living cells.
  • Miller-Urey Experiment (1953) — Stanley Miller and Harold Urey experimentally tested Oparin-Haldane hypothesis. They simulated early Earth conditions (H₂, CH₄, NH₃, H₂O vapour; electric sparks for lightning) and produced amino acids and other organic molecules abiogenically — providing direct experimental support for chemical evolution.
First Life — Key Milestones

The earliest life forms were chemoheterotrophs — anaerobic prokaryotes that obtained energy by breaking down organic molecules from the primordial soup. Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) were among the first photosynthetic organisms (~3.5 billion years ago), gradually oxygenating Earth's atmosphere — enabling aerobic life. The development of the ozone layer from atmospheric O₂ shielded land from UV radiation, enabling colonisation of land. First eukaryotes ~1.5–2 billion years ago (endosymbiont theory — Lynn Margulis: mitochondria and chloroplasts originated as ingested prokaryotes).

⚑ Miller-Urey Experiment — What It Did and Did NOT Prove
  • It proved that amino acids and organic molecules CAN be produced from inorganic precursors under simulated early Earth conditions — supporting the idea that life could have originated chemically.
  • It did NOT produce a living cell — just the building blocks. The step from organic molecules to a self-replicating, membrane-bound cell remains an active area of research.
  • The exact composition of early Earth's atmosphere is debated — modern estimates suggest it may have had more CO₂ and less CH₄ than Miller used.

Lamarck's Giraffe Example — Classic but Wrong

Lamarck explained long giraffe necks: ancestral giraffes stretched their necks to reach higher leaves → necks became slightly longer → this acquired longer neck was passed to offspring → over generations, necks became longer. Why this is wrong: Acquired characteristics (changes due to use during lifetime) are NOT encoded in DNA and cannot be inherited. A bodybuilder's muscles are not passed to their children genetically. DNA is not altered by somatic effort. However, Lamarck was correct that organisms change over time — he just got the mechanism wrong.

Section 3 — Darwin's Theory of Natural Selection
🦜
Charles Darwin & Natural Selection
The Beagle voyage, Galapagos finches, "On the Origin of Species" (1859)
▾

Charles Darwin (1809–1882) developed his theory of evolution by natural selection after a 5-year voyage aboard HMS Beagle (1831–1836), during which he observed remarkable biological diversity — especially in the Galapagos Islands. Alfred Russel Wallace independently reached the same conclusion — both presented their ideas jointly to the Linnean Society in 1858. Darwin published On the Origin of Species in 1859.

1
Overproduction (Superfecundity)
All organisms produce more offspring than can survive. A single bacterium dividing every 20 min could produce a mass equal to Earth in hours. A single oyster produces millions of eggs per year.
2
Variation
Individuals in a population show heritable variation — differences in traits that can be passed to offspring. No two individuals (except identical twins) are exactly alike.
3
Struggle for Existence
Since more offspring are produced than can survive, there is competition for limited resources (food, space, mates) — intraspecific (within species) and interspecific (between species).
4
Survival of the Fittest (Natural Selection)
Individuals with advantageous heritable variations survive and reproduce more successfully. "Fittest" means best adapted to the environment — not necessarily strongest. Coined by Herbert Spencer (not Darwin).
5
Inheritance & Gradual Change
Favourable traits are passed to offspring. Over many generations, the frequency of advantageous traits increases in the population → gradual evolution of new species. Darwin did NOT know the mechanism of inheritance (Mendel's work was unknown to him).
Darwin's Observations — Galapagos & Beyond
  • Darwin's Finches — 13–14 species of finches on Galapagos; all descended from a common South American ancestor; each island had different food sources → different beak shapes evolved (seed crackers, insect pickers, cactus feeders) — classic adaptive radiation
  • Fossils — extinct species resembled living species in the same region → species change over time
  • Homologous structures — whale flipper, bat wing, horse leg, human arm all have same bone pattern (humerus-radius-ulna-carpals) despite different functions → common ancestor
  • Geographic distribution — similar environments in different continents had different but ecologically equivalent species → independent evolution, not migration
  • Artificial selection — humans had already produced dramatic changes in domesticated animals and crops by selective breeding → natural analogue could drive evolution over geological time
⚑ Common Misconceptions About Natural Selection
  • "Survival of the fittest" means best adapted, NOT physically strongest. A tiny bacterium that is antibiotic-resistant is "fitter" than a large susceptible one in an antibiotic environment.
  • Evolution has no goal or direction — it is not "progress toward perfection." Adaptations are relative to the current environment.
  • Natural selection acts on individuals but evolution occurs in populations (change in allele frequencies over generations).
  • Darwin did NOT know about DNA or genes — he knew variation existed and was heritable, but not the mechanism. The "Modern Synthesis" (Neo-Darwinism) combined Darwin's natural selection with Mendelian genetics and population genetics.
  • "Survival of the fittest" was coined by Herbert Spencer, not Darwin. Darwin used "natural selection."

⚑ Homologous vs Analogous — Most-Tested Distinction
  • Homologous = same structure, different function → evidence of divergent evolution from a common ancestor. Example: human arm vs whale flipper — same bones, very different uses.
  • Analogous = same function, different structure → evidence of convergent evolution — unrelated organisms evolving similar adaptations independently. Example: butterfly wing and bat wing — both used for flying, but completely different internal structures.
  • Memory trick: HOMologous = same HOM(e)/origin. ANAlogous = ANAlogous function (look-alikes in function, strangers in origin).
Archaeopteryx — The Most Famous Transitional Fossil
  • Discovered in 1861 (Jurassic limestone, Bavaria, Germany) — just 2 years after Darwin's "Origin of Species"
  • Shows both reptilian features (teeth, clawed wings, long bony tail, separate vertebrae) and avian features (feathers, wishbone/furcula, bird-like feet) → classic transitional fossil between theropod dinosaurs and birds
  • Named Archaeopteryx lithographica — "ancient wing from lithographic stone"
  • Strong evidence that birds evolved from theropod dinosaurs
Section 5 — Modern Synthetic Theory & Population Genetics
🧬
Modern Synthetic Theory (Neo-Darwinism) & Hardy-Weinberg Law
Combining Darwin with Mendel, population genetics and allele frequencies
▾

The Modern Synthetic Theory of Evolution (Neo-Darwinism) — developed in the 1930s–1940s by Theodosius Dobzhansky, Julian Huxley, Ernst Mayr and others — unified Darwin's natural selection with Mendelian genetics, population genetics, and palaeontology.

Agents of Evolutionary Change (Forces That Change Allele Frequencies)
  • Mutation — random changes in DNA; ultimate source of all new variation; provides raw material for evolution; usually rare and random in direction
  • Natural Selection — differential survival and reproduction of individuals with advantageous traits; directional, stabilising or disruptive
  • Genetic Drift — random changes in allele frequencies due to chance, especially in small populations. Two forms: (a) Bottleneck effect — population sharply reduced (disaster) → survivors may not represent original gene pool → reduced genetic diversity. (b) Founder effect — small group colonises new area → limited gene pool of founders becomes dominant in new population
  • Gene Flow (Migration) — movement of alleles between populations when individuals migrate and interbreed; tends to reduce genetic differences between populations; can introduce new alleles
  • Recombination (Genetic Recombination) — new combinations of alleles produced by crossing over during meiosis and independent assortment; creates variation without changing allele frequencies
  • Reproductive Isolation — prevents gene flow between populations → allows them to diverge → eventual speciation
Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium (1908)

The Hardy-Weinberg Law (G.H. Hardy, mathematician + Wilhelm Weinberg, physician, 1908) states that in a large, randomly mating population, allele frequencies and genotype frequencies remain constant from generation to generation provided there is no mutation, no migration, no natural selection, no genetic drift, and random mating.

Mathematical expression: p² + 2pq + q² = 1 and p + q = 1, where p = frequency of dominant allele (A), q = frequency of recessive allele (a); p² = AA frequency, 2pq = Aa frequency, q² = aa frequency.

H-W equilibrium describes a non-evolving population. Real populations deviate from H-W conditions → evolution occurs. It provides a null model to detect evolutionary change.

Types of Natural Selection — Direction of Change
  • Directional Selection — one extreme phenotype favoured → population mean shifts in one direction. Example: industrial melanism in peppered moth (Biston betularia) — dark moths favoured in polluted forests; antibiotic resistance in bacteria.
  • Stabilising Selection — intermediate phenotypes favoured; extremes selected against → reduces variation, maintains current mean. Example: human birth weight (very low and very high birth weights have higher mortality); shell thickness in snails.
  • Disruptive (Diversifying) Selection — both extremes favoured; intermediate phenotypes selected against → population splits into two distinct groups. Example: beak size in black-bellied seedcracker bird (small beaks for soft seeds, large for hard seeds — intermediates fail).
Industrial Melanism — Peppered Moth (Biston betularia)
  • Pre-industrial Britain: mostly light-coloured moths (camouflaged on lichen-covered tree bark); rare dark (melanic) moths visible to predators → selected against
  • Industrial Revolution: soot killed lichens, darkened bark → dark moths now camouflaged; light moths now visible → natural selection reversed
  • By 1900s: dark moths dominated in industrial areas (~98% in Manchester)
  • Post-Clean Air Acts: lichens returned, light moths increasing again — real-time evolution observed
  • Studied by Bernard Kettlewell (1950s); classic textbook example of directional natural selection
Section 6 — Speciation & Adaptive Radiation
🌿
Speciation — How New Species Arise
Allopatric, sympatric, reproductive isolation and adaptive radiation
▾

A species is a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring. Speciation is the process by which new species arise from existing ones — the central process of macroevolution.

TypeMechanismExample
Allopatric Speciation (most common)Geographic isolation separates populations → no gene flow → populations evolve independently → reproductive isolation → new species. A physical barrier (mountain range, river, ocean) prevents interbreeding.Darwin's finches on different Galapagos islands; squirrels separated by Grand Canyon (Kaibab vs Abert's squirrel)
Sympatric SpeciationNew species arise within the same geographic area — without physical separation. Often via polyploidy (plants), habitat differentiation, or assortative mating.Apple maggot fly (Rhagoletis pomonella) — originally on hawthorn, some switched to introduced apple trees → now reproductively isolated; polyploidy in wheat and cotton
Parapatric SpeciationPopulations in adjacent areas with partial overlap diverge; gene flow reduced but not eliminated; strong selection across a boundary zone.Anthoxanthum grass on/off mine tailings (heavy metal tolerance boundary)
Reproductive Isolation — Types
  • Pre-zygotic barriers (prevent mating or fertilisation): Habitat isolation, temporal isolation (different breeding seasons), behavioural isolation (different courtship displays), mechanical isolation (incompatible genitalia/flowers), gametic isolation (sperm cannot fertilise egg)
  • Post-zygotic barriers (hybrid is formed but fails): Hybrid inviability (hybrid embryo dies early), hybrid sterility (hybrid survives but is infertile — e.g., mule = horse × donkey is sterile), hybrid breakdown (F₂ hybrids weak/infertile)
Adaptive Radiation — Rapid Diversification

Adaptive radiation is the rapid evolution of multiple species from a single ancestral species to fill different ecological niches — typically after colonising a new environment or after a mass extinction creates vacant niches.

  • Darwin's finches — one ancestral finch → 13–14 species with different beak shapes for different food sources (Galapagos Islands)
  • Australian marsupials — one ancestral marsupial → ~250 species: kangaroos (grazers), koalas (leaf eaters), Tasmanian devil (carnivore), wombats (burrowers), sugar gliders (aerial)
  • Hawaiian honeycreepers — one ancestral finch → 50+ species with diverse bills
  • Mammalian radiation after dinosaur extinction (~66 million years ago) — mammals rapidly diversified into ecological niches left vacant by dinosaurs
  • Convergent evolution is the opposite — unrelated species independently evolve similar adaptations to similar environments (e.g., cacti and euphorbs; dolphin and ichthyosaur body shape)

Human Evolution — Hominid Timeline
From Australopithecus to Homo sapiens — African origin of humans
▾

Humans belong to the family Hominidae (great apes). Our closest living relatives are chimpanzees (~98.5% DNA identity). Modern humans and chimpanzees shared a common ancestor ~6–7 million years ago. All human evolution occurred in Africa — the "Out of Africa" hypothesis.

~7–6 MYA
Sahelanthropus tchadensis / Early Hominids
Oldest known hominid fossils. Bipedal (walked upright) but small-brained. Discovered in Chad (Africa).
~4–2 MYA
Australopithecus
Genus of early hominids; fully bipedal; ape-like brain (~450 cc); lived in Africa. Famous specimen: "Lucy" (A. afarensis, discovered 1974 in Ethiopia by Donald Johanson). Also: A. africanus — "Taung Child" (discovered by Raymond Dart, 1924, South Africa).
~2.5–1.5 MYA
Homo habilis — "Handy Man"
First member of genus Homo. Brain ~650–800 cc. First to use stone tools (Oldowan stone tool industry). Lived in East Africa. Discovered by Louis and Mary Leakey in Tanzania (Olduvai Gorge), 1960.
~1.9–0.1 MYA
Homo erectus — "Upright Man"
Brain ~900 cc. First hominid to leave Africa and spread to Asia and Europe. Used Acheulean hand axes (more sophisticated tools). First to use fire. Specimens: Java Man (Indonesia), Peking Man (China). Larger, more modern body proportions.
~0.6–0.03 MYA
Homo neanderthalensis — Neanderthals
Brain ~1400–1600 cc (larger than modern humans!). Lived in Europe and western Asia. Buried their dead, made tools (Mousterian industry), cared for injured, wore clothes. NOT direct ancestors of modern humans — a separate lineage. Modern humans and Neanderthals interbred (~2–4% Neanderthal DNA in non-African modern humans). Went extinct ~30,000 years ago, possibly due to competition with H. sapiens.
~0.3 MYA–present
Homo sapiens — Modern Humans
Brain ~1350 cc. Originated in Africa (~300,000 years ago). Migrated out of Africa ~60,000–70,000 years ago. Cave paintings (Lascaux, France; Altamira, Spain), sophisticated tools (Aurignacian industry), symbolic thought, language. Anatomically and behaviourally modern. Only surviving hominid species.

⚑ Human Evolution Exam Traps
  • Humans did NOT evolve from chimpanzees — both share a common ancestor ~6–7 million years ago. Chimps and humans are evolutionary cousins, not ancestor-descendant.
  • Homo erectus was the FIRST to leave Africa — NOT H. habilis. H. habilis stayed in Africa.
  • Neanderthals are NOT direct human ancestors — they are a separate species (or subspecies). Modern humans coexisted with and interbred with Neanderthals in Europe.
  • Neanderthal brain was LARGER than modern human brain on average — bigger brain does NOT mean more intelligent in complex ways.
  • "Lucy" (Australopithecus afarensis) was discovered by Donald Johanson in Ethiopia, 1974 — NOT by the Leakeys (who found H. habilis in Tanzania).
  • H. habilis made the first stone tools. H. erectus was the first to use fire.

The Five Major Mass Extinctions ("Big Five")
  • Ordovician-Silurian (~444 MYA) — ~85% species lost; glaciation and sea level fall
  • Late Devonian (~375 MYA) — ~75% species lost; reef collapse; possible asteroid impacts
  • Permian-Triassic (~252 MYA — "The Great Dying") — LARGEST mass extinction; ~96% marine species, ~70% terrestrial vertebrate species lost; volcanic eruptions (Siberian Traps) + possible other causes
  • Triassic-Jurassic (~201 MYA) — ~80% species lost; opened niches for dinosaurs to diversify
  • Cretaceous-Palaeogene (K-Pg, ~66 MYA) — ~76% species lost; non-avian dinosaurs extinct; Chicxulub asteroid impact (Mexico) + Deccan Trap volcanism; mammals and birds survived and diversified
