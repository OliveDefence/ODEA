Section 1 — Magnets & Magnetic Properties
🧲
Natural & Artificial Magnets
Properties, poles, and the laws of magnetic attraction
▾

A magnet is a material that attracts iron and certain other materials (nickel, cobalt) and aligns itself in the north–south direction when freely suspended. The two ends of a magnet are called its poles — the north-seeking pole (N) and the south-seeking pole (S).

Fundamental Properties of Magnets — All Exam-Tested
  • Like poles repel; unlike poles attract — a north pole repels another north pole; north attracts south
  • Magnetic poles always exist in pairs — an isolated magnetic monopole has never been found; cutting a magnet in half produces two complete magnets, each with its own N and S pole
  • Strongest attraction at poles — the centre of a bar magnet (the neutral point) shows the least magnetic effect
  • Natural magnet: Lodestone (Magnetite, Fe₃O₄) — naturally occurring magnetic mineral; historically used in compasses
  • Magnetic substances (ferromagnetic) — strongly attracted to magnets: iron (Fe), nickel (Ni), cobalt (Co)
  • Non-magnetic substances — wood, copper, aluminium, glass, plastic; NOT attracted to magnets
TypeNatural MagnetArtificial Magnet
OriginFound in nature (lodestone/magnetite)Made by magnetising iron, steel, or alloys
ShapeIrregularRegular — bar, horseshoe (U-shape), needle
StrengthWeakCan be made very strong
PermanencePermanentPermanent (steel) or temporary (soft iron)
UseEarly compasses, historicalElectric motors, generators, loudspeakers, MRI machines, compasses
Permanent Magnet Steel

Made of hardened steel or special alloys (Alnico, Neodymium). Retains magnetism for long time. Used in: compass needles, loudspeakers, electric meters.

Temporary Magnet Soft Iron

Made of soft iron. Strong magnet while magnetising force is applied; loses magnetism immediately when removed. Used in: electromagnets, electric bells, cranes.

Electromagnet Current

Soft iron core wrapped with coil. Magnetised by passing current. Strength controlled by current. Used in: electric bell, telephone receiver, MRI, scrapyard cranes.

Demagnetisation Losing Magnetism

Magnets lose magnetism by: heating (above Curie temperature), hammering/rough handling, and storing N near N pole (repulsion). Store bar magnets in pairs with unlike poles together.

⚑ Monopole Trap — Most Tested Fact
  • Magnetic poles always come in pairs (dipoles) — you can NEVER isolate a single magnetic pole. Cutting a magnet ALWAYS produces two complete magnets with both poles.
  • Soft iron is used for temporary magnets (electromagnets) — it gains and loses magnetism quickly
  • Steel / Alnico is used for permanent magnets — it retains magnetism
  • Aluminium and copper are NOT magnetic — they are non-ferromagnetic metals
Section 2 — Magnetic Field & Field Lines
🌐
Magnetic Field Lines & Properties
Visualising the invisible force around magnets
▾

The region around a magnet where its influence can be felt is called the magnetic field. Magnetic field lines (lines of force) are imaginary lines used to represent the magnetic field — they show both the direction and relative strength of the field.

Properties of Magnetic Field Lines
  • They emerge from the North pole and enter the South pole outside the magnet; inside the magnet, they travel from South to North (forming closed loops)
  • They never cross each other — at any point in space, the field has only one direction
  • Closer lines = stronger field — field lines are densest near the poles where the field is strongest
  • They are closed continuous loops — unlike electric field lines (which begin and end on charges), magnetic field lines form complete closed loops with no beginning or end
  • They leave and enter the poles at right angles to the surface of the magnet
  • The direction of the field at any point is the tangent to the field line at that point
Magnetic Field Strength
B = F / (qv)   ·   F = qvB sinθ
B = magnetic field (Tesla, T)  ·  F = force (N)  ·  q = charge (C)  ·  v = velocity (m/s)
θ = angle between velocity and magnetic field direction

1 Tesla (T) = SI unit of magnetic field  ·  1 Gauss (G) = 10⁻⁴ T (CGS unit)
A charge moving parallel to B (θ = 0°) experiences zero force (sin 0° = 0)
⚑ Field Lines — Key Traps
  • Magnetic field lines are closed loops — they go from N to S outside and S to N inside. They have no beginning or end.
  • Two field lines never cross — if they did, a compass placed there would point in two directions simultaneously, which is impossible
  • A charge moving parallel to a magnetic field experiences zero magnetic force (F = qvB sin0° = 0)
  • SI unit of magnetic field is Tesla (T) — NOT Gauss. 1 T = 10,000 Gauss
Section 3 — Earth's Magnetism
🌍
Earth as a Magnet — Elements of Earth's Magnetism
Magnetic declination, dip, and horizontal intensity
▾

The Earth behaves like a giant bar magnet. Its magnetic field is believed to be generated by electric currents in the liquid outer core (molten iron-nickel). The Earth's geographic north pole coincides approximately with its magnetic south pole — which is why the north-seeking pole of a compass (its N pole) points towards geographic north.

Three Elements of Earth's Magnetism — Exam Essential
  • Magnetic Declination (θ) — the angle between the geographic (true) north and the magnetic north at a place. A compass needle does not point to exact geographic north — it points to magnetic north. The difference is the declination. Varies from place to place.
  • Magnetic Dip / Inclination (δ) — the angle made by the Earth's total magnetic field with the horizontal at a given place. At the magnetic equator: dip = 0°. At the magnetic poles: dip = 90° (field is vertical). In India, dip is about 17°–24°.
  • Horizontal Component (H) — the horizontal component of Earth's total magnetic field. H = B_total × cos(δ). At magnetic poles, H = 0 (field is entirely vertical). At magnetic equator, H = B_total (maximum; field is entirely horizontal).
Earth's Magnetic Field Components
H = B cosδ   ·   V = B sinδ   ·   tan δ = V/H
B = total magnetic field of Earth  ·  H = horizontal component  ·  V = vertical component
δ = angle of dip (inclination)

At magnetic equator: δ = 0°, H = B (maximum), V = 0
At magnetic poles: δ = 90°, H = 0, V = B (maximum)
Key Facts About Earth's Magnetism
  • Earth's magnetic south pole is near the geographic north pole — the N-pole of a compass needle is attracted to it
  • The magnetic axis of Earth is tilted at about 11.3° to its geographic axis (rotation axis)
  • Earth's magnetic poles are not fixed — they slowly drift (secular variation)
  • Magnetic equator (aclinic line) — the line where angle of dip is zero; a freely suspended magnet is horizontal here
  • Isogonic lines — lines joining places of equal magnetic declination
  • Agonic lines — lines where magnetic declination is zero (compass points to true north)
  • Isoclinic lines (Isogonal) — lines joining places of equal dip
  • Earth's magnetic field reverses periodically over geological timescales (every few hundred thousand years)
Why Geographic North = Magnetic South

The north pole of a compass needle (which is itself a magnet) is attracted to the geographic north of Earth. By the law of magnetism (unlike poles attract), the geographic north region of Earth must contain a magnetic south pole. Earth's geographic north and magnetic south are near each other — this is the reason a freely suspended magnet points north.

⚑ Earth's Magnetism — Exam Traps
  • Earth's geographic north = Earth's magnetic south pole (unlike poles attract compass needle)
  • At the magnetic poles: dip = 90°, horizontal component = zero
  • At the magnetic equator: dip = 0°, horizontal component = maximum
  • Compass does NOT point to true geographic north — it points to magnetic north. The difference is the declination.
  • A dip needle (magnetic needle free to rotate vertically) is horizontal at magnetic equator and vertical at magnetic poles
Section 4 — Magnetic Effect of Electric Current
⚡
Oersted's Discovery & Magnetic Effect of Current
A current-carrying conductor creates a magnetic field around it
▾

In 1820, Hans Christian Oersted discovered that a compass needle deflects when placed near a current-carrying wire — establishing that electric current produces a magnetic field. This was the first evidence that electricity and magnetism are related.

Rules for Direction of Magnetic Field Due to Current

Right-Hand Thumb Rule (for straight wire): Grasp the wire with the right hand, thumb pointing in the direction of conventional current. The curled fingers show the direction of the circular magnetic field around the wire.

Right-Hand Screw Rule (Corkscrew Rule): If a right-handed screw rotates in the direction of the induced field, the direction of advancement of the screw is the direction of the current — and vice versa.

Right-Hand Rule for solenoid/coil: Wrap right hand around solenoid with fingers in current direction; thumb points to the North pole of the solenoid.

Factors Affecting Magnetic Field Strength of a Current-Carrying Conductor
  • Magnitude of current (I) — greater current → stronger magnetic field (B ∝ I)
  • Distance from conductor (r) — farther from wire → weaker field (B ∝ 1/r for straight wire)
  • Number of turns in coil — more turns → stronger field (field adds up)
  • Nature of core material — iron core inside coil dramatically amplifies the magnetic field (up to 1000×)
Key Magnetic Field Formulas
B = μ₀I / (2πr)  ·  B = μ₀nI (solenoid)
Straight wire: B = μ₀I/(2πr); B decreases with distance r from wire
Solenoid: B = μ₀nI; n = number of turns per unit length, I = current
μ₀ = permeability of free space = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A

A solenoid is a long coil of wire — it produces a uniform magnetic field inside (like a bar magnet), with a definite N and S pole at its ends.
Applications of Magnetic Effect of Current
  • Electromagnet — current-carrying coil around soft iron core; used in scrapyard cranes, electric bells, relays, MRI machines
  • Electric bell — electromagnet attracts a metal strip (armature), hits the bell; contact breaks, demagnetises coil, armature returns, contact re-made → continuous ringing
  • Loudspeaker — current-varying signal through coil in permanent magnetic field creates varying force → cone vibrates → sound
  • Galvanometer — coil in magnetic field deflects proportionally to current; basis of all current-measuring instruments
  • Telephone earpiece — varying current creates varying magnetic field → varying force on metal diaphragm → sound
⚑ Oersted Trap
  • Oersted discovered (1820) that current produces magnetism — this is the foundation of electromagnetism
  • The magnetic field around a straight wire forms concentric circles in planes perpendicular to the wire — not straight lines
  • Increasing current → field strength increases; increasing distance → field strength decreases
  • A solenoid acts like a bar magnet — it has distinct N and S poles; its field is strongest inside and nearly uniform
Section 5 — Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor
🔁
Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor — Fleming's Left-Hand Rule
The motor principle — how electric current becomes motion
▾

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in an external magnetic field, it experiences a mechanical force. This is the principle behind electric motors. The force is maximum when the current is perpendicular to the field, and zero when the current is parallel to the field.

Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor
F = BIL sinθ
F = force (N)  ·  B = magnetic field (T)  ·  I = current (A)  ·  L = length of conductor (m)
θ = angle between current direction and magnetic field direction

Maximum force when θ = 90° (current ⊥ field): F = BIL
Zero force when θ = 0° (current ∥ field): F = 0
Fleming's Left-Hand Rule — The Motor Rule

Stretch the thumb, index finger, and middle finger of the left hand so they are mutually perpendicular:

Index finger (Forefinger) → direction of magnetic Field (B)
Middle finger → direction of conventional Current (I)
Thumb → direction of Thrust/Force (Motion) on the conductor

Memory: FBI — Forefinger = B (field), Middle = current (I), Thumb = Force. Left hand for motor (Left hand rule → eLectric motor).

Electric Motor — Working Principle
  • A rectangular coil (armature) carrying current is placed between the poles of a permanent magnet
  • By Fleming's Left-Hand Rule, forces on opposite sides of the coil are in opposite directions → the coil rotates
  • A commutator (split-ring) reverses the current direction every half turn to maintain continuous rotation in one direction
  • A DC motor uses a commutator; an AC motor (induction motor) uses alternating current directly
  • Converts: Electrical energy → Mechanical (kinetic) energy
  • Applications: electric fans, water pumps, washing machines, electric vehicles, lifts
⚑ Left vs Right Hand — Never Confuse
  • Fleming's Left-Hand Rule → Motor (current in field → force/motion)
  • Fleming's Right-Hand Rule → Generator (motion in field → induced current)
  • Memory trick: Motor = Left (M·L); Generator = Right (G·R) — alphabetical order M before G, L before R
  • Force is zero when current and field are parallel — directly tested in NDA
Section 6 — Electromagnetic Induction
⚙️
Faraday's Laws & Lenz's Law
How changing magnetism creates electricity — the generator principle
▾

In 1831, Michael Faraday discovered that a changing magnetic flux through a circuit induces an EMF (and hence a current) in that circuit. This is called electromagnetic induction — the reverse of Oersted's finding, and the foundation of all electrical power generation.

Faraday's Two Laws of Electromagnetic Induction
  • First Law: Whenever the magnetic flux through a circuit changes, an EMF is induced in the circuit. The induced EMF (and current) lasts only as long as the flux is changing — a static magnetic field induces nothing.
  • Second Law: The magnitude of the induced EMF is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux. EMF = −dΦ/dt. Faster change → larger induced EMF.
Faraday's Law & Lenz's Law
EMF = −N × (ΔΦ/Δt)
EMF = induced electromotive force (V)  ·  N = number of turns in coil
ΔΦ = change in magnetic flux (Weber, Wb)  ·  Δt = time interval (s)
The negative sign represents Lenz's Law — the induced EMF opposes the change causing it

Magnetic flux: Φ = B × A × cosθ (B = field, A = area, θ = angle between B and normal to area)
Lenz's Law — Conservation of Energy in Induction

The direction of the induced current is always such that it opposes the change in magnetic flux that caused it. If you push a magnet into a coil, the coil's induced current creates a magnetic field that repels the incoming magnet — you must do work to push it in. This work appears as electrical energy. Lenz's Law is a direct consequence of the conservation of energy.

Fleming's Right-Hand Rule — The Generator Rule

Stretch the thumb, index finger, and middle finger of the right hand mutually perpendicular:

Index finger (Forefinger) → direction of magnetic Field (B)
Middle finger → direction of induced Current
Thumb → direction of Motion (velocity) of conductor

Used to find the direction of induced current in a generator/dynamo when the conductor moves in a magnetic field.

AC Generator (Dynamo) — Working Principle
  • A rectangular coil is rotated inside a uniform magnetic field between strong poles
  • As the coil rotates, the magnetic flux through it changes continuously → alternating EMF is induced
  • The induced EMF is sinusoidal: E = E₀ sin(ωt), where E₀ is the peak EMF
  • Slip rings (not commutator) allow the alternating current to flow out to the external circuit without reversing it → produces AC
  • Converts: Mechanical energy → Electrical energy (AC)
  • Applications: power stations, bicycle dynamos, alternators in cars
  • A DC generator uses a commutator instead of slip rings → rectifies the AC to pulsating DC
Ways to Increase Induced EMF (and Current)
  • Increase the speed of rotation (or speed of relative motion between conductor and field)
  • Increase the number of turns (N) in the coil — EMF ∝ N
  • Increase the strength of the magnetic field (B) — use stronger magnets
  • Increase the area of the coil (A) — larger coil sweeps more flux
  • Use a soft iron core inside the coil to concentrate the magnetic field
⚑ Generator vs Motor — Key Exam Distinctions
  • A static magnetic field induces no EMF — only a changing flux induces EMF
  • Generator: uses slip rings → produces AC. DC generator uses commutator → produces DC
  • Motor: uses commutator to maintain rotation direction
  • Lenz's Law: induced current opposes the change — it does NOT stop the change entirely
  • Faraday was the discoverer of electromagnetic induction; the unit of capacitance (Farad) is also named after him
Section 7 — Transformer & Mutual Induction
🔌
Transformer — Step-Up & Step-Down
Changing voltage levels using mutual induction — AC only
▾

A transformer is a device that transfers electrical energy between two circuits through mutual induction — the changing current in the primary coil creates a changing magnetic flux, which induces an EMF in the secondary coil. Transformers work only with AC — a steady DC produces no changing flux and therefore no induction.

Transformer Equations
V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p = I_p/I_s
V_s, V_p = secondary and primary voltages  ·  N_s, N_p = secondary and primary turns
I_s, I_p = secondary and primary currents

For an ideal (100% efficient) transformer: V_p × I_p = V_s × I_s (input power = output power)
Note: current ratio is inverse of voltage ratio — voltage steps up → current steps down (energy conserved)
PropertyStep-Up TransformerStep-Down Transformer
TurnsN_s > N_p (more turns in secondary)N_s < N_p (fewer turns in secondary)
VoltageV_s > V_p (voltage increases)V_s < V_p (voltage decreases)
CurrentI_s < I_p (current decreases)I_s > I_p (current increases)
UsePower stations — raise voltage before long-distance transmission (reduces I²R losses)Substations, homes — reduce voltage to safe 220 V for domestic use
Why High Voltage for Power Transmission?
  • Power loss in transmission lines: P_loss = I²R. Lower current (I) → dramatically less power loss
  • Step-up transformer increases voltage (e.g., 11 kV → 220 kV) → current drops proportionally
  • At the destination, step-down transformer reduces voltage back to 220 V for home use
  • This is why AC is preferred for power distribution — transformers only work with AC
Transformer Losses — Reasons for Less Than 100% Efficiency
  • Copper losses (I²R losses) — heating of primary and secondary coil wires; minimised by using thick copper wire
  • Iron / core losses — two types: (a) Eddy current losses — induced circular currents in the iron core waste energy as heat; minimised by using a laminated core (thin insulated layers); (b) Hysteresis losses — energy lost in magnetising and demagnetising the core in each AC cycle; minimised by using silicon steel core
  • Flux leakage — not all magnetic flux from primary links with secondary coil; minimised by winding coils on the same core
⚑ Transformer Exam Traps
  • Transformers work on AC only — DC produces no changing flux → no induction → no output
  • When voltage steps up, current steps down (energy conservation — power = VI remains constant for ideal transformer)
  • Eddy current losses are reduced by laminating the core (NOT by using soft iron)
  • Transformer neither creates nor destroys energy — it only changes voltage level
  • A transformer with more turns in primary than secondary is a step-down transformer

Curie Temperature — Exam Fact
  • Curie temperature — the temperature above which a ferromagnetic material loses its ferromagnetic properties and becomes paramagnetic
  • Iron's Curie temperature ≈ 770°C
  • Nickel's Curie temperature ≈ 358°C
  • Cobalt's Curie temperature ≈ 1115°C (highest among common ferromagnets)
  • Magnets lose magnetism when heated above Curie temperature — this is why heating a magnet demagnetises it
Important Magnetic Materials & Their Uses
  • Alnico (Al-Ni-Co alloy) — high coercivity; used in permanent magnets, loudspeakers, motors
  • Neodymium magnets (Nd-Fe-B) — strongest permanent magnets known; used in hard disks, headphones, MRI machines
  • Ferrites (Fe₂O₃ compounds) — used in transformer cores, computer memories, radio antennae; low eddy current losses
  • Silicon steel (electrical steel) — used in transformer and motor cores; low hysteresis loss
  • Soft iron — high permeability, low coercivity; used in electromagnet cores, transformer cores, shielding
  • Bismuth — strongest diamagnetic material; used in magnetic levitation demonstrations
⚑ Classification Traps
  • Aluminium is paramagnetic, NOT diamagnetic — it is weakly attracted to magnets. This is directly tested.
  • Copper, gold, silver, bismuth are diamagnetic — weakly repelled by magnets. Most exam-goers think metals are magnetic.
  • Only iron, nickel, and cobalt (and their alloys) are ferromagnetic
  • Above Curie temperature, ferromagnets become paramagnetic — NOT diamagnetic
  • Superconductors exhibit perfect diamagnetism (Meissner effect) — they completely expel magnetic fields; this causes magnetic levitation
