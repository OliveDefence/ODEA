Section 1 — Electric Charge, Current & Potential
⚡
Electric Charge & Current
The flow of electrons that powers everything
▾

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter. There are two types — positive (protons) and negative (electrons). Like charges repel; unlike charges attract. The SI unit of charge is the Coulomb (C).

Current, Charge & Time
I = Q / t   ·   Q = ne
I = electric current (Ampere, A)  ·  Q = charge (Coulomb, C)  ·  t = time (s)
n = number of electrons  ·  e = charge of one electron = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C

Conventional current direction: positive terminal → external circuit → negative terminal (opposite to electron flow)
Electron flow: negative terminal → external circuit → positive terminal
Key Definitions — All Exam-Tested
  • Electric current (I) — rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor; 1 Ampere = 1 Coulomb per second
  • Potential difference / Voltage (V) — work done per unit charge to move charge between two points; unit = Volt (V) = J/C
  • EMF (Electromotive Force) — energy provided by a source (battery/cell) per unit charge; measured in Volts; drives current around the circuit
  • Resistance (R) — opposition to the flow of current; unit = Ohm (Ω)
  • Conductors — allow easy flow of current (metals, especially silver, copper, aluminium)
  • Insulators — oppose current flow (rubber, plastic, glass, wood, air)
⚑ Conventional Current vs Electron Flow — Exam Trap
  • Conventional current flows from positive to negative (outside the battery) — this is the historical convention, established before electrons were discovered
  • Actual electron flow is from negative to positive — opposite to conventional current
  • In all circuit analysis and exam questions, use conventional current direction unless specifically asked otherwise
Section 2 — Ohm's Law & Resistance
🔋
Ohm's Law
The single most tested relationship in electricity
▾

Ohm's Law states that the current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it, provided the temperature and other physical conditions remain constant.

Ohm's Law
V = IR   →   I = V/R   →   R = V/I
V = potential difference (Volts, V)  ·  I = current (Amperes, A)  ·  R = resistance (Ohms, Ω)

Ohmic conductors: V-I graph is a straight line through origin (e.g., metallic conductors at constant temperature)
Non-ohmic conductors: V-I relationship is non-linear (e.g., diode, LED, filament bulb, electrolytes)
Factors Affecting Resistance of a Wire
  • Length (L): R ∝ L — longer wire → higher resistance
  • Cross-sectional area (A): R ∝ 1/A — thicker wire → lower resistance
  • Material (ρ): R = ρL/A — ρ is resistivity (specific resistance), depends on the material
  • Temperature: For metals — resistance increases with temperature. For semiconductors and electrolytes — resistance decreases with temperature (opposite of metals)
Resistance Formula
R = ρL / A
ρ (rho) = resistivity of the material (Ω·m)  ·  L = length (m)  ·  A = cross-sectional area (m²)

Best conductors (lowest ρ): Silver > Copper > Gold > Aluminium
Nichrome (nickel-chromium alloy) has high resistivity + stable resistance with temperature → used in heaters and toasters
⚑ Resistance Traps
  • Resistance of metals increases with temperature — a bulb filament has more resistance when glowing than when cold
  • Resistance of semiconductors (e.g., thermistors) and electrolytes decreases with temperature
  • If a wire is stretched to double its length: L doubles, A halves (volume constant), so R increases by 4 times (R = ρL/A → 2L/(A/2) = 4ρL/A)
  • Resistivity depends only on the material and temperature — NOT on the shape or size of the conductor

Series & Parallel Resistance Formulas
Series: R_s = R₁ + R₂ + …   ·   Parallel: 1/R_p = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + …
Two resistors in parallel: R_p = R₁R₂/(R₁+R₂) (product over sum)
n identical resistors R in series: R_total = nR
n identical resistors R in parallel: R_total = R/n

Parallel resistance is ALWAYS less than the smallest individual resistance.
Why Parallel at Home? Domestic

All appliances get the same 220 V. Switching one off doesn't affect others. Total resistance decreases as more appliances are added — allowing each to draw its rated current independently.

Christmas Lights (Series) Series

Old string lights were wired in series. One bulb fusing → all go out. Modern lights use parallel connections so one fused bulb doesn't darken the whole string.

⚑ Series vs Parallel — Most Common Exam Errors
  • In series: current is same, voltage splits. In parallel: voltage is same, current splits.
  • Parallel R is always less than the smallest resistor — adding any resistor in parallel always reduces total resistance
  • Adding a bulb in series reduces total current → all bulbs get dimmer. Adding a bulb in parallel doesn't change voltage → other bulbs stay at same brightness
  • Household wiring is parallel — NOT series. This is why turning off one light doesn't affect other appliances.
Section 4 — Electrical Power & Energy
💡
Power, Energy & Commercial Units
Watts, kilowatt-hours, and your electricity bill
▾
Power & Energy Formulas
P = VI = I²R = V²/R   ·   E = Pt
P = power (Watt, W)  ·  V = voltage (V)  ·  I = current (A)  ·  R = resistance (Ω)
E = electrical energy (Joules, J)  ·  t = time (seconds)

1 Watt = 1 Joule per second = 1 J/s
1 kWh (kilowatt-hour) = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 10⁶ J — the commercial unit of electrical energy (one "unit" on your electricity bill)
Power Rating of Common Appliances
  • LED bulb ≈ 5–10 W  ·  CFL bulb ≈ 15–20 W  ·  Incandescent bulb ≈ 60–100 W
  • Ceiling fan ≈ 70–80 W  ·  Refrigerator ≈ 150–200 W
  • Air conditioner ≈ 1000–2000 W  ·  Electric iron ≈ 1000 W
  • Electric heater ≈ 1000–2000 W  ·  Microwave ≈ 800–1200 W
  • A 100 W bulb running for 10 hours uses 1 kWh = 1 unit of electricity
Brighter Bulb in Series vs Parallel

When two different bulbs (say 60 W and 100 W rated for 220 V) are connected in series: both receive less than 220 V; the 60 W bulb (higher resistance) glows brighter (more voltage across it). In parallel: both receive full 220 V; the 100 W bulb glows brighter (rated for more power). This is a classic CDS/NDA conceptual trap.

⚑ Power Traps — Series vs Parallel Bulbs
  • Higher wattage bulb has lower resistance (P = V²/R → higher P means lower R for same V)
  • In series: higher resistance bulb (lower wattage) is brighter — more voltage drop across it
  • In parallel: higher wattage bulb is brighter — same voltage, draws more current
  • 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J — this is the unit on your electricity bill; exam asks the joule equivalent frequently
Section 5 — Heating Effect of Current
🌡️
Joule's Law of Heating
Why wires heat up — and how it's put to use
▾

When electric current flows through a conductor, electrical energy is converted into heat energy. This is called the heating effect of current or Joule heating.

Joule's Law of Heating
H = I²Rt
H = heat produced (Joules)  ·  I = current (A)  ·  R = resistance (Ω)  ·  t = time (s)

Heat ∝ I² (doubling current → 4× more heat)
Heat ∝ R (higher resistance → more heat for same current)
Heat ∝ t (longer time → more heat)
Appliances Based on Heating Effect of Current
  • Electric iron — nichrome heating element converts electrical energy to heat
  • Electric heater / room heater — nichrome coil radiates heat
  • Electric kettle / immersion heater — heats water directly
  • Toaster — nichrome wire glows hot; toasts bread by radiation
  • Incandescent bulb — tungsten filament (very high melting point: 3422°C) glows white-hot; inefficient as most energy is heat, not light
  • Electric fuse — thin wire of low melting point alloy (tin-lead); melts and breaks circuit when excess current flows, protecting appliances
Why Nichrome is Used in Heating Elements
  • High resistivity — generates more heat for the same current
  • High melting point — does not melt at operating temperatures
  • Does not oxidise (corrode) easily at high temperatures — long service life
  • Resistance remains stable with temperature changes — consistent heating
⚑ Tungsten vs Nichrome — Exam Trap
  • Tungsten is used in bulb filaments — very high melting point (3422°C), glows white-hot to produce light
  • Nichrome is used in heating elements (heaters, irons, toasters) — high resistivity, stable resistance, does not oxidise
  • Both are chosen for different reasons: tungsten for its optical properties at extreme temperature; nichrome for its electrical and thermal stability at moderate temperatures
Section 6 — Fuse, Earthing & Domestic Circuits
🔧
Fuse, MCB, Earthing & Domestic Wiring
Protecting lives and appliances from electrical faults
▾
Electric Fuse — Working Principle

A fuse is a safety device — a thin wire of low melting point alloy (tin-lead, or bismuth-tin alloy) connected in series with the circuit. When current exceeds the fuse rating, the fuse wire heats up (Joule heating), melts, and breaks the circuit, protecting appliances and wiring from damage due to overloading or short circuit.

Fuse Melts

Thin alloy wire. Must be replaced after melting. Inserted in series, in the live wire. Rating: 1A, 2A, 5A, 15A etc. based on maximum safe current.

MCB Trips

Miniature Circuit Breaker. Automatically trips (disconnects) on overload or short circuit. Can be reset by flipping the switch — no replacement needed. More reliable and safer than fuses.

Earthing (Grounding) Safety

The metallic body of appliances is connected to the Earth (zero potential) via a green wire. If a fault occurs (live wire touches body), current flows safely to Earth instead of through a person.

ELCB / RCD Leakage

Earth Leakage Circuit Breaker. Detects tiny current imbalances (leakage to earth) and cuts power in milliseconds — protects against electrocution even when current is below fuse rating.

Domestic Wiring — Key Facts
  • Live wire (L) — brown (or red in older wiring); carries current at high potential (~220 V in India)
  • Neutral wire (N) — blue (or black); at zero or near-zero potential; completes the circuit
  • Earth wire (E) — green/yellow; safety wire connected to ground; zero potential
  • Fuse is always inserted in the live wire — breaking the live wire isolates the appliance from high potential
  • All appliances in Indian homes receive 220–240 V AC at 50 Hz
  • The main supply is alternating current (AC) — direction of current reverses 50 times per second (50 Hz)
  • Overloading — connecting too many high-power appliances simultaneously; total current exceeds safe limit; fuse blows
  • Short circuit — accidental direct connection between live and neutral wires; resistance drops to near zero → enormous current → fuse blows
⚑ Domestic Wiring Exam Traps
  • Fuse is always in the live wire — NEVER in the neutral wire (a fuse in the neutral wire would isolate neutral but leave appliance body at live potential — dangerous)
  • Indian domestic supply: 220 V, 50 Hz AC — both values are tested
  • Earthing protects against electric shock — it does NOT prevent overloading
  • MCB is reusable (just reset) — fuse must be replaced after blowing
  • Short circuit: very high current due to very low resistance — not high voltage
Section 7 — Magnetism & Electromagnetism
🧲
Electromagnetism — Oersted to Faraday
How electricity creates magnetism — and how magnetism creates electricity
▾

Oersted's discovery (1820): A current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field around it. This established that electricity and magnetism are related — the foundation of electromagnetism.

Key Laws & Rules of Electromagnetism
  • Right-hand thumb rule — point the thumb of the right hand in the direction of current in a straight wire; the curled fingers indicate the direction of the magnetic field (circular field lines around the wire)
  • Fleming's Left-Hand Rule — for a motor: point thumb (force/motion), index finger (field B), middle finger (current I) of the left hand mutually perpendicular; gives direction of force on current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field
  • Fleming's Right-Hand Rule — for a generator: thumb (motion of conductor), index (field B), middle (induced current) of right hand; gives direction of induced current (used in generators/dynamos)
  • Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction — when the magnetic flux through a circuit changes, an EMF is induced in the circuit. EMF ∝ rate of change of magnetic flux
  • Lenz's Law — the induced current always opposes the change in magnetic flux that caused it (consequence of conservation of energy)

Electric Generator / DynamoMoving conductor in magnetic field induces EMF (Faraday's Law). Fleming's Right-Hand Rule gives current direction.Mechanical energy → Electrical energy

Transformer Principle & Formula
  • Turns ratio: V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p (voltage ratio equals turns ratio)
  • Step-up transformer: N_s > N_p → V_s > V_p (increases voltage; used in power transmission)
  • Step-down transformer: N_s < N_p → V_s < V_p (decreases voltage; used at homes and substations)
  • Transformers work on AC only — they cannot work with DC (need changing flux)
  • Power is transmitted at high voltage to reduce I²R losses in transmission lines
⚑ Motor vs Generator — Fleming's Rules Trap
  • Motor uses Left-Hand Rule (force on current in field) — LHR for Motor
  • Generator uses Right-Hand Rule (motion induces current) — RHR for Generator
  • A simple memory aid: Motor → Left hand; Generator → Right hand (M comes before G, L comes before R)
  • Transformers work on AC only — DC produces no changing flux, so no induced EMF

Key Electrical Devices & Inventors — Exam Staples
  • Battery — invented by Alessandro Volta (1800); converts chemical energy to electrical energy
  • Dynamo (AC generator) — invented by Michael Faraday; converts mechanical → electrical energy
  • Electric motor — converts electrical → mechanical energy; used in fans, pumps, EVs
  • Galvanometer — detects and measures small currents; works on motor principle
  • Ammeter — measures current; connected in series; has very low resistance
  • Voltmeter — measures potential difference; connected in parallel; has very high resistance
  • Rheostat — variable resistor; controls current in a circuit
  • Diode — allows current in one direction only; used for rectification (AC → DC)
Why Ammeter in Series, Voltmeter in Parallel?

An ammeter must carry the full circuit current — so it is placed in series. It has very low resistance so it doesn't alter the circuit current significantly. A voltmeter measures potential difference between two points — so it is placed in parallel across the component. It has very high resistance so it draws negligible current and doesn't disturb the circuit.

⚑ Measuring Instruments — Exam Trap
  • Ammeter → series, low resistance — never connect in parallel (would short-circuit the component)
  • Voltmeter → parallel, high resistance — never connect in series (would read zero, block current)
  • Indian AC supply: 220 V, 50 Hz — peak voltage ≈ 311 V (RMS = peak/√2)
  • Galvanometer can be converted to ammeter by adding a shunt (low resistance in parallel)
  • Galvanometer can be converted to voltmeter by adding a multiplier (high resistance in series)

Series vs Parallel — Quick Rules
Circuit Configurations
  • Series: current same; voltage splits; R_total = R₁+R₂ (larger than any single R)

  • Series: higher-R bulb glows brighter; Parallel: higher-wattage bulb glows brighter
  • n equal R in series: R_total = nR; in parallel: R_total = R/n
