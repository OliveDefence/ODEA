The Excretory System
Organs of excretion, kidney structure, nephron filtration, urine formation, osmoregulation and excretory disorders — at CDS, NDA & AFCAT exam depth.
CDS NDA AFCAT Kidney Nephron Osmoregulation

Excretion is the removal of metabolic waste products from the body. It is different from egestion (removal of undigested food). The human excretory system primarily involves the kidneys (nitrogenous waste + water balance), but the lungs (CO₂), skin (salts, water via sweat) and liver (urea synthesis, bile pigments) are also organs of excretion.

Types of Nitrogenous Waste — Excretory Patterns
  • Ammonotelic — excretes ammonia directly; most toxic, needs lots of water. Examples: bony fish, aquatic invertebrates
  • Ureotelic — excretes urea; less toxic than ammonia; moderately water soluble. Examples: humans, mammals, adult amphibians, cartilaginous fish
  • Uricotelic — excretes uric acid; least toxic; almost insoluble in water; least water required. Examples: birds, reptiles, insects, terrestrial snails
⚑ Exam Trap — Excretion vs Egestion

Excretion = removal of metabolic waste produced by body cells (e.g., urea from amino acid breakdown, CO₂ from respiration). Egestion = removal of undigested food material via the anus (faeces). Faeces is NOT metabolic waste — it was never absorbed. Most exam MCQs deliberately try to confuse these two terms.

Section 2 — The Kidneys
🫘
Kidney — Structure & Key Facts
Position, gross anatomy and blood supply
▾

Humans have two bean-shaped kidneys located in the retroperitoneal space (behind the peritoneum) on either side of the vertebral column. The right kidney is slightly lower than the left (displaced downward by the liver).

Gross Anatomy of the Kidney
  • Cortex — outer region; contains Bowman's capsule, glomerulus and proximal + distal convoluted tubules of nephrons; lighter in colour
  • Medulla — inner region; contains loops of Henle and collecting ducts; organised into cone-shaped renal pyramids
  • Pelvis (Renal pelvis) — funnel-shaped chamber at hilum; collects urine from medulla → ureter
  • Hilum — medial indentation; entry/exit point for renal artery, renal vein, ureter and nerves
  • Each kidney is covered by a fibrous renal capsule
Kidney's Blood Supply — Extraordinarily Rich

The kidneys receive about 20–25% of cardiac output (~1.2 L/min) despite being small organs. Blood enters via the renal artery (from the aorta) and leaves via the renal vein (to the inferior vena cava). This massive blood supply allows the kidneys to filter the entire blood volume approximately 60 times per day. Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) = ~125 mL/min = ~180 litres/day of filtrate.

The Urinary Tract — Path of Urine
  • Kidney → ureter (muscular tube, peristalsis moves urine down) → urinary bladder (stores 300–500 mL) → urethra (exits body)
  • Urethra length: males ~20 cm · females ~4 cm (shorter → more prone to UTIs)
Section 3 — The Nephron: Structural & Functional Unit
🔬
Nephron — Structure
The filtration unit — every part and its location
▾

The nephron is the structural and functional unit of the kidney. Each kidney contains about 1 million nephrons. A nephron consists of a renal corpuscle (Bowman's capsule + glomerulus) and a renal tubule.

Glomerulus Cortex

Tuft of capillaries enclosed in Bowman's capsule. Site of ultrafiltration. Blood pressure forces fluid out of capillaries into the capsular space.

Bowman's Capsule Cortex

Cup-shaped double-walled structure surrounding the glomerulus. Collects the glomerular filtrate. Inner layer = podocytes (filtration cells).

PCT Cortex

Proximal Convoluted Tubule. First reabsorption zone. Reabsorbs ~65–70% of filtered water, glucose, amino acids, Na⁺ and other ions.

Loop of Henle Medulla

Hairpin-shaped tubule dipping into the medulla. Creates a concentration gradient in the medullary interstitium. Descending limb permeable to water; ascending limb impermeable to water (pumps ions out).

DCT Cortex

Distal Convoluted Tubule. Fine-tunes reabsorption of water and Na⁺ under hormonal control (ADH, aldosterone). Also secretes H⁺ and K⁺ ions.

Collecting Duct Medulla

Final concentration of urine. Very responsive to ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone). Drains into renal pelvis. Multiple nephrons share one collecting duct.

Two Types of Nephron
  • Cortical nephrons (~85%) — glomerulus in outer cortex; short loop of Henle that barely enters medulla; mainly involved in filtration
  • Juxtamedullary nephrons (~15%) — glomerulus near corticomedullary junction; long loop of Henle that dips deep into medulla; essential for producing concentrated urine; have vasa recta (capillary loops alongside loop of Henle)
Think of it this way

The nephron is like a highly selective coffee filter. First, everything (blood minus large proteins and cells) is forced through (filtration). Then the filter carefully takes back useful things like glucose, water and amino acids (reabsorption). Finally, a few extra toxins are added in through the wall (secretion). What remains is urine — exactly what the body doesn't need.

Section 4 — Urine Formation: Three Processes
💧
Glomerular Filtration — Step 1
Ultrafiltration under hydrostatic pressure
▾

Glomerular filtration is the first step — blood is filtered under high pressure as it passes through the glomerular capillaries into Bowman's capsule. The process is non-selective for small molecules.

What Passes Into the Filtrate vs What Stays in Blood

Passes into filtrate (glomerular filtrate): water, glucose, amino acids, urea, uric acid, creatinine, ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻), small hormones.
Stays in blood (cannot pass): plasma proteins (albumin, globulin — too large), RBCs and WBCs (too large).
Filtrate is essentially plasma minus proteins and cells.

⚑ Exam Trap

The kidneys filter ~180 litres of filtrate per day but produce only ~1.5 litres of urine. This means over 99% of the filtrate is reabsorbed. If proteins appear in urine (proteinuria), it indicates damaged glomerular filtration membrane — a sign of kidney disease.

♻️
Tubular Reabsorption — Step 2
Reclaiming useful substances from filtrate back into blood
▾

As filtrate flows through the tubules, useful substances are transported back into the peritubular capillaries (capillary network surrounding the tubules). This is a highly selective and energy-dependent process.

⚑ Key Reabsorption Traps
  • Glucose is completely reabsorbed in the PCT in normal conditions — therefore, no glucose in normal urine. Glucose appears in urine (glucosuria) only when blood glucose exceeds the renal threshold (~180 mg/dL) as in diabetes mellitus.
  • Ascending limb of loop of Henle is impermeable to water — it actively pumps out ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻) to create the medullary concentration gradient needed for water reabsorption in the collecting duct.
  • Urea is NOT fully reabsorbed — it is the main nitrogenous waste in urine.
➕
Tubular Secretion — Step 3
Moving extra waste from blood into the tubule
▾

Tubular secretion is the movement of substances from peritubular capillaries into the tubule lumen. This is essentially the opposite of reabsorption — it adds more waste to the filtrate for excretion.

Substances Secreted Into Tubules
  • H⁺ ions — helps regulate blood pH (acid-base balance)
  • K⁺ ions — regulated by aldosterone; DCT secretes K⁺ when Na⁺ is reabsorbed
  • Ammonia (NH₃) — produced by tubule cells; combines with H⁺ → NH₄⁺ for excretion
  • Creatinine — metabolic waste from muscle; both filtered AND secreted
  • Drugs and foreign substances — penicillin, aspirin, certain toxins
  • Tubular secretion is why creatinine clearance slightly overestimates GFR

RAAS — Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System
  • ↓ Blood pressure / ↓ Na⁺ detected by juxtaglomerular (JG) cells in afferent arteriole
  • JG cells secrete Renin (enzyme)
  • Renin converts Angiotensinogen (liver) → Angiotensin I
  • Angiotensin I → Angiotensin II (by ACE — Angiotensin Converting Enzyme, mainly in lungs)
  • Angiotensin II: (a) stimulates adrenal cortex → Aldosterone → Na⁺ reabsorption → ↑ BP, (b) causes vasoconstriction → ↑ BP directly
  • ACE inhibitors (e.g., enalapril) block this pathway → used as antihypertensive drugs
⚑ ADH vs Aldosterone — The Most Tested Pair
  • ADH controls water reabsorption (responds to osmolarity/dehydration). Absent ADH → Diabetes Insipidus (large volumes of very dilute urine, up to 20 L/day — NOT diabetes mellitus which is about glucose)
  • Aldosterone controls sodium reabsorption (responds to blood pressure/volume via RAAS)
  • Both act on DCT and collecting duct — but for different substances and different stimuli.

⚑ Abnormal Urine — Clinical Significance
  • Glucosuria (glucose in urine) → Diabetes mellitus (blood glucose > renal threshold ~180 mg/dL)
  • Proteinuria (protein in urine) → Glomerulonephritis, nephrotic syndrome (damaged filter)
  • Haematuria (blood/RBCs in urine) → Kidney stones, infection, cancer
  • Ketonuria (ketone bodies) → Uncontrolled diabetes, starvation
  • Bilirubinuria (bilirubin in urine) → Liver disease, obstructive jaundice
  • Polyuria (excess urine >3 L/day) → Diabetes insipidus, diabetes mellitus
  • Oliguria (<400 mL/day) → Dehydration, acute kidney failure
  • Anuria (<100 mL/day) → Complete kidney failure, medical emergency

Dialysis — Artificial Kidney

Haemodialysis: blood is removed from the body and filtered through an artificial semipermeable membrane (dialyser) which removes waste products (urea, creatinine, excess ions) by diffusion into the dialysis fluid. The cleaned blood is returned to the body. Typically done 3 times/week for 3–4 hours per session for patients with ESRD.

Peritoneal dialysis: the peritoneum (abdominal membrane) is used as the dialysing membrane; dialysis fluid is infused into the abdominal cavity, waste diffuses into the fluid, which is then drained out. Can be done at home.

Kidney Transplant — Key Facts
  • Best treatment for end-stage renal disease; one functioning kidney is sufficient for survival
  • Recipient needs immunosuppressive drugs lifelong to prevent rejection
  • Donor kidney can be from a living donor (related/unrelated) or cadaveric donor
  • India has the 3rd highest number of kidney transplants globally

Additional Excretory Functions — Skin & Liver
  • Skin — sweat glands produce sweat: mainly water and NaCl; also small amounts of urea and lactic acid. Sweat is primarily for thermoregulation, with excretion being a secondary function
  • Liver — converts toxic ammonia → urea (via the ornithine cycle / urea cycle); bile pigments (bilirubin from Hb breakdown) excreted via bile into intestine → faeces give brown colour
  • Lungs — excrete CO₂ (~200 mL/min) and water vapour; primary function is gas exchange but CO₂ elimination = excretory function
⚑ Malpighian Tubules — Frequently Tested

Malpighian tubules are the excretory organs of insects and spiders (arachnids). They are slender tubules arising from the junction of the midgut and hindgut. They absorb nitrogenous waste from the haemolymph (insect blood) and release uric acid into the hindgut, where water is reabsorbed. This allows insects to excrete nearly dry waste — a key adaptation to terrestrial life. Named after Marcello Malpighi.

Excretory Types & Organs
  • Ammonotelic — ammonia; bony fish, aquatic invertebrates
  • Ureotelic — urea; humans, mammals, adult amphibians
  • Uricotelic — uric acid; birds, reptiles, insects
  • Excretion ≠ Egestion — metabolic waste vs undigested food
  • Malpighian tubules — insects & spiders; uric acid
  • Flame cells — flatworms · Nephridia — earthworm · Green glands — crustaceans
Kidney — Numbers & Anatomy
  • ~1 million nephrons per kidney; right kidney slightly lower than left
  • GFR = 125 mL/min = 180 L/day filtrate; only ~1.5 L urine produced
  • Cortex: glomerulus, PCT, DCT · Medulla: loop of Henle, collecting duct
  • Hilum: entry point for renal artery, renal vein, ureter
  • Juxtamedullary nephrons (15%) — produce concentrated urine via long loop
  • Kidneys receive ~20–25% of cardiac output (~1.2 L/min)
Urine Formation — 3 Steps
  • Filtration (Bowman's capsule) — plasma minus proteins/cells = filtrate
  • Reabsorption — PCT reabsorbs all glucose, amino acids, 65–70% water
  • Ascending loop: impermeable to water; pumps Na⁺/K⁺/Cl⁻ ions out
  • Secretion — H⁺, K⁺, NH₃, creatinine, drugs added to filtrate
  • Glucosuria: blood glucose > renal threshold 180 mg/dL
  • Normal urine: water 95%, urea 2%, creatinine, salts; NO glucose/protein
Hormonal Regulation
  • ADH (Vasopressin) — dehydration → posterior pituitary → DCT/CD water reabsorption → concentrated urine
  • No ADH → Diabetes Insipidus (large dilute urine; NOT blood glucose)
  • Aldosterone — adrenal cortex → Na⁺ reabsorption; K⁺ secretion in DCT
  • RAAS: ↓BP → JG cells → Renin → Angiotensin I → ACE → Angiotensin II → Aldosterone
  • ANP — heart stretches → ↑ Na⁺ excretion → ↓ BP
  • ACE inhibitors block RAAS → antihypertensive drugs
Abnormal Urine — Clinical Links
  • Glucosuria → Diabetes mellitus
  • Proteinuria → Glomerulonephritis / nephrotic syndrome
  • Haematuria → Kidney stones, infection, cancer
  • Polyuria (>3 L/day) → Diabetes insipidus or mellitus
  • Oliguria (<400 mL/day) → Dehydration / AKI
  • Anuria (<100 mL/day) → Complete kidney failure (emergency)
Disorders & Treatments
  • Kidney stones — calcium oxalate most common; flank pain, haematuria
  • Gout — excess uric acid → joint crystals; big toe; treated with allopurinol
  • UTI — E. coli; more in females (short urethra); dysuria, frequency
  • CKD — diabetes + hypertension = top 2 causes; irreversible
  • Dialysis — haemodialysis (machine) or peritoneal dialysis (abdominal membrane)
  • Urea cycle (liver): ammonia → urea; aka ornithine cycle
