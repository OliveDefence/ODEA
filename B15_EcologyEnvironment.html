Ecology & Environment
Ecosystems, food chains, biogeochemical cycles, biodiversity, pollution and environmental issues for CDS · NDA · AFCAT
CDS NDA AFCAT Theory 15 MCQs

Ecology is the scientific study of the interactions between organisms and their environment. The term was coined by Ernst Haeckel in 1866 (from Greek oikos = house + logos = study). Ecology operates at multiple levels — individual organisms, populations, communities, ecosystems and the biosphere. Understanding how energy flows, how matter cycles, and how human activity disrupts these systems is central to defence exam biology.

Section 1 — Levels of Organisation & Ecosystem
🌍
Ecosystem — Structure & Components
Biotic and abiotic components, autotrophs, heterotrophs, decomposers
▾

An ecosystem is a functional unit of nature where living organisms (biotic components) interact with each other and with the non-living environment (abiotic components). The term was coined by A.G. Tansley in 1935.

Biotic Components of Ecosystem
  • Producers (Autotrophs) — synthesise organic food from inorganic substances using sunlight (photosynthesis) or chemical energy (chemosynthesis). Examples: green plants, algae, cyanobacteria, chemosynthetic bacteria (at hydrothermal vents). Form the base of all food chains.
  • Primary Consumers (Herbivores) — eat producers directly. Examples: grasshoppers, rabbits, deer, caterpillar, cow. Occupy 2nd trophic level.
  • Secondary Consumers (Primary carnivores) — eat herbivores. Examples: frogs, small fish, fox. Occupy 3rd trophic level.
  • Tertiary Consumers (Secondary carnivores) — eat primary carnivores. Examples: eagle, lion, large fish. Occupy 4th trophic level.
  • Decomposers (Saprotrophes/Reducers) — break down dead organic matter (detritus) into inorganic substances, returning nutrients to the soil. Examples: bacteria, fungi (mushrooms, moulds). CRITICAL for nutrient recycling. WITHOUT decomposers, nutrients would remain locked in dead organic matter.
Abiotic Components
  • Physical factors: Temperature, light (intensity, duration, quality), water/moisture, humidity, wind, fire
  • Chemical factors: Soil minerals (N, P, K, Ca, Mg), atmospheric gases (O₂, CO₂, N₂), pH, salinity
  • Climatic factors: Rainfall pattern, seasonality, altitude, latitude
  • Limiting factors (Liebig's Law of the Minimum): the factor present in shortest supply relative to requirement limits growth — e.g., phosphorus often limits lake productivity
Section 2 — Food Chains, Food Webs & Ecological Pyramids
🍃
Food Chains, Food Webs & Trophic Levels
Energy flow, 10% law, grazing vs detritus food chains
▾

A food chain is a linear sequence showing the flow of energy from one organism to the next. Energy flows unidirectionally — from producers to consumers — and is never recycled. Matter (nutrients), by contrast, is recycled through biogeochemical cycles.

10% Law of Energy Transfer — Lindeman (1942)

Proposed by Raymond Lindeman (1942): only about 10% of the energy available at one trophic level is transferred to the next trophic level. The remaining ~90% is lost as heat (respiration), used for metabolism, movement, or lost as excretory products. This is why food chains are short (rarely more than 4–5 trophic levels) and why it is more energy-efficient for humans to eat plants (grain) than meat. Example: 1000 kcal in grass → 100 kcal in grasshopper → 10 kcal in frog → 1 kcal in snake → 0.1 kcal in hawk.

Food Web — Key Points
  • A food web is an interconnected network of food chains in an ecosystem — more realistic than a single food chain
  • Food webs provide stability — if one species disappears, energy can flow through alternative pathways
  • Keystone species — a species that has a disproportionately large effect on ecosystem structure relative to its abundance. Removal causes ecosystem collapse. Example: Sea otter (controls sea urchin populations → prevents overgrazing of kelp forests); wolves in Yellowstone (trophic cascade)
  • Energy flows unidirectionally (one way) in a food chain — NEVER recycled. Matter is recycled; energy is not.
  • The trophic level of an organism = its position in a food chain. Producers = T1; Herbivores = T2; Primary carnivores = T3; etc.
Ecological Pyramids — Three Types
  • Pyramid of Numbers — shows number of organisms at each trophic level. Can be upright (grassland), inverted (tree ecosystem: one tree supports many insects) or irregular (parasitic chains). Devised by Charles Elton (1927) — "Eltonian pyramid"
  • Pyramid of Biomass — shows total dry weight (biomass) of organisms at each trophic level. Usually upright in terrestrial ecosystems (more plant biomass than herbivore biomass). Inverted in aquatic (ocean) ecosystems — phytoplankton (tiny, reproduce fast) supports a larger biomass of zooplankton + fish above them at any given moment
  • Pyramid of Energy — shows total energy at each trophic level per unit area per unit time. ALWAYS UPRIGHT — energy always decreases at each successive level (10% law); can never be inverted. This is the most accurate and fundamental pyramid.
⚑ Food Chain & Pyramid Exam Traps
  • Pyramid of Energy is ALWAYS UPRIGHT — it can NEVER be inverted. This is the most tested fact about ecological pyramids.
  • Pyramid of Biomass is INVERTED in aquatic/ocean ecosystems — phytoplankton have low standing biomass but very high productivity rate.
  • Energy flows unidirectionally; matter/nutrients are recycled. This is a classic trick question.
  • The 10% Law was proposed by Raymond Lindeman (1942) — NOT by Haeckel or Tansley.
  • Decomposers are NOT included in food chains but ARE part of food webs and play the critical role of nutrient recycling.
Section 3 — Biogeochemical Cycles
♻️
Biogeochemical Cycles — Carbon, Nitrogen, Water, Phosphorus
How matter cycles through biotic and abiotic components
▾

Biogeochemical cycles describe the movement of chemical elements between the biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) components of the ecosystem. Unlike energy, matter is cycled and recycled. Key cycles: Carbon, Nitrogen, Water (Hydrological), Oxygen, Phosphorus, Sulphur.

Carbon Cycle — Key Processes
  • Photosynthesis: CO₂ + H₂O → glucose + O₂ (removes CO₂ from atmosphere — carbon fixation by plants/algae)
  • Respiration: Glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy (releases CO₂ to atmosphere — all living organisms)
  • Decomposition: Decomposers break down dead organic matter → releases CO₂ and nutrients back
  • Combustion: Burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) and biomass releases stored CO₂ — major source of anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions
  • Ocean absorption: Oceans absorb ~30% of anthropogenic CO₂ (form bicarbonates/carbonates) — ocean acidification is a side effect
  • Sedimentation/Fossilisation: Organic matter buried → compressed over millions of years → fossil fuels (coal, petroleum, natural gas)
Nitrogen Cycle — Most Complex Biogeochemical Cycle
  • Nitrogen Fixation: Conversion of atmospheric N₂ (inert) into ammonia (NH₃) or nitrates usable by plants. Done by: (a) Biological fixation — Rhizobium (symbiotic in legume root nodules), Azotobacter, Clostridium, Nostoc, Anabaena (free-living); (b) Industrial fixation — Haber-Bosch process (N₂ + H₂ → NH₃); (c) Atmospheric fixation — lightning
  • Nitrification: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ (by Nitrosomonas) → NO₃⁻ (by Nitrobacter) — converts ammonia to nitrates that plants absorb
  • Assimilation: Plants absorb NO₃⁻/NH₄⁺ from soil → incorporated into amino acids, proteins, nucleic acids → passed to animals through food chains
  • Ammonification: Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) break down proteins/urea from dead organisms and excreta → NH₃ / NH₄⁺ (ammonia) — returns N to soil
  • Denitrification: Pseudomonas, Thiobacillus convert NO₃⁻ → N₂ (returns nitrogen gas to atmosphere) — completes the cycle
Water (Hydrological) Cycle
  • Evaporation: Water from oceans/lakes/rivers → water vapour (driven by solar energy); oceans = 85% of evaporation
  • Transpiration: Water loss from plant leaves → atmosphere; evapotranspiration = evaporation + transpiration combined
  • Condensation: Water vapour cools → liquid droplets → clouds
  • Precipitation: Rain, snow, sleet, hail → returns water to land and oceans
  • Runoff & Infiltration: Surface runoff → rivers → oceans; infiltration → groundwater recharge
  • The Sun drives the water cycle (energy source); gravity drives precipitation and runoff
Key Nitrogen-Fixing Bacteria — Exam List
  • Rhizobium — symbiotic in root nodules of LEGUMES (peas, beans, soyabean, groundnut, clover) — most important for agriculture
  • Azotobacter — free-living in aerobic soil
  • Clostridium — free-living in anaerobic soil
  • Nostoc and Anabaena — cyanobacteria (blue-green algae); free-living in soil and water; Anabaena is symbiotic in Azolla (water fern used as green manure in rice fields)
  • Frankia — symbiotic in non-legume plant Alnus (alder tree)
Phosphorus Cycle — Sedimentary Cycle (No Gaseous Phase)

Phosphorus is unique — its cycle has NO gaseous phase (unlike C, N, O, S cycles). Phosphorus moves from rocks (lithosphere) → soil water → plants → animals → decomposers → back to soil. Weathering of phosphate rocks releases phosphate ions (PO₄³⁻) → absorbed by plant roots → consumed by animals → returned by decomposition. Phosphorus is often the limiting nutrient in freshwater ecosystems (causes algal blooms / eutrophication when in excess from agricultural runoff).

Section 4 — Biodiversity & Conservation
🦋
Biodiversity — Types, Importance & Threats
Alpha, beta, gamma diversity; hotspots; IUCN categories; conservation strategies
▾

Biodiversity (Biological Diversity) refers to the variety of life on Earth — at all levels from genes to ecosystems. The term was popularised by E.O. Wilson in the 1980s. India is a megadiverse country — one of 17 recognised globally.

Three Levels of Biodiversity
  • Genetic Diversity — variation in genes within a species or population. Example: different varieties of rice (Oryza sativa); different breeds of dogs. Basis of evolution and adaptation. Measured by allele frequency differences.
  • Species Diversity (Alpha diversity) — number and relative abundance of species in a defined area. Measured by species richness (number of species) and evenness (how evenly individuals are distributed). Tropical regions have highest species diversity.
  • Ecosystem (Ecological) Diversity — variety of habitats, communities and ecological processes in a region. India has diverse ecosystems: deserts, rainforests, mangroves, coral reefs, wetlands, grasslands, alpine meadows.
Biodiversity Hotspots — India
  • A biodiversity hotspot is a region with exceptionally high species richness AND endemism (species found nowhere else), but that has already lost >70% of its original habitat. Concept proposed by Norman Myers (1988)
  • Globally: 36 biodiversity hotspots (as of 2023), covering ~2.5% of Earth's land surface but harbouring >60% of the world's plant, reptile, bird and amphibian species
  • India's hotspots: (1) Western Ghats & Sri Lanka (2) Eastern Himalayas (Indo-Burma) (3) Sundaland (Nicobar Islands) (4) Western Ghats are often split as a separate India-specific hotspot in exam contexts
  • India has ~7–8% of world's biodiversity on ~2.4% of world's land area
IUCN Red List Categories (Threat Status)
  • Extinct (EX) — no individual known to survive anywhere. Ex: Dodo (Raphus cucullatus), Passenger pigeon, Pyrenean ibex
  • Extinct in the Wild (EW) — survives only in captivity. Ex: Hawaiian crow (Corvus hawaiiensis)
  • Critically Endangered (CR) — extremely high risk. Ex: Indian Vulture, Sumatran orangutan, Amur leopard
  • Endangered (EN) — high risk. Ex: Bengal tiger, Snow leopard, Asiatic lion, Ganges river dolphin
  • Vulnerable (VU) — facing high risk. Ex: Indian elephant, Great white shark, Polar bear, Hippopotamus
  • Near Threatened (NT) — close to qualifying for threatened category. Ex: Striped hyena
  • Least Concern (LC) — widespread and abundant. Ex: House sparrow, Common crow

In situ ConservationConservation of species in their natural habitat (on-site)National parks (no human activity allowed), Wildlife sanctuaries (some human activity), Biosphere reserves, Sacred groves (Dev vans)

India — Protected Area Network
  • India has 106 National Parks, 567 Wildlife Sanctuaries and 18 Biosphere Reserves (as of 2024)
  • Notable National Parks: Jim Corbett (first NP in India, 1936, Uttarakhand); Kaziranga (one-horned rhino, Assam); Ranthambore (tigers, Rajasthan); Gir (Asiatic lions, Gujarat); Sundarbans (Royal Bengal tigers, mangroves, West Bengal)
  • World Heritage Sites (Natural): Kaziranga, Manas, Keoladeo Ghana (Bharatpur), Sundarbans, Western Ghats, Great Himalayan NP
  • Biosphere Reserve: large multi-use protected area with core zone (strict), buffer zone and transition zone. Examples: Nilgiri (first in India, 1986), Nanda Devi, Gulf of Mannar
Causes of Biodiversity Loss — HIPPO
  • Habitat loss, degradation and fragmentation — LARGEST cause of species extinction globally (deforestation, urbanisation, agriculture)
  • Invasive alien species — non-native species introduced to new ecosystems that outcompete natives. Examples: Water hyacinth (Eichhornia crassipes) in India's water bodies; Lantana camara in forests; Nile perch in Lake Victoria → wiped out 200+ native cichlid species
  • Pollution — air, water and soil pollution kill organisms and disrupt food chains. DDT caused eggshell thinning in raptors → near extinction of Peregrine falcon and Bald eagle
  • Population (human overexploitation) — overhunting, overfishing, poaching. Examples: Dodo hunted to extinction; Blue whale hunted to near extinction; Tiger and rhino poached for body parts
  • Over-exploitation — taking resources faster than they can regenerate (fishing, logging, bushmeat)
  • Climate Change — shifting temperature and rainfall patterns → habitat shifts, coral bleaching, polar ice melt → species cannot adapt fast enough
  • Co-extinction — when a species goes extinct, species dependent on it (parasites, mutualists, commensals) also go extinct. Example: extinction of a specific plant → extinction of its specialist pollinator
⚑ Biodiversity Exam Traps
  • Jim Corbett National Park was the first National Park in India, established in 1936 (originally called Hailey National Park). Do NOT confuse with Kaziranga (first UNESCO World Heritage Site among NPs).
  • In situ = in natural habitat (national parks, sanctuaries); Ex situ = outside natural habitat (zoos, seed banks). Both are equally important.
  • Water hyacinth (Eichhornia crassipes) is an invasive alien species, NOT a native Indian plant — it is native to South America.
  • The first Biosphere Reserve in India was Nilgiri (1986), NOT Jim Corbett or Kaziranga.
  • Co-extinction is often the FORGOTTEN cause of biodiversity loss in exams — remember: loss of one species causes chain reactions.
Section 5 — Environmental Pollution
🏭
Air, Water & Soil Pollution
Pollutants, effects, major disasters, air quality index
▾
Air Pollution — Key Pollutants & Effects
  • Carbon Monoxide (CO) — produced by incomplete combustion (vehicle exhaust, burning); colourless, odourless; binds haemoglobin (~240× stronger than O₂) → reduces O₂ carrying capacity → suffocation; primary pollutant
  • Sulphur Dioxide (SO₂) — burning of coal and oil (power plants, smelters); causes acid rain (SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃); irritates respiratory tract; London smog (1952) killed ~4,000–12,000 people
  • Nitrogen Oxides (NOₓ) — vehicle exhaust, power plants; involved in photochemical smog (Los Angeles type); causes acid rain (NO₂ + H₂O → HNO₃)
  • Particulate Matter (PM₂.₅ and PM₁₀) — tiny particles in air; penetrate deep into lungs; cause lung cancer, cardiovascular disease, COPD; major concern in Indian cities
  • Lead (Pb) — leaded petrol (phased out); paint; damages nervous system (neurotoxin) especially in children; causes learning disability
  • Ozone (O₃) — secondary pollutant in troposphere; formed by sunlight + NOₓ + VOCs → photochemical smog; irritates lungs; at stratospheric level, protective (absorbs UV-B)
  • Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) — paints, solvents, fuel vapours; precursor to ground-level ozone and smog
Water Pollution — Key Concepts
  • Eutrophication — excessive nutrient enrichment (N, P from agricultural runoff, sewage) → algal blooms → algae consume O₂ during decomposition → Biological Oxygen Demand (BOD) rises → fish die (dead zones). Cultural eutrophication = human-accelerated eutrophication
  • BOD (Biochemical Oxygen Demand) — amount of O₂ required by bacteria to decompose organic matter in water. High BOD = more polluted water. Pure water: BOD < 1 mg/L; highly polluted: BOD > 17 mg/L
  • Biomagnification (Biological magnification) — concentration of a pollutant increases at each successive trophic level. Example: DDT → plankton (low DDT) → small fish → large fish → fish-eating birds (osprey, eagle) — highest DDT concentration; caused eggshell thinning → reproductive failure → population crash
  • Mercury poisoning (Minamata disease) — industrial mercury discharge → Minamata Bay, Japan (1956); methylmercury concentrated in fish → neurological damage in humans eating fish
  • Arsenic poisoning — geogenic (natural) arsenic in groundwater in West Bengal and Bangladesh; cause of largest mass poisoning in history
  • Fluorosis — excess fluoride in drinking water → dental and skeletal fluorosis; endemic in Rajasthan, Gujarat, Andhra Pradesh
Soil Pollution — Causes & Effects
  • Pesticides and herbicides — DDT, BHC, Endosulfan; persist in soil (non-biodegradable) → enter food chains → biomagnification
  • Heavy metals — Cadmium (Cd), Lead (Pb), Mercury (Hg), Chromium (Cr) from industrial effluents; toxic to plants and animals; accumulate in soil
  • Itai-Itai disease — cadmium contamination from Zn-mining runoff in Japan's Jinzū River; caused painful brittle bones (Itai-Itai = "it hurts it hurts" in Japanese)
  • Solid waste — plastics, e-waste, municipal solid waste; microplastics now found in soil worldwide
  • Radioactive waste — nuclear power plants, mining; long half-lives; causes mutations, cancer
Major Environmental Disasters — Exam List

Bhopal Gas Tragedy (2–3 December 1984) — Union Carbide India Ltd plant released Methyl Isocyanate (MIC) gas; deadliest industrial disaster (~3,787 immediate deaths; 500,000+ affected); Bhopal, Madhya Pradesh, India. Chernobyl (1986) — nuclear reactor explosion; radioactive cesium and iodine released; Ukraine (then USSR). Fukushima (2011) — tsunami triggered nuclear meltdown; Japan. Exxon Valdez (1989) — oil tanker spill off Alaska; major marine pollution. London Smog (1952) — SO₂ + fog → deadly smog; ~4,000–12,000 deaths; led to Clean Air Act 1956 (UK).

Section 6 — Global Environmental Issues
🌡️
Climate Change, Ozone Depletion & Greenhouse Effect
Global warming, GHGs, ozone hole, acid rain, international agreements
▾
Greenhouse Effect & Global Warming
  • Greenhouse Effect: Short-wave solar radiation passes through atmosphere → absorbed by Earth's surface → re-emitted as long-wave infrared (heat) → greenhouse gases (GHGs) absorb this heat → warm the atmosphere. A natural greenhouse effect is essential for life (without it, Earth's average T would be –18°C instead of +15°C)
  • Enhanced Greenhouse Effect (Global Warming): Human activities increase GHG concentrations → trapping more heat → rising temperatures
  • Major Greenhouse Gases (by contribution to global warming): CO₂ (most by volume, ~60% of warming) > CH₄ (methane, ~20%) > N₂O (nitrous oxide) > O₃ (ground-level ozone) > CFCs (highest Global Warming Potential per molecule)
  • Water vapour is the most abundant natural GHG but is NOT directly anthropogenic
  • Global Warming Potential (GWP): CO₂ = 1 (reference); CH₄ = 25; N₂O = 298; SF₆ = ~23,500 (highest); CFCs = 4,000–10,000+
  • Consequences of global warming: sea level rise, more frequent extreme weather events, melting glaciers and ice caps, coral bleaching, species range shifts, agricultural disruption
Ozone Layer Depletion
  • The ozone layer is in the stratosphere (15–35 km altitude); absorbs harmful UV-B radiation (280–315 nm wavelength) from the Sun
  • Ozone hole first discovered over Antarctica in 1985 by British Antarctic Survey (Farman, Gardiner, Shanklin)
  • Culprits: CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons) — refrigerants (Freon), aerosol propellants, foam blowing agents — release Cl atoms that catalytically destroy O₃ (one Cl atom can destroy 100,000 ozone molecules). Also: Halons (fire extinguishers), N₂O, CH₄
  • Effects of increased UV-B: skin cancer (melanoma), cataracts, weakened immune system in humans; phytoplankton decline → disrupts marine food chains; crop damage
  • Montreal Protocol (1987) — international treaty to phase out CFC production and consumption; most successful international environmental treaty; CFCs being replaced by HFCs (hydrofluorocarbons) — but HFCs are potent GHGs (irony!)
Acid Rain
  • Acid rain = precipitation (rain, snow, fog) with pH < 5.6 (normal rain pH ~5.6 due to dissolved CO₂ forming carbonic acid)
  • Caused by: SO₂ (from coal combustion, smelters) and NOₓ (from vehicles, power plants) reacting with atmospheric water → sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄) and nitric acid (HNO₃)
  • Effects: acidification of lakes and rivers (kills fish; Sweden's lakes severely affected in 1970s–80s); leaches toxic metals (Al, Pb) from soil into water; damages forests (Black Forest in Germany); corrodes buildings and monuments (Taj Mahal — marble cancer from SO₂)
  • Taj Mahal is threatened by acid rain from Mathura oil refinery and Agra industrial units — marble (CaCO₃) + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ (gypsum) → powdering/yellowing
Key International Environmental Agreements
  • Stockholm Conference (1972) — first UN conference on human environment; established UNEP (United Nations Environment Programme); June 5 declared World Environment Day
  • Ramsar Convention (1971) — wetlands of international importance; India has 75+ Ramsar sites (2023); Chilika Lake (Odisha) and Keoladeo Ghana (Rajasthan) among first in India (1981)
  • CITES (1973) — Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species; regulates trade in wild flora and fauna; 3 appendices (I = total ban; II = controlled; III = specific country request)
  • Montreal Protocol (1987) — phase-out of CFCs to protect ozone layer; universally ratified
  • Kyoto Protocol (1997) — legally binding emission reduction targets for developed nations (Annex I countries); USA did not ratify; India and China excluded as developing nations
  • Paris Agreement (2015) — limit global temperature rise to <2°C (ideally 1.5°C) above pre-industrial levels; nationally determined contributions (NDCs); 196 parties
  • Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD, 1992) — Rio Earth Summit; Nagoya Protocol (2010) on access and benefit sharing of genetic resources

Section 7 — Ecological Succession & Biomes
🌱
Ecological Succession, Biomes & Population Ecology
Primary/secondary succession, major biomes, population growth models
▾
Ecological Succession
  • Ecological succession — the gradual and predictable directional change in the species composition and community structure of an ecosystem over time, until a stable climax community is reached
  • Pioneer species — first colonisers; hardy, stress-tolerant; e.g., lichens on bare rock, grasses after a fire. They modify the environment (soil building, shade) → allow subsequent species to establish
  • Primary succession — begins on bare, lifeless substrate that has never had life (bare rock, lava flows, sand dunes, glacial moraines). Very slow (100s–1000s of years). Pioneer species = lichens → mosses → grasses → shrubs → trees (forest)
  • Secondary succession — begins on a previously inhabited area after disturbance (fire, flood, agriculture). Faster than primary succession as soil already exists. Pioneer species = grasses, annual weeds → perennial plants → shrubs → forest
  • Climax community — the final, stable, self-sustaining community in equilibrium with the climate. Species composition changes slowly. Example: tropical rainforest in warm-humid regions; deciduous forest in temperate zones
  • Hydrarch succession — begins in water (pond, lake); ends with terrestrial (forest) climax over time as the water body fills up
  • Xerarch succession — begins on dry, xeric habitats (bare rock); ends with mesophytic forest climax

Population Growth Models

Two fundamental growth models: (1) Exponential (J-shaped) growth — unlimited resources; population grows without bound; N = N₀e^(rt); seen in bacteria in ideal conditions, invasive species entering new territory. (2) Logistic (S-shaped/sigmoid) growth — limited resources; growth slows as population approaches Carrying Capacity (K) — the maximum population an environment can support sustainably. Real populations show logistic growth. The logistic model was described by Verhulst (1838). Concept of r (intrinsic rate of natural increase) and K (carrying capacity) is key to population ecology.

Global Issues & International Agreements
  • Greenhouse gases: CO₂ (most by volume) < CH₄ < N₂O < CFCs (highest GWP per molecule)
  • Ozone layer in stratosphere; UV-B absorber; CFC destroys O₃; hole over Antarctica
  • Montreal Protocol (1987) — phase-out of CFCs; most successful enviro treaty
  • Stockholm 1972 — UNEP formed; World Environment Day = June 5
  • Ramsar Convention — wetland protection; India has 75+ Ramsar sites
  • Paris Agreement (2015) — limit warming to <2°C; NDCs from all nations
  • Kyoto Protocol (1997) — binding targets for developed countries only
Biomes & Succession
  • Tropical rainforest — highest biodiversity; multi-layered canopy; >200 cm rain
  • Tundra — permafrost; mosses/lichens/sedges; no trees; polar bear, caribou
  • Taiga — largest land biome; coniferous (spruce, fir, pine)
  • Primary succession — bare rock; very slow; lichens → moss → grass → forest
  • Secondary succession — disturbed soil; faster; grass → shrubs → forest
  • Climax community — final stable community; in equilibrium with climate
  • Carrying capacity (K) = max population environment can support; logistic (S-curve) growth
