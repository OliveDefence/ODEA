The Circulatory System
Heart anatomy, double circulation, blood vessels, blood composition, cardiac cycle and cardiovascular disorders — at CDS, NDA & AFCAT exam depth.
CDS NDA AFCAT Heart Blood Circulation

The circulatory system (cardiovascular system) transports oxygen, nutrients, hormones and waste products throughout the body. It consists of the heart (pump), blood vessels (pipes) and blood (fluid medium). Humans have a closed, double circulatory system — blood circulates through the heart twice per complete circuit.

Section 1 — The Heart
❤️
Heart — Structure & Chambers
Four chambers, valves and the cardiac wall layers
▾

The human heart is a muscular, hollow organ about the size of a closed fist, located in the thoracic cavity between the lungs (in the mediastinum), slightly tilted to the left. It is enclosed in a double-walled sac called the pericardium.

Three Layers of the Heart Wall
  • Pericardium — outermost double-walled protective sac; contains pericardial fluid to reduce friction
  • Myocardium — middle, thickest layer; cardiac muscle responsible for pumping action
  • Endocardium — innermost smooth lining of heart chambers and valves

Think of it this way

The heart is like a double pump side by side. The right side is a low-pressure pump sending blood to the nearby lungs. The left side is a high-pressure pump pushing blood all the way around the body — which is why its wall is much thicker.

⚑ Exam Trap

The left ventricle has the thickest wall — it must generate enough pressure to push blood to the entire body via the aorta. The right ventricle only pumps to the nearby lungs and has a thinner wall. Confusing left and right wall thickness is a classic mistake.

🚪
Heart Valves — The Four Gatekeepers
Preventing backflow and producing heart sounds
▾

Heart valves ensure one-way blood flow through the heart. They open and close passively in response to pressure differences. There are four valves:

ValveLocationAlso CalledFunction
Tricuspid valveBetween RA and RVRight atrioventricular (AV) valvePrevents backflow from RV → RA; has 3 cusps (leaflets)
Pulmonary valveBetween RV and pulmonary arteryPulmonary semilunar valvePrevents backflow from pulmonary artery → RV
Mitral valveBetween LA and LVBicuspid / Left AV valvePrevents backflow from LV → LA; has 2 cusps
Aortic valveBetween LV and aortaAortic semilunar valvePrevents backflow from aorta → LV
Heart Sounds — "Lub-Dub"

First heart sound (S1) — "Lub": caused by closure of the AV valves (tricuspid + mitral) at the start of ventricular contraction (systole). Second heart sound (S2) — "Dub": caused by closure of the semilunar valves (aortic + pulmonary) at the end of ventricular contraction (beginning of diastole). Heart murmur = abnormal sound caused by faulty valve or turbulent blood flow.

⚑ Exam Traps — Valves
  • Mitral (Bicuspid) = 2 cusps; Tricuspid = 3 cusps. Mitral is on the LEFT (Mitral → M → Memory: M for Mitral and L for Left).
  • Mitral valve prolapse and rheumatic fever commonly damage the mitral valve — most tested cardiac valve pathology.
  • "Lub" = AV valve closure. "Dub" = semilunar valve closure. This order is frequently tested.
Section 2 — Double Circulation
🔄
Pulmonary & Systemic Circulation
The two complete loops of blood flow
▾

In double circulation, blood passes through the heart twice in one complete circuit — once through the pulmonary loop and once through the systemic loop. This keeps oxygenated and deoxygenated blood completely separate.

Pulmonary Circulation — Right side of heart → Lungs → Left side
1
Right Ventricle
Pumps deoxygenated blood into the pulmonary artery (only artery carrying deoxygenated blood — exam trap!).
2
Lungs (Gas Exchange)
CO₂ released; O₂ absorbed → blood becomes oxygenated in pulmonary capillaries.
3
Left Atrium
Pulmonary veins (4 veins) return oxygenated blood to the left atrium (only veins carrying oxygenated blood — exam trap!).
Systemic Circulation — Left side of heart → Body → Right side
4
Left Ventricle
Pumps oxygenated blood into the aorta (largest artery) at high pressure to supply entire body.
5
Body Tissues
O₂ delivered to tissues; CO₂ and waste collected. Blood becomes deoxygenated in tissue capillaries.
6
Right Atrium
Superior vena cava (upper body) + inferior vena cava (lower body) drain deoxygenated blood back into right atrium.
⚑ The Most Tested Circulatory Traps
  • Pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood — arteries carry blood AWAY from the heart, not necessarily oxygenated blood.
  • Pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood — veins carry blood TOWARD the heart, not necessarily deoxygenated blood.
  • The portal vein is unique — it carries nutrient-rich blood from the intestines to the liver (not directly back to the heart). It is a vein between two capillary beds.
  • The hepatic artery supplies oxygenated blood to the liver; the hepatic portal vein brings nutrient-rich blood from gut.
Types of Circulation in Animals
  • Open circulation — blood flows into open spaces (haemocoel), not enclosed vessels. Examples: insects, crustaceans, molluscs (except cephalopods)
  • Closed circulation — blood always remains in blood vessels. Examples: all vertebrates, earthworms, cephalopods (octopus, squid)
  • Single circulation — blood passes through heart once per circuit. Example: fish (1 atrium + 1 ventricle)
  • Double circulation — blood passes through heart twice. Example: all mammals, birds (complete double); amphibians and most reptiles (incomplete double — 3 chambers)
Section 3 — Cardiac Cycle & ECG
📈
Cardiac Cycle — Systole & Diastole
One complete heartbeat — timings and pressures
▾

The cardiac cycle is one complete sequence of contraction and relaxation of the heart. At a normal heart rate of 72 beats/min, each cycle lasts about 0.8 seconds.

PhaseEventDurationValves
Atrial SystoleAtria contract → blood pushed into ventricles0.1 secAV valves open; semilunar closed
Ventricular SystoleVentricles contract → blood ejected into arteries0.3 secAV valves close (S1 — Lub); semilunar open
Joint DiastoleAll chambers relax; heart fills passively0.4 secSemilunar valves close (S2 — Dub); AV valves open
Key Cardiac Numbers
  • Normal heart rate: 70–72 beats per minute
  • Stroke Volume (SV): volume of blood ejected per beat ≈ 70 mL
  • Cardiac Output (CO) = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume = 72 × 70 ≈ 5 litres/min
  • Blood pressure (normal): systolic 120 mmHg / diastolic 80 mmHg (written 120/80)
  • Systolic pressure: peak pressure during ventricular contraction
  • Diastolic pressure: lowest pressure during ventricular relaxation
  • Pulse pressure = Systolic − Diastolic = 120 − 80 = 40 mmHg
ECG — Electrocardiogram Waves

An ECG records the electrical activity of the heart. Key waves: P wave — atrial depolarisation (atria contract). QRS complex — ventricular depolarisation (ventricles contract — systole). T wave — ventricular repolarisation (ventricles relax — diastole). PR interval — AV node conduction time. A normal ECG confirms proper rhythm, rate and conduction.

⚑ Exam Trap

The QRS complex represents ventricular depolarisation (contraction), NOT atrial. Atrial repolarisation is hidden within the QRS complex and not seen separately. The T wave = ventricular repolarisation (relaxation). Getting P, QRS and T wave events confused is one of the most common exam errors.

⚡
Conduction System of the Heart
SA node, AV node, Bundle of His and Purkinje fibres
▾

The heart generates its own electrical impulse — it is myogenic (the impulse originates in the heart muscle itself, not from nerves).

1
SA Node — Sinoatrial Node
Located in the wall of the right atrium. The "pacemaker" of the heart — generates 70–75 impulses/min. Sets the normal heart rhythm (sinus rhythm). Impulse spreads across both atria → atrial contraction.
2
AV Node — Atrioventricular Node
Located at the junction of atria and ventricles. Receives impulse from SA node; delays it by ~0.1 sec to allow atria to finish contracting before ventricles fire.
3
Bundle of His & Bundle Branches
Impulse travels down the interventricular septum via the Bundle of His (AV bundle), splits into left and right bundle branches.
4
Purkinje Fibres
Rapidly conduct impulse throughout the ventricular myocardium → ventricles contract from apex upward, efficiently ejecting blood into arteries.
⚑ Exam Trap

The heart is myogenic — it can beat without any nervous input (proven by isolated heart preparations). The SA node spontaneously generates electrical impulses. An artificial pacemaker is implanted when the SA node fails. If only the AV node functions, the heart beats at ~40–50 bpm (slower than SA node's 70–75 bpm).

Named Major Blood Vessels to Know

  • Superior vena cava — drains upper body (head, arms) into right atrium

  • Hepatic portal vein — carries nutrient-rich blood from intestines to liver
  • Renal artery/vein — supply and drain the kidneys
⚑ Exam Trap — Veins Have Valves, Arteries Don't

Veins carry blood at low pressure against gravity (from legs to heart). Their valves prevent backflow. When these valves fail → varicose veins. Arteries have no valves (except at the heart outlet) because their high pressure keeps blood moving forward. Capillaries have no valves — they are the site of exchange.

Section 5 — Blood: Composition & Functions
🩺
Blood — Components & Functions
Plasma, RBCs, WBCs and Platelets
▾

Blood is a fluid connective tissue (≈5–6 litres in adults) consisting of a liquid plasma (55%) and formed elements — RBCs, WBCs and Platelets (45%). The ratio of RBC volume to total blood volume is the haematocrit (normal ≈ 45%).

RBC (Erythrocytes) Red cells

Most numerous (~5 million/mm³). Biconcave disc, no nucleus (in mature human RBCs), no mitochondria. Contains haemoglobin. Life span: 120 days. Made in red bone marrow. Destroyed in spleen ("graveyard of RBCs").

WBC (Leukocytes) White cells

~5,000–10,000/mm³. Nucleated. Key defenders. Types: Neutrophils (most common, 60–70%), Lymphocytes (20–25%), Monocytes, Eosinophils, Basophils. Life span: days to years.

Platelets (Thrombocytes) Clotting

1.5–4 lakh/mm³. Smallest formed elements. No nucleus. Fragments of megakaryocytes. Essential for blood clotting (haemostasis). Life span: 5–10 days.

Plasma 55% of blood

Straw-coloured liquid. 90% water. Contains: proteins (albumin, globulins, fibrinogen), glucose, hormones, ions, urea, CO₂. Serum = plasma minus clotting factors.

Blood Clotting — Key Steps
  • Injury → platelet plug forms at wound site
  • Clotting cascade activated → prothrombin → thrombin (enzyme; needs Ca²⁺ and Vitamin K)
  • Fibrinogen (soluble) → Fibrin (insoluble threads) — forms the clot mesh trapping RBCs
  • Haemophilia — genetic disorder; deficiency of clotting factor VIII (Haemophilia A) or IX (Haemophilia B); blood fails to clot normally
  • Vitamin K is essential for synthesis of clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X)
⚑ Exam Traps — Blood
  • Mature human RBCs have NO nucleus and NO mitochondria — they produce energy by anaerobic glycolysis only.
  • Serum = plasma minus fibrinogen (and other clotting factors). Serum is what's left after blood has clotted.
  • RBCs live 120 days, not 30 or 60 days. They are destroyed in the spleen — the spleen is the "graveyard of RBCs."
  • Platelets are not complete cells — they are fragments of megakaryocytes (large bone marrow cells).
Section 6 — Blood Groups & Transfusion
🅰️
ABO Blood Group System
Antigens, antibodies and transfusion compatibility
▾

Blood groups are determined by the presence of antigens (agglutinogens) on the surface of RBCs and antibodies (agglutinins) in the plasma. The ABO system was discovered by Karl Landsteiner (Nobel Prize, 1930).

Rh Factor — Rhesus Blood Group
  • Rh factor (Rhesus antigen, mainly Rh D) — discovered in rhesus monkeys by Landsteiner & Wiener (1940)
  • Rh positive (Rh+): RBCs have Rh antigen (~80–85% of people in India)
  • Rh negative (Rh−): RBCs lack Rh antigen; no naturally occurring anti-Rh antibodies (unlike ABO)
  • Erythroblastosis foetalis (Haemolytic disease of newborn): occurs when Rh− mother carries Rh+ foetus. Mother produces anti-Rh antibodies after first pregnancy. In second Rh+ pregnancy, maternal antibodies cross placenta and destroy foetal RBCs → life-threatening anaemia in foetus.
  • Prevention: Anti-D immunoglobulin (Rh immunoglobulin) given to Rh− mother within 72 hours of delivery of Rh+ baby.
⚑ Exam Traps — Blood Groups
  • Group O = universal donor (no antigens to react). Group AB = universal recipient (no antibodies to react). These roles are for packed RBC transfusions; whole blood transfusion compatibility still follows ABO rules.
  • ABO antigens are inherited; determined by a single gene with three alleles (Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i). Iᴬ and Iᴮ are codominant; i is recessive.
  • Anti-Rh antibodies are NOT naturally present — they develop only after Rh− person is exposed to Rh+ blood (transfusion or pregnancy). This is why the first incompatible pregnancy is usually safe; the second is dangerous.
Section 7 — Lymphatic System & Cardiovascular Disorders
🌿
Lymphatic System
Lymph, lymph nodes and immune drainage
▾

The lymphatic system is a one-way drainage network that collects excess tissue fluid (interstitial fluid) and returns it to the bloodstream. The fluid within lymph vessels is called lymph.

Lymphatic System — Key Facts
  • Lymph is similar to plasma but has no RBCs and fewer proteins
  • Lymph flows in one direction — toward the heart — via the thoracic duct (largest lymph vessel; drains most of body) and right lymphatic duct
  • Lymph nodes — bean-shaped organs along lymph vessels; contain lymphocytes; filter lymph and trap pathogens → immune response
  • Lacteals — lymph capillaries in intestinal villi; absorb dietary fats as chylomicrons
  • Spleen — largest lymphoid organ; filters blood, destroys old RBCs, stores platelets, produces lymphocytes
  • Thymus — primary lymphoid organ; site of T-lymphocyte maturation; most active in childhood, involutes with age
  • Oedema — excess fluid accumulation in tissues when lymphatic drainage is blocked (e.g., filariasis/elephantiasis caused by Wuchereria bancrofti)

Sickle Cell Anaemia — Frequently Tested

Sickle cell anaemia is a genetic disorder caused by a point mutation in the beta-globin gene of haemoglobin. Normal HbA is replaced by HbS. Under low oxygen, HbS polymerises → RBCs become rigid sickle (crescent) shapes → block capillaries, cause pain crises, haemolytic anaemia. Heterozygotes (HbA/HbS) have sickle cell TRAIT — they are resistant to malaria, explaining why the mutation is common in malaria-endemic regions.
