Microorganisms — Bacteria, Viruses & Beyond
Bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, algae — their structure, diseases, beneficial roles, antibiotics, vaccines and immunity — at CDS, NDA & AFCAT exam depth.
CDS NDA AFCAT Bacteria Viruses Immunity

Microorganisms (microbes) are living organisms too small to be seen with the naked eye — typically <1 mm. They include bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, algae and prions. The study of microorganisms is microbiology, founded by Antonie van Leeuwenhoek (1670s — first to observe bacteria and protozoa using his hand-crafted microscope) and advanced by Louis Pasteur and Robert Koch. Microbes are both the cause of devastating diseases and essential partners in nutrient cycling, food production, and medicine.

⚑ Critical Exam Traps — Classification
  • Viruses are NOT cells — they are acellular and considered non-living outside a host cell. They have no metabolism, no ribosomes, and cannot reproduce independently.
  • Fungi cell walls are made of chitin (NOT cellulose like plants; NOT peptidoglycan like bacteria).
  • Bacteria cell walls are made of peptidoglycan (also called murein). This is the target of penicillin antibiotics.
  • Prions contain NO nucleic acid (no DNA, no RNA) — they are infectious proteins only. This makes them unique among all infectious agents.
  • Algae are NOT plants — they are classified in Kingdom Protista (or their own kingdoms in modern classification). However, they are photosynthetic like plants.

Key Bacterial Structures — Functions
  • Cell wall (peptidoglycan) — maintains shape and prevents osmotic lysis; target of penicillin/cephalosporins (inhibit cell wall synthesis)
  • Flagella — whip-like appendages for motility; composed of flagellin protein; Vibrio cholerae is highly motile via single polar flagellum
  • Pili (fimbriae) — hair-like projections for attachment to host cells and conjugation (gene transfer); important for virulence (e.g. Neisseria gonorrhoeae)
  • Plasmids — small circular extra-chromosomal DNA; carry antibiotic resistance genes; transferred by conjugation — key mechanism of antibiotic resistance spread
  • Endospores — dormant, highly resistant structures formed by Bacillus and Clostridium species; survive boiling, drying, UV radiation, disinfectants; destroyed only by autoclaving (121°C, 15 psi, 15 min). Examples: B. anthracis (anthrax), C. tetani (tetanus), C. botulinum (botulism)
  • Capsule — polysaccharide outer layer; protects against phagocytosis by host immune cells; virulence factor. Examples: Streptococcus pneumoniae (pneumonia), Bacillus anthracis
Gram Staining Procedure — Hans Christian Gram (1884)

Step 1: Apply crystal violet (primary stain) — all bacteria stain purple. Step 2: Add iodine (mordant) — fixes crystal violet. Step 3: Alcohol wash (decolouriser) — Gram+ve retain purple (thick wall); Gram−ve lose colour. Step 4: Safranin counterstain — Gram−ve pick up pink/red. Result: Gram+ve = purple; Gram−ve = pink. Clinical importance: determines which antibiotics to use.

Special Cases — Mycobacterium & Mycoplasma
  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis (TB) and M. leprae (leprosy) — Gram staining unreliable; use Ziehl-Neelsen (acid-fast) stain (stains red due to waxy mycolic acid cell wall). Hence called acid-fast bacteria.
  • Mycoplasma — the smallest known free-living bacterium; has NO cell wall at all; naturally resistant to penicillin (which targets cell walls); causes atypical pneumonia (walking pneumonia); treated with macrolides or tetracyclines.

⚑ Nobel Prizes Related to Bacteria — Direct Exam Questions
  • Robert Koch — Nobel 1905: Discovery of Mycobacterium tuberculosis (1882) and Vibrio cholerae (1883). Formulated Koch's Postulates — criteria to establish a microorganism as the cause of a disease.
  • Alexander Fleming — Nobel 1945 (shared with Florey & Chain): Discovery of penicillin in 1928 from the mould Penicillium notatum. Fleming noticed that the mould contaminating his Staphylococcus culture plates killed the bacteria around it.
  • Selman Waksman — Nobel 1952: Discovered streptomycin (1943), the first antibiotic effective against TB, from soil bacterium Streptomyces griseus. Coined the term “antibiotic.”
Section 3 — Viruses in Depth
🦠
Viral Structure, Classification & Replication
Capsid, envelope, DNA/RNA viruses, lytic vs lysogenic cycles
▾
Viral Structure — Components
  • Nucleic acid core — genetic material, either DNA or RNA (never both); single-stranded (ss) or double-stranded (ds)
  • Capsid — protein coat surrounding the nucleic acid; protects genetic material; composed of protein subunits called capsomeres; shapes: icosahedral (spherical-like), helical, or complex (e.g. bacteriophage T4)
  • Envelope — lipid bilayer membrane derived from host cell membrane; present in enveloped viruses (HIV, influenza, herpes, COVID-19); absent in non-enveloped viruses (polio, rotavirus, adenovirus)
  • Spikes/glycoproteins — project from envelope; used for host cell recognition and attachment. Examples: Haemagglutinin (H) and Neuraminidase (N) in influenza virus (hence H1N1, H3N2 subtypes); Spike protein in SARS-CoV-2
FeatureDNA VirusesRNA Viruses
Genetic materialDNA (ss or ds)RNA (ss or ds)
Mutation rateLower (DNA repair mechanisms)HIGHER (RNA polymerase lacks proofreading) → rapid evolution, vaccine challenges
ExamplesSmallpox (Variola), chickenpox (Varicella zoster), hepatitis B, herpes simplex, adenovirus, papillomavirus (HPV)HIV, influenza, polio, rabies, measles, mumps, rubella, dengue, COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2), hepatitis A & C, Ebola
Special class—Retroviruses (HIV) — RNA genome; use reverse transcriptase to make DNA from RNA (reverse of central dogma); Nobel 1975 (Baltimore & Temin for reverse transcriptase)
Bacteriophage Replication Cycles

Lytic cycle: Phage injects DNA → host cell machinery used to make phage copies → cell lyses (bursts) → new phages released. End result: host cell death.

Lysogenic cycle: Phage DNA integrates into host chromosome as a prophage → replicates silently with host DNA for many generations → triggered by stress into lytic cycle. End result: long-term viral persistence. Viral DNA integrated in host = prophage in bacteria; provirus in eukaryotes (e.g. HIV provirus in CD4+ T cells).

⚑ Viruses — Critical Exam Points
  • Viruses are NOT killed by antibiotics (antibiotics target bacterial cell walls, ribosomes, etc. — viruses have none). Antivirals target specific viral enzymes (e.g. oseltamivir/Tamiflu targets neuraminidase in influenza; AZT/zidovudine inhibits HIV reverse transcriptase).
  • Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV) — first virus to be discovered (Dmitri Ivanovsky, 1892; confirmed by Beijerinck, 1898). It is an RNA virus. Infects tobacco plants.
  • Viroids — smaller than viruses; consist of ONLY RNA (no protein coat); infect plants; e.g. Potato spindle tuber viroid. Not to be confused with viruses.
  • Bacteriophages (phages) — viruses that infect bacteria. The T4 phage is the classic example. Used in phage therapy as an alternative to antibiotics.
  • HIV destroys CD4+ T helper cells (not B cells or cytotoxic T cells), causing immunodeficiency. Normal CD4 count: 500–1500 cells/μL; AIDS diagnosed at <200 cells/μL.

Protozoan Diseases — Malaria & Beyond
Malaria life cycle, Plasmodium species, vectors, treatment
▾
Malaria — Most Important Protozoan Disease for Exams
  • Causative agent: Plasmodium species — 4 main species: P. falciparum (most dangerous; cerebral malaria; drug-resistant), P. vivax (most common globally; relapses from dormant hypnozoites in liver), P. malariae (quartan malaria — 72-hour cycle), P. ovale
  • Vector: Female Anopheles mosquito (male mosquitoes feed on nectar, NOT blood). Bites at night. Named mosquito genera for other diseases: Aedes = dengue, Zika, yellow fever, chikungunya; Culex = filariasis, Japanese encephalitis
  • Life cycle — two hosts: (1) In mosquito: sexual reproduction → sporozoites in salivary glands. (2) In human: sporozoites → liver (schizogony; no symptoms) → merozoites released → RBCs (asexual cycle; schizogony in RBCs → rupture → fever)
  • Fever pattern: Quotidian (daily) in P. falciparum; Tertian (every 48 h) in P. vivax/P. ovale; Quartan (every 72 h) in P. malariae
  • Diagnosis: Peripheral blood smear (gold standard); Rapid Diagnostic Tests (RDTs); PCR
  • Treatment: Chloroquine (sensitive strains); Artemisinin-based Combination Therapy (ACT) for resistant P. falciparum; Primaquine for liver-stage P. vivax (kills hypnozoites)
  • Ronald Ross (Nobel 1902): Proved that malaria is transmitted by Anopheles mosquito (1897, Secunderabad, India); first Indian Nobel Prize location

Section 5 — Antibiotics, Vaccines & Immunity

Antibiotics — Discovery, Mechanism & Resistance
Penicillin, streptomycin, modes of action, antibiotic resistance
▾

Antibiotics are chemical substances produced by microorganisms that kill or inhibit the growth of other microorganisms. The term was coined by Selman Waksman (1942). They work ONLY on bacteria, NOT viruses, fungi (antifungals needed) or parasites.

Antibiotic Resistance — Mechanisms & Why It Matters
  • Enzymatic inactivation: bacteria produce enzymes that destroy antibiotic. e.g. beta-lactamase enzymes destroy the beta-lactam ring of penicillin → MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus) produces extended-spectrum beta-lactamases.
  • Target modification: bacteria alter the antibiotic’s binding site (e.g. altered penicillin-binding proteins in MRSA).
  • Efflux pumps: bacteria pump antibiotics out of the cell before they can act.
  • Reduced permeability: Gram−ve bacteria reduce outer membrane permeability to block antibiotic entry.
  • Spread of resistance: Resistance genes on plasmids spread between bacteria by conjugation (horizontal gene transfer). This is why resistance spreads rapidly.
  • MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus) and NDMR TB (Non-Drug-Resistant Mycobacterium tuberculosis) are critical global health threats.
⚑ Antibiotic Targets — Most-Tested Facts
  • Penicillin → cell wall synthesis (peptidoglycan). Works on Gram+ve; poor on Gram−ve; useless on Mycoplasma (no cell wall) and viruses.
  • Streptomycin, Tetracycline → 30S ribosomal subunit. Chloramphenicol, Erythromycin, Linezolid → 50S ribosomal subunit. (Mnemonic: 30S = StrEpTomycin, TEtracycline = S-T-3-0)
  • Ciprofloxacin → DNA gyrase (topoisomerase II). Rifampicin → RNA polymerase.
  • Human cells have 80S ribosomes; bacteria have 70S (30S + 50S). Antibiotics targeting 70S ribosomes don’t affect human cells — this is the basis of selective toxicity.

Vaccines, Immunity & Louis Pasteur’s Contributions
Types of vaccines, active vs passive immunity, herd immunity, important vaccines
▾

Vaccination is the process of stimulating the immune system to produce immunity to a disease without causing the disease. Edward Jenner (1796) created the first vaccine (against smallpox, using cowpox). The word “vaccine” comes from vacca (Latin: cow). Louis Pasteur developed vaccines for cholera (chickens, 1879), anthrax (1881), and rabies (humans, 1885) — and generalised the germ theory of disease.

Types of Vaccines
  • Live Attenuated — weakened (attenuated) live pathogen; produces strong, long-lasting immunity; single dose often sufficient. Examples: OPV (Sabin polio), BCG (TB), MMR (measles/mumps/rubella), yellow fever, varicella. Risk: can revert to virulence in immunocompromised; not given in pregnancy.
  • Inactivated (Killed) — killed pathogen; safer but weaker immunity; requires booster doses. Examples: IPV (Salk polio), influenza (injected), rabies (IPV), hepatitis A, whole-cell pertussis.
  • Subunit / Component — only specific antigens (proteins/polysaccharides) of the pathogen; very safe; requires adjuvant. Examples: Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg), HPV vaccine (Gardasil — capsid proteins), acellular pertussis.
  • Toxoid — inactivated toxin; stimulates anti-toxin antibodies. Examples: Diphtheria toxoid, Tetanus toxoid (DPT vaccine).
  • mRNA Vaccines (newest) — mRNA encoding a viral protein (e.g. SARS-CoV-2 spike protein) injected; cells read mRNA and produce antigen → immune response. No live pathogen; cannot integrate into DNA; degraded rapidly. Examples: Pfizer-BioNTech (BNT162b2), Moderna (mRNA-1273) COVID-19 vaccines. Nobel 2023 — Katalin Karikó & Drew Weissman.
Herd Immunity — Concept for Exams

Herd immunity (community immunity) occurs when a sufficiently large proportion of a population is immune to an infection (through vaccination or prior infection) that the spread of the pathogen is blocked, indirectly protecting unvaccinated individuals. The herd immunity threshold (HIT) is the proportion of population that must be immune to stop transmission: HIT = 1 − (1/R₀), where R₀ is the basic reproduction number. For measles (R₀ ~15): HIT = ~93–95%. For COVID-19 (R₀ ~2–3): HIT = ~50–67%. For smallpox: global herd immunity via vaccination led to eradication by 1980.

Beneficial Roles of Microorganisms
Nitrogen fixation, fermentation, decomposition, industrial applications
▾
Nitrogen Fixation — Critical for Agriculture

Biological nitrogen fixation is the conversion of atmospheric N₂ (inert) to ammonia (NH₃) usable by plants. Only certain prokaryotes possess the enzyme nitrogenase.

  • Free-living (non-symbiotic): Azotobacter (aerobic, soil), Clostridium (anaerobic, soil), Anabaena and Nostoc (cyanobacteria, free-living; also in rice paddies)
  • Symbiotic: Rhizobium — lives in root nodules of legumes (peas, beans, peanuts, soybean); most efficient nitrogen fixer in agriculture; mutualistic relationship (plant provides carbohydrates; bacteria provide fixed nitrogen)
  • Anabaena-Azolla association: Cyanobacterium Anabaena azollae lives in water fern Azolla; used as biofertiliser in rice cultivation in Asia
  • Mycorrhizae: Fungal associations with plant roots that enhance phosphorus absorption; not nitrogen fixation but critical for plant nutrition
🍷 Fermentation — Food & Drink

Saccharomyces cerevisiae (yeast) — bread rising (CO₂), beer/wine (ethanol). Lactobacillus — curd/yogurt (lactic acid). Acetobacter — vinegar (acetic acid). Streptococcus thermophilus + Lactobacillus bulgaricus — yogurt. Aspergillus — soy sauce, sake.

⚗️ Industrial Microorganisms

Penicillium — penicillin antibiotic. Streptomyces — streptomycin, erythromycin, tetracycline. E. coli — recombinant insulin (Humulin), interferon. Saccharomyces — insulin, hepatitis B vaccine (recombinant). Aspergillus niger — citric acid production.

🌎 Decomposers & Nutrient Cycling

Bacteria and fungi decompose dead organic matter → release nutrients (carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus) back into ecosystem. Bacillus, Pseudomonas, Trichoderma. Sewage treatment uses bacteria to break down organic waste aerobically and anaerobically.

🌿 Biocontrol & Biofertilisers

Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) — produces Cry proteins that kill insect pests; used in Bt cotton and Bt brinjal. Trichoderma — fungal biocontrol agent against plant pathogens. Biofertilisers: Rhizobium, Azotobacter, Anabaena, mycorrhizae — reduce dependence on chemical fertilisers.

Sterilisation vs Disinfection — Key Distinctions
  • Sterilisation — complete destruction of ALL microorganisms including endospores. Methods: Autoclaving (121°C, 15 psi, 15 min — gold standard), dry heat oven (160°C, 2h), incineration, filtration (Seitz filter/membrane filter).
  • Disinfection — destruction of most pathogenic microorganisms from non-living surfaces (does NOT kill all spores). Agents: bleach (NaOCl), phenol, alcohol (70% ethanol), formaldehyde, glutaraldehyde.
  • Pasteurisation — mild heat treatment to reduce pathogen load without full sterilisation. HTST (72°C for 15 sec); UHT (135°C for 2 sec); developed by Louis Pasteur; used for milk, wine, beer.
  • Antiseptic — chemical applied to living tissue to prevent infection (e.g. iodine solution, hydrogen peroxide, Dettol). Joseph Lister (1867) introduced carbolic acid (phenol) as surgical antiseptic — founded antiseptic surgery.
Important WHO & India Health Programmes
  • Pulse Polio Programme: India — led to India being declared polio-free on 27 March 2014 by WHO
  • RNTCP/NTP: Revised National TB Control Programme — DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment Short-course)
  • National Vector Borne Disease Control Programme: Covers malaria, dengue, filariasis, kala-azar, Japanese encephalitis, chikungunya

Major Groups — Quick Identifiers
  • Bacteria: prokaryote; peptidoglycan cell wall; 1–10 μm; binary fission
  • Viruses: acellular; DNA or RNA + capsid; 20–300 nm; obligate intracellular parasites
  • Fungi: eukaryote; chitin cell wall; heterotrophic; spores
  • Protozoa: eukaryote; unicellular; heterotrophic; no cell wall; motile
  • Prions: protein only; NO nucleic acid; extremely resistant; BSE, CJD
  • Viroids: RNA only; no protein coat; infect plants only; e.g. potato spindle tuber viroid

  • Smallpox: DNA; eradicated 1980; Jenner’s cowpox vaccine 1796
  • Rabies: RNA; Rhabdovirus; 100% fatal after symptoms; Pasteur vaccine 1885
  • Dengue: RNA; Aedes mosquito; 4 serotypes; platelet count drops
  • Influenza: RNA; H and N antigens; H1N1 Spanish Flu 1918; antigenic shift/drift
  • COVID-19: SARS-CoV-2; RNA; ACE2 receptor; mRNA vaccines; Nobel 2023 (Karikó & Weissman)
  • TMV: first virus discovered (Ivanovsky 1892); RNA; infects tobacco
Antibiotics — Targets
  • Cell wall synthesis: Penicillin, Cephalosporins, Vancomycin
  • 30S ribosome: Streptomycin, Tetracycline, Aminoglycosides
  • 50S ribosome: Chloramphenicol, Erythromycin, Linezolid
  • DNA gyrase: Ciprofloxacin (fluoroquinolones)
  • RNA polymerase: Rifampicin (used in TB)
  • Penicillin source: Penicillium notatum; Fleming 1928; Nobel 1945
  • Streptomycin source: Streptomyces griseus; Waksman 1943; Nobel 1952
  • Mycoplasma: NO cell wall → naturally resistant to penicillin
Vaccines — Types & Examples
  • Live attenuated: BCG, OPV (Sabin), MMR, yellow fever, varicella — strong immunity; risk of reversion
  • Inactivated: IPV (Salk), inactivated flu, rabies — safe; need boosters
  • Subunit: Hepatitis B, HPV (Gardasil), acellular pertussis
  • Toxoid: Diphtheria toxoid, Tetanus toxoid (DPT)
  • mRNA: Pfizer & Moderna COVID-19; Nobel 2023
  • Passive immunity: anti-venom, anti-rabies Ig, tetanus antitoxin — immediate but short-lived
  • Herd immunity: for measles ~93–95%; depends on R₀
Protozoa, Vectors & Nobel Prizes
  • Malaria: Plasmodium; female Anopheles; Ronald Ross Nobel 1902; P. falciparum most dangerous; ACT treatment
  • Dengue/Zika/Chikungunya: Aedes aegypti mosquito
  • Filariasis: Culex mosquito; Wuchereria bancrofti
  • Sleeping sickness: Trypanosoma brucei; tsetse fly
  • Kala-azar: Leishmania donovani; sandfly; endemic Bihar
  • Koch Nobel 1905: TB + cholera discovery; Koch’s Postulates
  • HIV Nobel 2008: Montagnier & Barre-Sinoussi
  • mRNA Nobel 2023: Karikó & Weissman
