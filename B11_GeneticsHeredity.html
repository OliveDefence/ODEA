Genetics & Heredity
Mendel's laws, monohybrid & dihybrid crosses, DNA structure, mutations, sex-linked inheritance, blood groups, chromosomal disorders and molecular genetics — at CDS, NDA & AFCAT exam depth.
CDS NDA AFCAT Mendel DNA Inheritance

Genetics is the science of heredity and variation — how traits are passed from parents to offspring. Gregor Johann Mendel (1822–1884), an Augustinian monk working with garden peas (Pisum sativum), discovered the fundamental laws of inheritance through meticulous experimentation. His work, published in 1866, was rediscovered in 1900 and forms the foundation of modern genetics.

Section 1 — Mendel's Experiments & Laws
🌿
Why Mendel Succeeded — His Experimental Design
Choice of organism, seven traits and true-breeding lines
▾

Mendel chose Pisum sativum (garden pea) for excellent reasons: short generation time, easy cultivation, naturally self-fertilising (could control crosses), large number of offspring (good statistics), and crucially — seven pairs of contrasting traits that happened to be on different chromosomes (independent assortment).

Mendel's Seven Traits in Pea Plants
  • Seed shape — Round (dominant) vs Wrinkled (recessive)
  • Seed colour — Yellow (dominant) vs Green (recessive)
  • Seed coat colour — Grey/coloured (dominant) vs White (recessive)
  • Pod shape — Inflated (dominant) vs Constricted (recessive)
  • Pod colour — Green (dominant) vs Yellow (recessive)
  • Flower position — Axial/along stem (dominant) vs Terminal/at tip (recessive)
  • Plant height — Tall (dominant) vs Dwarf/short (recessive)
Key Genetic Terminology

Gene — unit of heredity; segment of DNA coding for a trait. Allele — alternative forms of a gene (e.g., T for tall, t for dwarf). Locus — specific position of a gene on a chromosome. Homozygous — both alleles identical (TT or tt). Heterozygous — two different alleles (Tt). Dominant — allele expressed in heterozygote (T). Recessive — allele masked in heterozygote, expressed only in homozygous state (tt). Genotype — genetic makeup (TT, Tt, tt). Phenotype — observable/expressed trait (Tall or Dwarf). F₁ — first filial generation. F₂ — second filial generation. P — parental generation.

⚖️
Law of Segregation — Monohybrid Cross
Mendel's First Law — 3:1 phenotypic ratio in F₂
▾

Law of Segregation (Mendel's First Law): During gamete formation, the two alleles of a gene separate (segregate) so that each gamete receives only one allele. The alleles reunite randomly at fertilisation.

Think of it this way

Imagine you have a pair of socks — one red, one blue. When you "make gametes," you can only put ONE sock in each bag. Each gamete carries only one allele, never a pair. At fertilisation, two gametes combine → the offspring gets a pair again.

Cross: Tall (TT) × Dwarf (tt)

P generation: TT × tt → all F₁ gametes are T from tall parent and t from dwarf parent → F₁ = all Tt (Tall) — dominant trait expressed.

⚑ Test Cross — Detecting Hidden Recessives

A test cross crosses an individual of unknown genotype (tall — either TT or Tt) with a homozygous recessive (tt). If all offspring are Tall → parent was TT. If offspring are 50% Tall : 50% Dwarf → parent was Tt. Test crosses are fundamental to breeding programmes and genetics experiments.

🔀
Law of Independent Assortment — Dihybrid Cross
Mendel's Second Law — 9:3:3:1 ratio in F₂
▾

Law of Independent Assortment (Mendel's Second Law): Genes for different traits assort independently of one another during gamete formation — provided they are on different chromosomes (non-linked).

Cross: Round Yellow (RRYY) × Wrinkled Green (rryy)

F₁ = all RrYy (Round Yellow) — both dominant traits expressed. F₁ × F₁ → F₂ has 16 combinations (4 gamete types × 4 gamete types).

9Round Yellow (R_Y_)
3Round Green (R_yy)
3Wrinkled Yellow (rrY_)
1Wrinkled Green (rryy)
F₁ Dihybrid Gametes — 4 Types (FOIL method)
  • RY — Round Yellow (9/16)
  • Ry — Round Green (3/16)
  • rY — Wrinkled Yellow (3/16)
  • ry — Wrinkled Green (1/16)
⚑ When Independent Assortment FAILS — Linkage
  • Mendel's second law only applies to genes on different chromosomes (or far apart on the same chromosome).
  • When two genes are on the same chromosome, they are linked and tend to be inherited together — they do NOT assort independently.
  • Thomas Hunt Morgan discovered gene linkage and sex-linked inheritance using Drosophila melanogaster (fruit fly) — Nobel Prize 1933.
  • Crossing over (recombination) during meiosis I can separate linked genes → recombinant offspring.

⚑ Incomplete Dominance vs Codominance — Critical Distinction
  • Incomplete dominance — blending; heterozygote has intermediate phenotype (pink flower = blend of red and white pigment). Pink is a NEW phenotype not seen in either parent.
  • Codominance — BOTH parental phenotypes expressed simultaneously; no blending. Blood group AB expresses BOTH A AND B antigens — not some new intermediate antigen. A roan horse has both red and white hairs (not pink hairs).

DNA — Structure, Replication & the Genetic Code
Watson-Crick double helix, base pairing and central dogma
▾

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) is the hereditary material in most organisms. Its structure was elucidated by James Watson and Francis Crick in 1953 (using X-ray crystallography data from Rosalind Franklin). Nobel Prize 1962: Watson, Crick and Maurice Wilkins.

DNA Structure — Key Points
  • Double helix — two antiparallel strands wound around each other; backbone = deoxyribose sugar + phosphate; rungs = nitrogenous base pairs
  • Nitrogenous bases: Purines (Adenine, Guanine — double ring); Pyrimidines (Thymine, Cytosine — single ring)
  • Chargaff's Rules: A always pairs with T (2 hydrogen bonds); G always pairs with C (3 hydrogen bonds). Therefore: %A = %T and %G = %C in any double-stranded DNA
  • Antiparallel: one strand runs 5'→3', the complementary strand runs 3'→5'
  • RNA vs DNA: RNA uses Uracil (U) instead of Thymine (T); RNA has ribose sugar (not deoxyribose); RNA is usually single-stranded
Central Dogma of Molecular Biology (Francis Crick, 1958)

DNA → RNA → Protein

Transcription: DNA used as template to make mRNA (messenger RNA); occurs in nucleus; enzyme = RNA polymerase. Translation: mRNA decoded by ribosomes in cytoplasm to assemble amino acids into protein; tRNA brings amino acids; codon (3 bases on mRNA) = 1 amino acid. Reverse Transcription: RNA → DNA; done by reverse transcriptase enzyme (in retroviruses like HIV). Note: protein sequence CANNOT be reverse-translated back to DNA — information flow is one-way in normal cells.

Genetic Code — Key Facts
  • A codon = 3 nucleotides on mRNA; codes for one amino acid
  • Total codons: 4³ = 64 codons; only 20 amino acids → code is degenerate (multiple codons per amino acid)
  • Start codon: AUG (codes for Methionine — starts translation)
  • Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA (terminate translation; no amino acid)
  • Code is universal — same codons code for same amino acids in virtually all organisms (from bacteria to humans)
  • Code is non-overlapping and comma-free — read sequentially without gaps
⚑ DNA Replication — Semi-Conservative
  • DNA replication is semi-conservative — each new DNA molecule contains one original (template) strand and one newly synthesised strand. Proved by Meselson & Stahl (1958) using ¹⁵N/¹⁴N labelling in E. coli.
  • Enzyme: DNA polymerase synthesises new strand; can only add nucleotides in 5'→3' direction. Also requires a short RNA primer.
  • Helicase unwinds the double helix; ligase joins Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand.
Section 4 — Sex-Linked Inheritance
🔗
Sex Chromosomes & X-Linked Disorders
Colour blindness, haemophilia and why males are more affected
▾

Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs): 22 pairs of autosomes and 1 pair of sex chromosomes. Females = XX; Males = XY. The Y chromosome is much smaller and carries very few genes (mainly SRY). Genes on the X chromosome that have no corresponding allele on Y are called X-linked.

Why Males are More Affected by X-linked Recessive Disorders

Females (XX) have two copies of every X-linked gene — if one X has a faulty allele, the healthy allele on the other X can compensate (carrier female). Males (XY) have only one X — if that X carries the faulty allele, there is no back-up → disease expressed. Males are called hemizygous for X-linked genes.

Carrier Female Cross — Colour Blindness

Carrier mother (X^N X^cb) × Normal father (X^N Y):

X^NY
X^NX^N X^N (Normal female)X^N Y (Normal male)
X^cbX^N X^cb (Carrier female)X^cb Y (Colour blind male)

Result: 50% sons affected; daughters not affected but 50% are carriers. A colour-blind daughter can only result if the father is colour blind AND the mother is at least a carrier.

⚑ X-Linked Inheritance Exam Traps
  • Affected father CANNOT pass X-linked recessive disorder to his sons (sons get Y from father, not X). He passes the X^cb to ALL daughters (who become carriers).
  • An affected male gets the disorder from his carrier mother (who is phenotypically normal).
  • A colour-blind female must have been born to a colour-blind father AND a carrier/affected mother — both X chromosomes must carry the cb allele.
  • Y-linked (holandric) inheritance: only males affected; passed from father to ALL sons (e.g., hairy pinna in some populations).

ABO Genetics — Codominance & Multiple Alleles
  • ABO is controlled by a single gene with three alleles: I^A, I^B and i (multiple allelism)
  • I^A and I^B are codominant — both expressed equally in AB individuals
  • i is recessive to both I^A and I^B; only expressed in homozygous state (ii = Group O)
  • Discovered by Karl Landsteiner — Nobel Prize 1930
  • Group O (ii) = Universal Donor for packed RBCs (no antigens to cause reaction), but O plasma contains BOTH anti-A and anti-B antibodies
  • Group AB = Universal Recipient (no antibodies in plasma to attack donor's RBCs)
Rh Blood Group System

The Rh factor (Rhesus factor) is a separate antigen on RBCs, controlled by the RHD gene. Rh+ (Rh positive) — RhD antigen present (dominant, ~85% of people). Rh− (Rh negative) — no RhD antigen (recessive, ~15%). Unlike ABO, Rh− individuals do not naturally have anti-Rh antibodies — they develop them only after exposure to Rh+ blood. Critical in pregnancy: Erythroblastosis Foetalis (Haemolytic Disease of the Newborn) — Rh− mother + Rh+ foetus: first pregnancy usually safe (initial sensitisation only); second Rh+ pregnancy → maternal anti-Rh IgG crosses placenta → attacks foetal RBCs → severe haemolytic anaemia, jaundice, even death. Prevented by administering Anti-D immunoglobulin (RhoGAM) to Rh− mother within 72 hours of delivery.

⚑ Blood Group Exam Traps
  • Blood group O is the Universal Donor for packed RBCs but NOT for whole blood or plasma (O plasma has both antibodies).
  • AB is the Universal Recipient for RBCs but AB plasma is universal donor (no antibodies).
  • Blood group is determined by antigens on RBCs — NOT antibodies in plasma.
  • A child with blood group O (ii) cannot have a parent who is I^A I^A (pure AA) or I^B I^B (pure BB) — at least one parent must carry the 'i' allele.
Section 6 — Chromosomal & Gene Mutations
⚠️
Mutations — Gene & Chromosomal Disorders
Down's syndrome, Turner's, Klinefelter's and point mutations
▾

A mutation is a heritable change in the DNA sequence or chromosome number/structure. Mutations can be spontaneous or caused by mutagens (UV radiation, X-rays, chemicals like benzene).

Types of Gene (Point) Mutations
  • Substitution — one base replaced by another. If in coding region → may change amino acid (missense mutation) or create stop codon (nonsense mutation). Example: Sickle Cell Anaemia — single base change in codon 6 of beta-globin gene (GAG → GTG) → glutamic acid replaced by valine → HbS (sickle haemoglobin)
  • Insertion — extra base(s) added → shifts reading frame (frameshift mutation) → completely altered protein
  • Deletion — base(s) removed → also causes frameshift mutation
  • Silent mutation — base change does NOT alter amino acid (due to degeneracy of genetic code) → no effect on protein

Sickle Cell Anaemia — Genetics & Biology
  • Caused by point mutation in the HBB gene (beta-globin): GAG → GUG (codon 6); glutamic acid → valine
  • Autosomal recessive: heterozygotes (HbA/HbS) are carriers — have sickle cell trait (largely normal but partial protection against malaria)
  • Homozygous affected (HbS/HbS): RBCs sickle in low O₂ → block capillaries → pain crises, organ damage, stroke, anaemia
  • High frequency in malaria-endemic regions (Africa, India) — balanced polymorphism: heterozygotes have selective advantage against Plasmodium falciparum
⚑ Non-Disjunction — Chromosomal Number Errors
  • Non-disjunction = failure of chromosomes or chromatids to separate during meiosis → gametes with too many or too few chromosomes → aneuploid offspring
  • Down's syndrome risk increases dramatically with maternal age (especially after 35) — because oocytes are arrested in prophase I from birth; older oocytes accumulate errors in meiotic spindle
  • Trisomy = extra chromosome (2n+1). Monosomy = missing chromosome (2n−1). Turner's (45,X) is the only viable monosomy in humans.
Section 7 — Human Genome & Applied Genetics
🗺️
Human Genome Project, Biotechnology & Genetic Disorders
HGP, PCR, recombinant DNA, genetic fingerprinting
▾
Human Genome Project (HGP) — Key Facts
  • International collaborative project — launched 1990, completed 2003
  • Goal: sequence ALL ~3 billion base pairs of the human genome and map all human genes
  • Findings: ~20,000–25,000 protein-coding genes; only ~1.5% of genome codes for proteins; ~98.5% was initially called "junk DNA" (now known to have regulatory roles)
  • India's contribution: CSIR labs sequenced chromosome 1 portion
  • Applications: identifying disease genes, personalised medicine, gene therapy, forensic science
Key Biotechnology Techniques — CDS/NDA Frequency
  • PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction) — amplifies specific DNA sequences millions of times in vitro using thermostable Taq polymerase; invented by Kary Mullis (Nobel 1993); used in forensics, COVID-19 testing, paternity testing, disease diagnosis
  • Gel Electrophoresis — separates DNA fragments by size (smaller fragments move farther through gel toward positive electrode); used in DNA fingerprinting
  • DNA Fingerprinting (DNA Profiling) — uses variable number tandem repeats (VNTRs) / STRs that are unique to each individual; used in forensics, paternity disputes, wildlife crime; developed by Alec Jeffreys (1984, UK); in India, first used in a court case in 1989
  • Recombinant DNA Technology (rDNA) — cutting DNA with restriction enzymes (molecular scissors) and inserting it into a vector (plasmid) → transformed into host cells to produce desired protein. Example: human insulin produced by E. coli (Humulin) — first commercially available rDNA product (1982)
  • CRISPR-Cas9 — revolutionary gene-editing tool; allows precise DNA cutting and editing at specific sites; developed by Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier (Nobel 2020)
  • Gene Therapy — introducing correct copies of a defective gene into a patient's cells; used for certain immunodeficiencies (SCID), haemophilia
Important Autosomal Genetic Disorders Summary
  • Phenylketonuria (PKU) — autosomal recessive; deficiency of phenylalanine hydroxylase → toxic phenylalanine accumulation → intellectual disability; detected by newborn screening; managed by low-phenylalanine diet
  • Cystic Fibrosis (CF) — autosomal recessive; defective CFTR chloride channel gene → thick mucus in lungs, pancreas, GI tract; most common life-limiting autosomal recessive in Caucasians
  • Huntington's Disease — autosomal dominant; CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion in HTT gene → progressive neurodegeneration, onset typically 30–50 years; affected individuals have 50% chance of passing it to each child regardless of sex
  • Albinism — autosomal recessive; deficiency of tyrosinase → no melanin production → white skin, hair, pink eyes; photophobia; high skin cancer risk
  • Thalassaemia — autosomal recessive; reduced/absent globin chain synthesis → haemolytic anaemia; common in Mediterranean, Middle East, India; managed with transfusions + chelation
Mendel's Laws & Ratios
  • Law of Segregation — alleles separate during gamete formation; F₂ phenotypic ratio = 3:1; genotypic = 1:2:1
  • Law of Independent Assortment — genes on different chromosomes assort independently; dihybrid F₂ = 9:3:3:1
  • Test cross: unknown genotype × homozygous recessive (tt)
  • Incomplete dominance: F₂ = 1:2:1 (new intermediate phenotype)
  • Codominance: both alleles expressed simultaneously (e.g., AB blood group)
  • Linkage: genes on same chromosome → Morgan; Drosophila; Nobel 1933
DNA Structure & Central Dogma
  • Watson & Crick (1953) — double helix; Nobel 1962; X-ray data from Rosalind Franklin
  • Base pairs: A–T (2H bonds) · G–C (3H bonds); Chargaff's Rules: %A=%T, %G=%C
  • Purines: Adenine, Guanine (double ring) · Pyrimidines: Thymine, Cytosine (Uracil in RNA)
  • Central Dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein (Transcription → Translation)
  • Replication is semi-conservative (Meselson & Stahl, 1958)
  • Codon = 3 bases; 64 codons for 20 amino acids; AUG = start; UAA/UAG/UGA = stop
Sex-Linked Inheritance
  • Females = XX; Males = XY; X-linked recessive = more males affected (hemizygous)
  • Colour blindness — X-linked recessive; ~8% males; ~0.5% females; also called Daltonism
  • Haemophilia A — Factor VIII deficiency; X-linked recessive; Queen Victoria was a carrier
  • DMD — Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy; dystrophin gene; largest known human gene
  • Affected father → all daughters become carriers; sons get Y from father, NOT X
  • Colour-blind female: father must be colour blind + mother at least carrier
ABO Blood Groups & Rh Factor
  • O = ii (universal donor RBC, no antigens); AB = I^A I^B (universal recipient, no antibodies)
  • ABO = codominance + multiple alleles (I^A, I^B, i); Landsteiner — Nobel 1930
  • Rh+ = RhD antigen present (dominant); Rh− = absent (recessive)
  • Erythroblastosis foetalis: Rh− mother + Rh+ foetus (2nd pregnancy risk)
  • Prevention: Anti-D immunoglobulin (RhoGAM) within 72 hours of delivery
  • Blood group determined by antigens on RBC, NOT antibodies in plasma
Chromosomal Disorders
  • Down's syndrome — Trisomy 21 (47,+21); ↑ risk with maternal age; most common chromosomal disorder in live births
  • Turner's syndrome — 45,X (monosomy X); short stature, webbed neck, infertility; phenotypically female
  • Klinefelter's — 47,XXY; gynecomastia, infertility; phenotypically male
  • Non-disjunction = failure of chromosomes to separate in meiosis
  • Sickle cell anaemia — autosomal recessive; point mutation HBB gene (Glu→Val); protection against malaria in heterozygotes
  • Huntington's = autosomal dominant; onset 30–50 years; trinucleotide repeat
