The Nervous System
Neuron structure, CNS & PNS, brain regions, spinal cord, reflex arc, action potential, synapse and neurological disorders — at CDS, NDA & AFCAT exam depth.
CDS NDA AFCAT Brain Neuron Reflex Arc

The nervous system is the body's rapid communication and control network. It detects stimuli, processes information and coordinates responses. In humans it is divided into the Central Nervous System (CNS) — brain and spinal cord — and the Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) — all nerves outside the CNS. The basic unit is the neuron (nerve cell).

Section 1 — The Neuron
🧠
Neuron — Structure & Types
The structural and functional unit of the nervous system
▾

The neuron (nerve cell) is the structural and functional unit of the nervous system. Unlike most cells, mature neurons in the CNS cannot divide (non-mitotic). The human brain contains ~86 billion neurons.

Parts of a Neuron
  • Cell body (Soma/Cyton) — contains nucleus and most organelles; metabolic centre of the neuron
  • Dendrites — short, branched projections that receive incoming signals and carry them toward the cell body (afferent to soma). A neuron may have many dendrites.
  • Axon (Nerve fibre) — single long process that carries impulse away from cell body. Can be up to 1 metre long (e.g., sciatic nerve axons). Covered by myelin sheath in myelinated neurons.
  • Myelin sheath — fatty insulating layer around the axon; produced by Schwann cells (PNS) or oligodendrocytes (CNS). Speeds up conduction.
  • Nodes of Ranvier — gaps in the myelin sheath; allow saltatory conduction (impulse jumps node to node).
  • Axon terminals (synaptic knobs) — release neurotransmitters into the synapse.
  • Synaptic vesicles — tiny membrane-bound sacs storing neurotransmitters in axon terminals.

⚑ Dendrite vs Axon — Direction of Signal
  • Dendrites → cell body: afferent direction (toward soma)
  • Axon → away from cell body: efferent direction
  • Signals always travel in ONE direction in a neuron: dendrite → cell body → axon. This is the law of dynamic polarisation.
  • Do NOT confuse afferent neurons (sensory, carry signals TO brain) with efferent neurons (motor, carry signals FROM brain).
⚡
Nerve Impulse — Action Potential
How electrical signals travel along a neuron
▾

A nerve impulse is an electrochemical signal — a wave of electrical disturbance travelling along the neuron membrane. It depends on the movement of Na⁺ and K⁺ ions across the membrane.

Resting Membrane Potential

At rest, the inside of the neuron is negatively charged relative to outside (resting potential ≈ −70 mV). This is maintained by the Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase pump, which pumps 3 Na⁺ OUT and 2 K⁺ IN per cycle, keeping Na⁺ concentrated outside and K⁺ concentrated inside.

1
Depolarisation
A stimulus triggers voltage-gated Na⁺ channels to open → Na⁺ rushes INTO the cell → inside becomes positive → membrane potential rises to +35 mV. This is the action potential.
2
Repolarisation
Na⁺ channels close; K⁺ channels open → K⁺ flows OUT → inside becomes negative again → membrane repolarises.
3
Hyperpolarisation & Refractory Period
K⁺ channels close slightly late → membrane overshoots briefly below −70 mV. During this refractory period no new impulse can be generated — ensures one-directional propagation.
4
Saltatory Conduction (Myelinated Fibres)
In myelinated neurons, the impulse jumps from node to node (Nodes of Ranvier) — saltatory conduction — much faster than continuous conduction in unmyelinated fibres.
Key Numbers — Nerve Conduction
  • Resting potential: −70 mV
  • Action potential peak: +35 mV
  • Conduction speed: unmyelinated fibres ~0.5–2 m/s; myelinated fibres up to 120 m/s
  • All-or-nothing law: an action potential either occurs fully or not at all — no partial impulses
⚑ Exam Trap — All-or-Nothing Law

Nerve impulses follow the All-or-Nothing Law — if the stimulus reaches the threshold, a full action potential fires; if it doesn't, nothing happens. The strength of a stimulus is coded by the frequency (rate) of impulses, NOT by the amplitude of each impulse.

🔗
Synapse — Junction Between Neurons
Chemical transmission across the synaptic cleft
▾

A synapse is the junction between two neurons (or between a neuron and an effector). The tiny gap between them (~20–40 nm) is the synaptic cleft. Transmission across the synapse is chemical — via neurotransmitters.

1
Impulse Arrives at Axon Terminal (Pre-synaptic)
Action potential reaches the synaptic knob → voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ rushes in.
2
Neurotransmitter Release
Ca²⁺ influx triggers synaptic vesicles to fuse with the pre-synaptic membrane → neurotransmitters released into the synaptic cleft by exocytosis.
3
Receptor Binding (Post-synaptic)
Neurotransmitters diffuse across cleft → bind to receptors on post-synaptic membrane → generate new impulse (excitatory) or inhibit it (inhibitory).
4
Termination
Neurotransmitter is degraded by enzymes (e.g., acetylcholinesterase breaks down acetylcholine) or reabsorbed into the pre-synaptic terminal → synapse resets.
Key Neurotransmitters — CDS/NDA Frequency
  • Acetylcholine (ACh) — neuromuscular junction; parasympathetic NS; memory and arousal. Degraded by acetylcholinesterase. Inhibition of this enzyme by nerve agents (organophosphates) causes uncontrolled nerve firing.
  • Dopamine — reward, movement, motivation. Deficiency → Parkinson's disease. Excess → associated with schizophrenia.
  • Serotonin (5-HT) — mood, sleep, appetite. Deficiency → depression. SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors) treat depression by increasing synaptic serotonin.
  • Noradrenaline (Norepinephrine) — fight-or-flight response; sympathetic NS; alertness.
  • GABA (γ-aminobutyric acid) — main inhibitory neurotransmitter of CNS. Benzodiazepines enhance GABA action → sedation.
  • Glutamate — main excitatory neurotransmitter of CNS; learning and memory.
⚑ Synaptic Transmission is One-Way

Synaptic transmission is unidirectional — only pre-synaptic → post-synaptic — because neurotransmitters are stored in vesicles only in the pre-synaptic terminal, and receptors are only on the post-synaptic membrane. This ensures signals travel in one direction through neural circuits.

Think of it this way

The sympathetic system is your body's accelerator — it revs everything up for action (fight or flight). The parasympathetic system is the brake — it calms things down for rest and recovery. Most organs have both, maintaining balance (homeostasis).

Sympathetic vs Parasympathetic — Quick Comparison
  • Heart rate: Sympathetic ↑; Parasympathetic ↓ (vagus nerve)
  • Pupils: Sympathetic dilates (mydriasis); Parasympathetic constricts (miosis)
  • Digestion: Sympathetic inhibits; Parasympathetic stimulates (↑ peristalsis, ↑ secretions)
  • Bronchi: Sympathetic dilates (bronchodilation); Parasympathetic constricts
  • Bladder: Sympathetic relaxes (urine retention); Parasympathetic contracts (urination)
  • Sweat glands: Sympathetic only (acetylcholine as transmitter here — exception)
  • Neurotransmitter: Sympathetic post-ganglionic → noradrenaline; Parasympathetic → acetylcholine
Section 3 — The Brain
🧠
Brain — Divisions & Functions
Forebrain, midbrain, hindbrain — each region's role
▾

The human brain weighs approximately 1.4 kg and is protected by the skull (cranium) and three membranes — the meninges (dura mater, arachnoid mater, pia mater). The space between arachnoid and pia mater contains cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).

Cerebrum Forebrain

Largest part (~85%). Two hemispheres connected by corpus callosum. Controls intelligence, memory, reasoning, voluntary movement, speech, emotions. Cerebral cortex = outer grey matter.

Diencephalon Forebrain

Thalamus — relay station; routes sensory info to cortex. Hypothalamus — master regulator; controls temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep, circadian rhythm; controls pituitary gland.

Midbrain Mesencephalon

Controls visual and auditory reflexes (superior and inferior colliculi). Houses reticular activating system (RAS) — controls arousal and consciousness. Connects forebrain and hindbrain.

Cerebellum Hindbrain

"Little brain". Controls coordination, balance and fine muscle movement. Damage → ataxia (inability to coordinate movements). Does NOT initiate movement.

Pons Hindbrain

Bridge between cerebrum and cerebellum. Contains pneumotaxic and apneustic centres (breathing control). Relays signals between higher brain and spinal cord.

Medulla Oblongata Hindbrain

Most vital brain region — controls heart rate, blood pressure, breathing, swallowing, coughing, vomiting. Connects brain to spinal cord. Damage = fatal.

Cerebral Lobes & Their Functions
  • Frontal lobe — voluntary movement, speech (Broca's area), personality, decision-making, reasoning
  • Parietal lobe — sensory perception (touch, pain, temperature), spatial awareness, body position sense (proprioception)
  • Temporal lobe — hearing, language comprehension (Wernicke's area), memory (hippocampus lies beneath)
  • Occipital lobe — vision processing (primary visual cortex)
  • Limbic system (includes amygdala + hippocampus) — emotions, memory formation, motivation
⚑ Brain Region Exam Traps
  • Cerebellum coordinates movement but does NOT initiate it. Initiation of voluntary movement = motor cortex (frontal lobe).
  • Medulla oblongata controls vital involuntary functions — it is the most critical region for survival. Damage here is rapidly fatal.
  • Hypothalamus is the master regulator of homeostasis and controls the pituitary gland (the "master endocrine gland"). It links nervous and endocrine systems.
  • Broca's area (frontal) = speech production; Wernicke's area (temporal) = speech comprehension. Damage to Broca's = cannot speak properly; damage to Wernicke's = cannot understand speech.
Section 4 — Spinal Cord & Reflex Arc
🔌
Spinal Cord — Structure
31 pairs of spinal nerves, grey matter, white matter
▾

The spinal cord extends from the medulla oblongata to the L1–L2 vertebral level in adults (ends as the conus medullaris; below this is the cauda equina — a bundle of nerve roots). It is about 45 cm long and is protected by the vertebral column and meninges.

Spinal Cord — Cross-Section
  • Grey matter (H-shaped, inner) — cell bodies of neurons. Dorsal horn: receives sensory input (sensory neurons synapse here). Ventral horn: houses motor neuron cell bodies that send signals to muscles.
  • White matter (outer) — myelinated axons forming ascending (sensory) and descending (motor) tracts. White colour from myelin.
  • Dorsal root — carries sensory signals INTO spinal cord (contains dorsal root ganglion with sensory neuron cell bodies)
  • Ventral root — carries motor signals OUT of spinal cord to effectors
  • Together, dorsal + ventral roots form the spinal nerve (mixed — both sensory and motor)
  • 31 pairs of spinal nerves: 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal
⚑ Bell-Magendie Law

Bell-Magendie Law: Dorsal root = sensory (afferent); Ventral root = motor (efferent). This is one of the most directly tested facts about the spinal cord. Remember: "Dorsal = Detective senses things" and "Ventral = Vehicle drives out motor signals."

⚡
Reflex Arc — Fast, Involuntary Response
The 5-component pathway of a reflex
▾

A reflex is a rapid, automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus that does NOT require conscious thought. It is mediated through the spinal cord (for spinal reflexes), bypassing the brain for speed. The pathway is the reflex arc.

1
Receptor
Detects the stimulus (e.g., pain from hot object, tap on patellar tendon). Converts stimulus to nerve impulse.
2
Afferent (Sensory) Neuron
Carries impulse from receptor → spinal cord via the dorsal root. Fast conduction, myelinated.
3
Nerve Centre (Interneuron in Spinal Cord)
Processes and integrates signal in the grey matter of spinal cord. A copy of the signal is simultaneously sent up to the brain (we feel pain slightly after we've already pulled away).
4
Efferent (Motor) Neuron
Carries impulse from spinal cord → effector via the ventral root.
5
Effector
Muscle or gland that produces the response (e.g., hand pulls away from heat; quadriceps contracts in knee-jerk reflex).
Common Examples of Reflexes
  • Knee-jerk (Patellar reflex) — monosynaptic (only 1 synapse); tap on patellar tendon → quadriceps contracts. Tests L3–L4 spinal segment integrity.
  • Withdrawal reflex — hand pulled away from pain; polysynaptic (involves interneurons)
  • Pupillary light reflex — bright light → pupils constrict (cranial nerve II and III); tests midbrain integrity
  • Babinski reflex — stroking sole of foot → big toe extends in infants (normal); in adults = sign of upper motor neuron damage
  • Blink reflex — corneal touch → eyelid closes (cranial nerve V and VII)
⚑ Reflex vs Voluntary Action
  • Reflexes do NOT require the brain — they are integrated at the spinal cord level. However, the brain receives a copy of the signal and is aware of the reflex after the fact.
  • The knee-jerk reflex is monosynaptic (NO interneuron) — the only truly 2-neuron reflex arc. All others involve interneurons.
  • Conditioned reflexes (Pavlov's dogs) ARE learned and involve higher brain centres — they are NOT spinal reflexes.

Eye — Structure & Vision
Rods, cones, retina and common eye disorders
▾

The eye is the organ of vision. Light enters through the cornea → aqueous humour → pupil → lens → vitreous humour → retina. The retina contains the photoreceptors.

Eye Anatomy — Key Terms
  • Cornea — transparent, avascular outer layer; refracts most light; site of LASIK surgery
  • Iris — coloured muscular ring; controls pupil size (light intensity)
  • Lens — flexible; changes shape for near/far focus (accommodation); controlled by ciliary muscles
  • Retina — inner layer with rods and cones; where image forms (inverted)
  • Fovea centralis — area of sharpest vision; densely packed cones; no rods
  • Blind spot (Optic disc) — where optic nerve exits; no photoreceptors → no vision here
  • Optic nerve (CN II) — carries visual signals to occipital cortex
  • Aqueous humour (front of lens) and vitreous humour (behind lens) maintain eye shape
⚑ Eye Disorder Exam Traps
  • Night blindness — deficiency of Vitamin A (needed for rhodopsin synthesis) → rods cannot function in low light
  • Colour blindness — X-linked recessive; deficiency in one or more types of cone pigment; more common in males; red-green most common type
  • Glaucoma — increased intraocular pressure (aqueous humour build-up) → optic nerve damage → blindness. Leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide.
  • Cataract — clouding of the lens; leading cause of treatable blindness worldwide; treated by lens replacement surgery
  • Myopia (short-sightedness) — eyeball too long; image forms in FRONT of retina; corrected with concave lens
  • Hypermetropia (long-sightedness) — eyeball too short; image forms BEHIND retina; corrected with convex lens

Key Ear Facts
  • The three ossicles (smallest bones in the body) in order: Malleus (hammer) → Incus (anvil) → Stapes (stirrup)
  • Stapes is the smallest bone in the human body
  • Cochlea — organ of hearing; hair cells in the organ of Corti detect sound vibrations
  • Semicircular canals (3, at right angles) — detect angular/rotational movement; maintain dynamic balance
  • Utricle and Saccule — in inner ear; detect linear acceleration and gravity (static balance)
  • Eustachian tube — connects middle ear to pharynx; equalises air pressure on both sides of eardrum

Pavlov's Conditioned Reflex — Classical Conditioning

Ivan Pavlov demonstrated that animals can be trained to respond to a neutral stimulus (bell) when it is repeatedly paired with a natural stimulus (food → salivation). The conditioned reflex is a learned response involving the cerebral cortex — unlike spinal reflexes. Pavlov received the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1904 for this work on digestion (not reflexes per se, though reflexes made it possible).
