Biology · Chapter B13 · Olive Defence
Human Diseases
Infectious & non-infectious diseases, pathogens, immunity, vaccines and high-yield facts for CDS · NDA · AFCAT
CDS NDA AFCAT Theory 15 MCQs

A disease is any condition that impairs the normal functioning of the body. Diseases are broadly classified as infectious (communicable) — caused by pathogens and transmissible — and non-infectious (non-communicable) — caused by genetic defects, nutritional deficiencies, physiological disorders or environmental factors. Understanding the causative agent, mode of transmission and prevention is essential for every defence exam.

Section 1 — Classification of Diseases
🦠
Types of Diseases — Overview
Infectious vs non-infectious, acute vs chronic, congenital vs acquired
▾

Diseases are classified on several bases. The most important classification for exams is infectious vs non-infectious.

BasisTypeDescription & Examples
Causative AgentInfectiousCaused by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, helminths); transmissible. E.g., TB, malaria, cholera, COVID-19
Non-infectiousNot transmissible; caused by genetic defects, nutritional deficiency, physiological dysfunction, degenerative changes. E.g., diabetes, cancer, anaemia, kwashiorkor
DurationAcuteShort duration, sudden onset, severe symptoms. E.g., influenza, cholera
ChronicLong duration, slow onset, persistent. E.g., tuberculosis, diabetes, asthma
OriginCongenitalPresent at birth; genetic or developmental. E.g., haemophilia, Down syndrome, cleft palate
AcquiredDevelops after birth due to infection, lifestyle or environment. E.g., AIDS, scurvy, malaria
Key Pathogen Categories
  • Viruses — smallest; obligate intracellular parasites; no cells; replicate inside host cells. Examples: Influenza, HIV, Polio, Dengue, Hepatitis B, Rabies, Measles, Smallpox
  • Bacteria — prokaryotes; single-celled; treated with antibiotics. Examples: TB (Mycobacterium tuberculosis), Typhoid (Salmonella typhi), Cholera (Vibrio cholerae), Plague (Yersinia pestis)
  • Fungi (Mycoses) — eukaryotes; treated with antifungals. Examples: Ringworm (Tinea), Athlete's foot, Candidiasis (Candida albicans)
  • Protozoa — unicellular eukaryotes; treated with antiprotozoal drugs. Examples: Malaria (Plasmodium), Amoebic dysentery (Entamoeba histolytica), Sleeping sickness (Trypanosoma), Kala-azar (Leishmania donovani)
  • Helminths (Worms) — multicellular parasites. Examples: Ascariasis (Ascaris lumbricoides), Filariasis (Wuchereria bancrofti), Tapeworm (Taenia)

Section 2 — Bacterial Diseases
🧫
Major Bacterial Diseases
TB, Typhoid, Cholera, Plague, Tetanus, Leprosy — causative agents, vectors, symptoms
▾

Bacterial diseases are caused by prokaryotic pathogens and are generally treatable with antibiotics (unlike viral diseases, which have no antibiotic treatment). Alexander Fleming discovered penicillin in 1928.

DiseaseCausative AgentMode of TransmissionKey Features
Tuberculosis (TB)Mycobacterium tuberculosisAirborne droplets (coughing, sneezing)Affects lungs primarily; Koch's disease; BCG vaccine; DOTS treatment; MDR-TB is drug-resistant
Typhoid (Enteric Fever)Salmonella typhiContaminated food & water (faecal-oral)Rose spots on abdomen; Widal test for diagnosis; fever rises in steps; Typhoid Mary — famous asymptomatic carrier
CholeraVibrio choleraeContaminated water; faecal-oralRice-water stools; severe dehydration; discovered by Robert Koch (1883); ORS is treatment
Plague (Black Death)Yersinia pestisBite of rat flea (Xenopsylla cheopis); rats are reservoirThree forms: Bubonic (swollen lymph nodes/buboes), Pneumonic (lungs), Septicaemic (blood); killed ~50 million in 14th century Europe
Tetanus (Lockjaw)Clostridium tetaniWound contamination; spores in soilToxin causes muscle spasms; jaw locks (trismus); DPT vaccine; not transmitted person-to-person

DPT Vaccine — Triple Protection

The DPT (Diphtheria-Pertussis-Tetanus) vaccine provides combined protection against three bacterial diseases. It is given as part of the Universal Immunisation Programme (UIP) in India. BCG (Bacillus Calmette-Guérin) vaccine protects against tuberculosis and is given at birth under UIP.

⚑ Bacterial Disease Exam Traps
  • Cholera was discovered by Robert Koch (1883) — the same Koch who discovered TB's causative agent. "Koch's disease" refers to TB, not cholera.
  • Plague vector is the rat flea (Xenopsylla cheopis) — NOT mosquito, NOT louse. Rats are the reservoir host.
  • Tetanus spores survive in soil — it is a wound infection, NOT airborne. A rusty nail is a classic trigger.
  • Leprosy is caused by a bacterium (M. leprae), NOT a virus or fungus. It is NOT highly contagious.
  • Typhoid carrier state: a person can carry Salmonella typhi without symptoms (like "Typhoid Mary") — important concept for exam.
Section 3 — Viral Diseases
🔬
Major Viral Diseases
HIV/AIDS, Dengue, Polio, Hepatitis, Rabies, Influenza, Smallpox
▾

Viruses are non-cellular, obligate intracellular parasites. They have no metabolism of their own and hijack host cell machinery to replicate. Antibiotics are ineffective against viruses. Treatment is with antiviral drugs; prevention is mainly through vaccines.

DiseaseVirus / Causative AgentVector / ModeKey Facts
AIDSHIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) — RetrovirusBlood, sexual contact, mother-to-child; NOT casual contactDestroys CD4⁺ T-helper lymphocytes; ELISA test for diagnosis; ART (antiretroviral therapy); discovered 1983 by Montagnier & Barré-Sinoussi (Nobel 2008)
Dengue FeverDengue virus (4 serotypes; Flavivirus)Aedes aegypti mosquito (day-biter)Breakbone fever; thrombocytopenia (low platelets); no specific antiviral; same vector as Chikungunya, Zika, Yellow fever

Poliomyelitis (Polio)Poliovirus (Enterovirus)Faecal-oral; contaminated waterAttacks motor neurons; irreversible paralysis; OPV (Sabin oral) and IPV (Salk injectable) vaccines; India polio-free since 2014
Hepatitis BHBV (Hepadnavirus)Blood, sexual contact, mother-to-childLiver inflammation; can cause cirrhosis and liver cancer; vaccine available; Hepatitis A is faecal-oral route
RabiesRabies virus (Lyssavirus)Bite of infected animal (dog, bat, fox)100% fatal once symptoms appear; attacks nervous system; hydrophobia (fear of water); post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) vaccine effective if given early
SmallpoxVariola virus (Orthopoxvirus)Airborne droplets; contactEradicated globally in 1980 (declared by WHO); last natural case 1977 in Somalia; Edward Jenner developed first vaccine (cowpox, 1796)
InfluenzaInfluenza A/B/C virus (Orthomyxovirus)Airborne dropletsHigh mutation rate (antigenic shift and drift); Pandemic strains: H1N1 (Spanish flu 1918 — killed ~50 million; Swine flu 2009)

HIV / AIDS — High-Yield Details
  • HIV = Human Immunodeficiency Virus; a retrovirus (RNA virus that uses reverse transcriptase to make DNA from RNA)
  • Attacks CD4⁺ T-helper lymphocytes → progressive destruction of immune system
  • AIDS diagnosed when CD4 count <200 cells/μL (normal: 500–1500 cells/μL)
  • Transmission: blood transfusion, sharing needles, unprotected sex, mother-to-child (vertical). Not transmitted by hugging, handshaking, mosquitoes, sharing food/utensils
  • Diagnosis: ELISA test (screening), confirmed by Western Blot
  • Treatment: ART (Antiretroviral Therapy) — controls but does not cure HIV
  • Discovered: 1983 by Luc Montagnier & Françoise Barré-Sinoussi (Institut Pasteur, France) — Nobel Prize 2008

⚑ Viral Disease Exam Traps
  • Dengue vector is Aedes aegypti — a DAY-biting mosquito. Malaria vector (Anopheles) bites at night.
  • Smallpox was eradicated in 1980 (WHO declaration), NOT 1977. Last natural case was 1977; global eradication certified 1980.
  • Polio vaccines: Sabin = oral (OPV); Salk = injectable (IPV). "S" for Sabin = Swallow; "S" for Salk = Shot.
  • Rabies is 100% fatal once clinical symptoms appear — but is preventable with post-exposure vaccine. A bitten person can be saved if vaccinated promptly.
  • Hepatitis A = faecal-oral (dirty water); Hepatitis B = blood/sexual (like HIV). Different viruses, different routes.
  • HIV is a retrovirus — it has RNA as genetic material and uses reverse transcriptase to convert RNA → DNA.
Section 4 — Fungal & Protozoan Diseases
🦟
Fungal, Protozoan & Helminth Diseases
Malaria, Amoebic dysentery, Kala-azar, Filariasis, Ringworm
▾
Protozoan Diseases — High Yield
  • Malaria — Causative agent: Plasmodium spp. (P. falciparum most dangerous — causes cerebral malaria); Vector: female Anopheles mosquito; Symptoms: cyclic fever (48 hrs in P. vivax/ovale, 72 hrs in P. malariae), chills, sweating; Treatment: Chloroquine, Artemisinin combination therapy (ACT); RDT (Rapid Diagnostic Test); Female Anopheles bites at dusk/night; Ross discovered malaria parasite in Anopheles in 1897 (Nobel Prize 1902)
  • Amoebic Dysentery (Amoebiasis) — Agent: Entamoeba histolytica; Route: contaminated food/water (faecal-oral); Symptoms: bloody diarrhoea (dysentery), abdominal cramps; can cause liver abscess; Treatment: Metronidazole
  • Kala-azar (Visceral Leishmaniasis) — Agent: Leishmania donovani; Vector: Phlebotomus (sandfly); Symptoms: irregular fever, enlarged spleen/liver, weight loss; endemic in Bihar, Jharkhand; Treatment: Amphotericin B, Miltefosine
  • Sleeping Sickness (African Trypanosomiasis) — Agent: Trypanosoma brucei; Vector: Tsetse fly; Attacks nervous system → daytime sleepiness, coma; found in sub-Saharan Africa
  • Giardiasis — Agent: Giardia lamblia; Route: contaminated water; Symptoms: fatty, foul-smelling diarrhoea; common in travellers ("Traveller's diarrhoea")
Helminth (Worm) Diseases
  • Ascariasis — Roundworm (Ascaris lumbricoides); most common intestinal worm in humans; contaminated soil/food; larvae migrate through lungs → coughing; treated with Albendazole/Mebendazole
  • Filariasis (Elephantiasis) — Wuchereria bancrofti; vector: Culex mosquito; lymphatic blockage → swelling of limbs/scrotum (elephantiasis); treated with DEC (Diethylcarbamazine)
  • Tapeworm (Taeniasis/Cysticercosis) — Taenia solium (pork) or T. saginata (beef); ingestion of infected undercooked meat; larvae can enter brain (Neurocysticercosis); treated with Praziquantel
  • Hookworm — Ancylostoma duodenale; penetrates skin of bare feet from soil; causes anaemia by sucking blood
  • Pinworm — Enterobius vermicularis; anal itching at night; common in children; faecal-oral spread
Fungal Diseases (Mycoses)
  • Ringworm (Tinea) — Not a worm! Caused by Trichophyton / Microsporum fungi; ring-shaped skin lesions; types: Tinea capitis (scalp), Tinea pedis = Athlete's foot, Tinea corporis (body), Tinea cruris = Jock itch; treated with antifungals (clotrimazole, fluconazole)
  • Candidiasis — Candida albicans; thrush (oral/vaginal); common in immunocompromised patients (e.g., AIDS); treated with Fluconazole/Nystatin
  • Aspergillosis — Aspergillus fumigatus; primarily affects immunocompromised individuals; can invade lungs
Malaria — Life Cycle Essentials
  • Sporozoites injected by mosquito → liver (pre-erythrocytic stage) → merozoites → RBCs (erythrocytic stage) → rupture of RBCs releases toxins → fever episode
  • P. vivax / P. ovale — 48-hour cycle → tertian malaria; can cause relapses (hypnozoites persist in liver)
  • P. malariae — 72-hour cycle → quartan malaria
  • P. falciparum — malignant tertian (most dangerous); blocks capillaries → cerebral malaria, black-water fever
  • Ronald Ross discovered role of Anopheles mosquito in 1897 → Nobel Prize in Medicine, 1902
Disease-Vector Summary
  • Female Anopheles mosquito → Malaria
  • Aedes aegypti mosquito → Dengue, Chikungunya, Zika, Yellow fever
  • Culex mosquito → Filariasis (Elephantiasis), Japanese Encephalitis
  • Xenopsylla cheopis (rat flea) → Plague
  • Phlebotomus (sandfly) → Kala-azar (Leishmaniasis)
  • Tsetse fly → Sleeping Sickness
  • Ixodes tick → Lyme Disease
  • House fly (Musca domestica) → Typhoid, Cholera, Dysentery (mechanical carrier)

Deficiency diseases, diabetes, cancer, cardiovascular, genetic disorders

Genetic / Hereditary Disorders
  • Haemophilia — X-linked recessive; deficiency of clotting factor VIII (Haemophilia A) or IX (Haemophilia B); excessive bleeding; "Royal Disease" — affected Queen Victoria's descendants
  • Sickle Cell Anaemia — autosomal recessive; point mutation in β-globin gene (Val→Glu); RBCs become sickle-shaped; provides partial protection against malaria; common in malaria-endemic regions
  • Thalassaemia — autosomal recessive; reduced/absent globin chain production; microcytic anaemia; requires blood transfusions; common in Mediterranean and South-East Asian populations
  • Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21) — extra chromosome 21 (47 chromosomes); intellectual disability, characteristic facial features; risk increases with maternal age; discovered by John Langdon Down (1866)
  • Turner Syndrome — 45,XO (missing one X chromosome); affects females only; short stature, infertility, webbed neck
  • Klinefelter Syndrome — 47,XXY; affects males; infertility, gynecomastia, tall stature
  • Phenylketonuria (PKU) — autosomal recessive; cannot metabolise phenylalanine (PAH enzyme deficient); intellectual disability if untreated; detected by newborn screening
Vitamin Summary for Exams

Fat-soluble vitamins: A, D, E, K — stored in body fat; overdose possible. Water-soluble: B complex, C — not stored; regular dietary intake needed. Key deficiency diseases: A = night blindness; B₁ = Beriberi; B₃ = Pellagra; B₁₂ = Pernicious anaemia; C = Scurvy; D = Rickets/Osteomalacia; K = Impaired blood clotting.

Section 6 — Immunity, Vaccines & Public Health
💉
Immunity — Innate vs Adaptive; Active vs Passive
How the body defends itself; vaccines, serum, antibodies
▾

Immunity is the ability of the body to resist infection. It has two major branches: Innate (non-specific) immunity and Adaptive (specific/acquired) immunity.

TypeMechanismFeatures
Innate ImmunityPhysical barriers (skin, mucus), fever, inflammation, phagocytes (neutrophils, macrophages), NK cells, complement systemPresent from birth; non-specific (acts against all pathogens); no memory; fast response (immediate)
Adaptive ImmunityT lymphocytes (cell-mediated immunity) and B lymphocytes (humoral immunity — produces antibodies)Specific to each pathogen; has memory (memory cells → faster response on re-exposure); develops after exposure
Active ImmunityBody produces its own antibodies after exposure to antigen (natural infection or vaccination)Long-lasting; memory cells formed; slower to develop; Examples: BCG vaccine, COVID vaccine, MMR
Passive ImmunityPre-formed antibodies transferred from another sourceImmediate but short-lived (no memory cells); Examples: Maternal antibodies (IgG across placenta; IgA in breast milk); anti-rabies serum; anti-tetanus serum (ATS)
Key Vaccines & Discoverers
  • Smallpox vaccine (1796) — Edward Jenner; used cowpox (Vaccinia) to immunise against smallpox; the word "vaccine" comes from vacca (Latin: cow)
  • Rabies vaccine (1885) — Louis Pasteur; first laboratory-made vaccine; also developed vaccines for anthrax and chicken cholera
  • BCG (Bacillus Calmette-Guérin) — Albert Calmette & Camille Guérin (1921); live attenuated M. bovis; protects against TB
  • Polio vaccine — OPV (Oral) — Albert Sabin (1957); live attenuated; oral drops; used in India's pulse polio programme
  • Polio vaccine — IPV (Injectable) — Jonas Salk (1955); killed virus; injectable
  • DPT vaccine — combined diphtheria toxoid + pertussis + tetanus toxoid
  • MMR vaccine — combined Measles + Mumps + Rubella (live attenuated)
  • Hepatitis B vaccine — recombinant DNA technology (first vaccine made by genetic engineering)
India's Universal Immunisation Programme (UIP)
  • Launched 1985; one of the world's largest public health programmes
  • Vaccines at birth: BCG, OPV-0, Hepatitis B (HepB-0)
  • 6, 10, 14 weeks: OPV, Pentavalent (DPT+HepB+Hib), Rotavirus, PCV
  • 9–12 months: MR vaccine (Measles-Rubella)
  • India declared polio-free by WHO on 27 March 2014
  • Mission Indradhanush — intensified immunisation drive from 2014 to increase vaccine coverage
Allergy & Autoimmune Conditions
  • Allergy (Hypersensitivity) — exaggerated immune response to harmless substances (allergens like pollen, dust, food). Mediated by IgE antibodies and mast cells; histamine release → symptoms. Anaphylaxis = severe systemic allergic reaction; treat with adrenaline (epinephrine)
  • Autoimmune Disease — immune system attacks body's own cells (failure of self-tolerance). Examples: Rheumatoid Arthritis (joints), Type 1 Diabetes (β cells), Multiple Sclerosis (myelin sheaths), Lupus (SLE — multiple organs), Graves' disease (thyroid)
  • Immunodeficiency — weakened immune system; can be congenital (SCID — Severe Combined Immunodeficiency) or acquired (AIDS)
⚑ Immunity & Vaccine Exam Traps
  • Jenner used cowpox (Vaccinia virus) to immunise against smallpox (Variola) — not the other way round. Both are related Orthopoxviruses.
  • Pasteur developed the rabies vaccine — NOT the smallpox vaccine. Pasteur also coined "vaccine" as a general term (honouring Jenner).
  • Passive immunity is short-lived because no memory cells are formed. Maternal antibodies (IgG) protect infants for ~6 months.
  • The word "vaccine" derives from vacca (cow) — named after Jenner's use of cowpox by Pasteur.
  • OPV = Sabin (oral/swallowed); IPV = Salk (injectable/shot). Salk's is killed, Sabin's is live attenuated.
  • Hepatitis B vaccine is the first vaccine produced using recombinant DNA technology — NOT a live or killed whole organism.
Section 7 — Drug Abuse & Addiction
⚠️
Drug Abuse — Types, Effects & Consequences
Opioids, cannabinoids, stimulants, hallucinogens, depressants
▾

Drug abuse is the use of substances in a manner that harms health or functioning. Psychoactive drugs act on the central nervous system (CNS), altering mood, perception or behaviour. They cause tolerance (needing more for same effect), dependence (psychological/physical), and withdrawal symptoms on stopping.

CannabinoidsCannabis (marijuana, hashish/charas, ganja)From Cannabis sativa; active ingredient THC (Tetrahydrocannabinol); euphoria, altered perception; affects cardiovascular system; short-term memory impairment
StimulantsCocaine, Amphetamines, Ecstasy (MDMA), NicotineCocaine from Erythroxylum coca; increases dopamine/norepinephrine; increases heart rate, BP; cocaine — "crack cocaine" is smoked form; highly addictive
