Section 1 — Atomic Structure & Atomic Models
⚛️
Atomic Models — Thomson to Bohr
The evolving picture of the atom — and what each model got right
▾

The atom — once thought indivisible — was revealed to have internal structure through a series of landmark experiments in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Each model improved on the last, culminating in the quantum mechanical model used today.

ModelScientist & YearDescriptionLimitation
Plum PuddingJ.J. Thomson, 1904Atom = positive sphere with electrons embedded like plums in pudding. Discovered the electron (1897) using cathode ray tubes.Could not explain Rutherford's scattering experiment — why most α-particles passed straight through
Nuclear ModelRutherford, 1911Atom has a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus; electrons orbit it at a distance. Most of the atom is empty space. Based on gold foil experiment (α-particle scattering).Could not explain atomic stability (orbiting electron should radiate energy and spiral into nucleus) or discrete spectral lines
Bohr's ModelNiels Bohr, 1913Electrons orbit nucleus in fixed quantised orbits (shells/energy levels) without radiating. Electron emits/absorbs a photon when it jumps between orbits. Successfully explained hydrogen spectrum.Only worked for hydrogen (single-electron atoms); could not explain fine spectral structure or multi-electron atoms

Nuclear Structure — Key Numbers for Exams
  • Proton — positively charged; charge = +1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C; mass = 1.672 × 10⁻²⁷ kg; inside the nucleus
  • Neutron — no charge (neutral); mass ≈ proton mass (1.674 × 10⁻²⁷ kg); inside the nucleus; discovered by Chadwick (1932)
  • Electron — negatively charged; charge = −1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C; mass = 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg (≈ 1/1836 of proton); orbits nucleus
  • Atomic number (Z) — number of protons in the nucleus; defines the element
  • Mass number (A) — total number of protons + neutrons (nucleons) in the nucleus
  • Isotopes — atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons (same Z, different A). Example: ¹H, ²H (deuterium), ³H (tritium)
  • Isobars — atoms of different elements with the same mass number A but different Z. Example: ⁴⁰Ca and ⁴⁰Ar
  • Isotones — atoms with the same number of neutrons but different Z and A
Bohr's Model — Key Relations
E_n = −13.6 / n² eV   ·   r_n = 0.529 × n² Å
E_n = energy of electron in nth orbit of hydrogen  ·  n = principal quantum number (1, 2, 3…)
r_n = radius of nth orbit (Bohr radius a₀ = 0.529 Å for n=1)

Ground state (n=1): E₁ = −13.6 eV (most stable; lowest energy)
n=2: E₂ = −3.4 eV  ·  n=3: E₃ = −1.51 eV  ·  n=∞: E = 0 eV (ionised)
Energy to ionise hydrogen from ground state = 13.6 eV
⚑ Atomic Model Exam Traps
  • Thomson discovered the electron (1897); Rutherford discovered the proton (1919); Chadwick discovered the neutron (1932)
  • Rutherford's gold foil experiment: most α-particles passed through (atom is mostly empty space); a few deflected (nucleus is small and dense); very few bounced back (nucleus is positively charged)
  • Bohr's model is valid only for hydrogen-like atoms (H, He⁺, Li²⁺) — single electron systems
  • Isotopes have same Z (same element), different A (different neutrons) — NOT same A different Z (those are isobars)
Section 2 — Radioactivity & Types of Radiation
☢️
Radioactivity — Alpha, Beta & Gamma Radiation
Spontaneous nuclear decay — properties and penetrating power
▾

Radioactivity is the spontaneous disintegration of unstable atomic nuclei with the emission of radiation. It was discovered by Henri Becquerel in 1896, and further studied by Marie Curie and Pierre Curie, who isolated radium and polonium. Radioactive decay is a nuclear process — it is not affected by temperature, pressure, or chemical state of the substance.

Penetrating Power Memory Aid

"Paper stops Alpha, Aluminium stops Beta, only Lead stops Gamma." Think of it as increasing effort needed: A sheet of paper blocks α; a few mm of metal block β; several cm of lead (or concrete wall) barely reduces γ. Paradoxically, alpha is the most ionising despite being the least penetrating — it dumps all its energy very quickly in a small volume.

Alpha & Beta Decay Equations
  • Alpha decay: ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ⁻⁴_(Z−2) Y + ⁴₂He   (Z decreases by 2, A decreases by 4)
  • Beta-minus decay: ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ_(Z+1) Y + ⁰₋₁e + antineutrino   (neutron → proton + electron; Z increases by 1, A unchanged)
  • Beta-plus decay: ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ_(Z−1) Y + ⁰₊₁e + neutrino   (proton → neutron + positron; Z decreases by 1, A unchanged)
  • Gamma emission: No change in Z or A — nucleus just releases excess energy as a γ photon (often follows alpha or beta decay)
⚑ Radioactivity Traps
  • Gamma radiation is NOT deflected by electric or magnetic fields (it is neutral/electromagnetic)
  • Alpha is the most ionising but least penetrating — these are opposite rankings. Gamma is least ionising but most penetrating.
  • Beta particles are deflected more than alpha particles in the same field (much lighter mass)
  • In beta-minus decay, a neutron converts to a proton — the atomic number increases by 1, mass number unchanged. The electron (beta particle) was NOT pre-existing in the nucleus; it is created during decay.
  • Radioactive decay rate is not affected by temperature, pressure, or chemical bonding
Section 3 — Half-Life & Radioactive Decay Law
⏳
Half-Life, Decay Constant & Activity
How fast a radioactive substance decays — the maths of nuclear decay
▾

The half-life (T½) of a radioactive substance is the time taken for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. It is a constant characteristic of each radioactive isotope — it does not depend on the initial amount of material, temperature, or any external conditions.

Radioactive Decay Law
N = N₀ × (1/2)^(t/T½)   ·   N = N₀ e^(−λt)
N = number of nuclei remaining at time t  ·  N₀ = initial number of nuclei
T½ = half-life  ·  λ = decay constant (λ = 0.693/T½)
Activity (A) = rate of decay = λN  ·  Unit: Becquerel (Bq) = 1 disintegration per second
Older unit: Curie (Ci) = 3.7 × 10¹⁰ Bq (activity of 1 gram of radium)
Half-Life Calculation — Step by Step
  • After 1 half-life: N = N₀/2   (50% remains)
  • After 2 half-lives: N = N₀/4   (25% remains)
  • After 3 half-lives: N = N₀/8   (12.5% remains)
  • After n half-lives: N = N₀/2ⁿ   (fraction remaining = 1/2ⁿ)
  • Amount decayed after n half-lives: N₀ − N₀/2ⁿ = N₀(1 − 1/2ⁿ)
Half-Lives of Important Isotopes — Exam Favourites
  • Carbon-14 (¹⁴C) — T½ ≈ 5730 years; used in radiocarbon dating of organic matter
  • Uranium-238 (²³⁸U) — T½ ≈ 4.5 × 10⁹ years; geological age dating
  • Iodine-131 (¹³¹I) — T½ ≈ 8 days; used in thyroid cancer treatment
  • Cobalt-60 (⁶⁰Co) — T½ ≈ 5.27 years; cancer radiotherapy (gamma knife)
  • Radon-222 (²²²Rn) — T½ ≈ 3.8 days; radioactive gas, health hazard in buildings
  • Strontium-90 (⁹⁰Sr) — T½ ≈ 28.8 years; nuclear fallout concern (mimics calcium in bones)
⚑ Half-Life Exam Traps
  • Half-life is a constant — it does not change with initial amount, temperature, or pressure
  • After n half-lives, NOT zero nuclei remain — theoretically radioactive material never fully decays (approaches zero asymptotically)
  • SI unit of radioactivity is the Becquerel (Bq), named after the discoverer; 1 Bq = 1 decay per second. The older Curie (Ci) is still used in medicine.
  • Carbon-14 dating works for objects up to ~50,000 years old — not for geological age (use U-238 for that)
Section 4 — Nuclear Fission, Fusion & Mass-Energy
💥
Nuclear Fission & Fusion — E = mc²
The energy of the nucleus — splitting and joining atoms
▾
Einstein's Mass-Energy Equivalence
E = mc²
E = energy (Joules)  ·  m = mass defect (kg)  ·  c = speed of light = 3 × 10⁸ m/s

Mass defect (Δm) = difference between the mass of separate nucleons and the actual mass of the nucleus. This "missing" mass is converted to binding energy.
Binding energy per nucleon = total binding energy / number of nucleons. Iron-56 has the highest binding energy per nucleon (~8.8 MeV) — most stable nucleus.

ProcessHeavy nucleus splits into two or more lighter nuclei + neutrons + energyTwo light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus + energy
FuelHeavy isotopes: U-235, Pu-239Light isotopes: H-2 (deuterium), H-3 (tritium)
Energy released per reaction~200 MeV per fission of U-235~17.6 MeV per D-T fusion
Energy per kg of fuelVery high; more than chemical~4× more than fission per kg (greatest known energy density)
ConditionsNeutron bombardment; chain reactionExtremely high temperature (≥ 10⁷ K) and pressure — plasma state
Radioactive wasteSignificant — long-lived radioactive productsMuch less — helium is the main product
ApplicationsNuclear power plants; atomic bomb (gun-type/implosion)Sun & stars; hydrogen bomb (thermonuclear bomb); future fusion reactors (ITER)
Chain reactionYes — each fission releases 2–3 neutrons which can trigger more fissionsNot a self-sustaining chain reaction; requires continuous input
Nuclear Fission — Chain Reaction & Critical Mass
  • When U-235 absorbs a neutron, it splits into two smaller nuclei + 2–3 fast neutrons + ~200 MeV energy
  • Chain reaction — released neutrons trigger further fissions; self-sustaining if enough fissile material is present
  • Critical mass — minimum mass of fissile material needed to sustain a chain reaction
  • Subcritical: reaction dies out; Critical: steady reaction (power plant); Supercritical: exponentially growing reaction (bomb)
  • Moderator — slows down fast neutrons to thermal speed (needed for U-235 fission); common moderators: heavy water (D₂O), graphite, ordinary water
  • Control rods — absorb excess neutrons to control reaction rate; made of boron, cadmium, or hafnium
  • Coolant — removes heat from reactor core; water, CO₂, or liquid sodium
Sun's Energy — Proton-Proton Chain
  • The Sun generates energy by nuclear fusion — primarily the proton-proton chain where hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium
  • Net reaction: 4¹H → ⁴He + 2e⁺ + 2ν + 2γ + 26.7 MeV
  • The Sun converts about 4 million tonnes of mass into energy every second (E = mc²)
  • The Sun has enough hydrogen fuel for approximately 5 billion more years
⚑ Fission vs Fusion Exam Traps
  • Nuclear power plants use fission (not fusion) — controlled fission of U-235 or Pu-239
  • Hydrogen bomb uses fusion (triggered by a fission bomb) — thermonuclear weapon
  • Atomic bomb uses fission of U-235 or Pu-239
  • Fusion releases more energy per kg of fuel than fission, but requires extreme conditions — not yet commercially viable
  • ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor) in France is the world's largest fusion experiment
  • Iron-56 is the most stable nucleus (highest binding energy per nucleon) — splitting or fusing iron costs energy rather than releasing it
Section 5 — Photoelectric Effect & Quantum Nature of Light
💡
Photoelectric Effect — Einstein's Nobel Prize
Light as particles — photons and the quantum revolution
▾

The photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons from a metal surface when light of sufficient frequency falls on it. First observed by Hertz (1887), it was explained by Albert Einstein in 1905 using the concept of photons (quantised packets of light energy). Einstein received the Nobel Prize in Physics (1921) for this — NOT for relativity.

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation
KE_max = hf − φ   ·   hf = φ + ½mv²_max
KE_max = maximum kinetic energy of emitted photoelectrons
h = Planck's constant = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
f = frequency of incident light  ·  φ (phi) = work function of metal (minimum energy to eject an electron)
Threshold frequency (f₀): minimum frequency needed; φ = hf₀
Energy of photon: E = hf = hc/λ
Key Observations of Photoelectric Effect
  • No emission below threshold frequency (f₀) — no matter how intense the light, if f < f₀, no photoelectrons are emitted
  • Emission is instantaneous — no time delay, even at low intensity; one photon ejects one electron
  • KE of photoelectrons depends on frequency, NOT intensity — higher frequency → higher KE of electrons
  • Number of photoelectrons depends on intensity — brighter light → more photons → more electrons emitted (but same KE)
  • These observations could NOT be explained by wave theory of light — only the particle (photon) model explains them
Applications of Photoelectric Effect
  • Solar cells (photovoltaic cells) — convert sunlight into electricity
  • Photodiodes and phototransistors — light-sensitive electronic components
  • Light meters (in cameras) — measure light intensity
  • Burglar alarms and automatic doors — light beam interrupted → circuit triggered
  • Night-vision devices — photoelectric effect used to amplify faint light
Why Wave Theory Failed to Explain It

Wave theory predicted: (1) even low-frequency light should eventually eject electrons if intense enough — wrong; (2) there should be a time delay for energy to build up — wrong; (3) brighter light should give electrons more KE — wrong. Einstein's photon model (light comes in discrete packets of energy E = hf) explained all observations perfectly. This was the birth of quantum physics.

⚑ Photoelectric Effect Traps
  • Einstein won the Nobel Prize for the photoelectric effect — NOT for relativity (commonly mistaken)
  • KE of photoelectrons depends on frequency — increasing intensity (brightness) does NOT increase KE, only increases the number of electrons
  • Below threshold frequency, no emission occurs regardless of intensity — a key quantum result
  • Planck's constant h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s — frequently asked in NDA numericals
Section 6 — X-Rays & Their Applications
🔬
X-Rays — Discovery, Production & Uses
Electromagnetic radiation that sees through matter
▾

X-rays were discovered by Wilhelm Röntgen in 1895 — he received the first Nobel Prize in Physics (1901) for this discovery. X-rays are electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths of 0.01–10 nm, lying between gamma rays and ultraviolet in the EM spectrum.

Production of X-Rays — Coolidge X-Ray Tube
  • Electrons are emitted from a heated cathode (filament) by thermionic emission
  • Electrons are accelerated by a high voltage (20,000–100,000 V) towards a metal anode (target, usually tungsten or molybdenum)
  • When high-speed electrons hit the metal target, they rapidly decelerate — energy is emitted as X-rays (Bremsstrahlung radiation — "braking radiation")
  • The target must have: (a) high melting point (tungsten: 3422°C) to withstand heat; (b) high atomic number for efficient X-ray production
  • Energy of X-rays: higher accelerating voltage → shorter wavelength (harder, more penetrating X-rays)
Properties & Applications of X-Rays
  • Travel at speed of light in straight lines; not deflected by electric or magnetic fields
  • High penetrating power — pass through soft tissue but absorbed by dense materials (bone, metal)
  • Cause fluorescence — X-rays cause certain materials to glow (used in X-ray screens)
  • Medical diagnosis — chest X-rays (pneumonia, tuberculosis), dental X-rays, bone fractures, CT (Computed Tomography) scans
  • Cancer treatment (Radiation therapy) — high-energy X-rays destroy cancer cells
  • Security scanning — airport baggage scanners
  • Industrial inspection — detecting cracks and flaws in metal structures
  • Crystal structure analysis (X-ray crystallography) — used to determine the structure of DNA (Watson & Crick used Rosalind Franklin's X-ray images)
⚑ X-Ray Traps
  • X-rays are NOT deflected by electric or magnetic fields (they are electromagnetic radiation, not charged particles)
  • Röntgen discovered X-rays in 1895 — won the first Nobel Prize in Physics (1901)
  • X-rays and gamma rays are both electromagnetic radiation but differ in origin: X-rays come from electron transitions or deceleration; gamma rays come from the nucleus
  • Longer wavelength X-rays = softer (less penetrating) = lower energy; Shorter wavelength = harder (more penetrating) = higher energy

Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Semiconductors & Doping
  • Intrinsic semiconductor — pure semiconductor (pure Si or Ge); very few charge carriers at room temperature; conduction by thermally generated electron-hole pairs
  • Doping — adding tiny amounts of impurity atoms to dramatically increase conductivity
  • N-type semiconductor — doped with pentavalent atom (5 valence electrons: P, As, Sb); extra electron becomes a free charge carrier; majority carriers = electrons; minority = holes
  • P-type semiconductor — doped with trivalent atom (3 valence electrons: B, Al, In, Ga); creates electron holes; majority carriers = holes; minority = electrons
P-N Junction Diode Rectifier

P-type and N-type joined. Forward bias: current flows freely. Reverse bias: no current (acts as off switch). Used to convert AC to DC (rectification).

LED Light

Light Emitting Diode. Emits light when forward biased. Colour depends on band gap of semiconductor (GaAs→infrared, GaP→green/red, GaN→blue). Used in displays, remotes, indicators.

Transistor Amplifier/Switch

3-layer device (NPN or PNP). Acts as: (1) amplifier (small input → large output) or (2) switch (on/off). Foundation of all digital computers and electronics. Invented 1947 (Bell Labs: Bardeen, Brattain, Shockley).

Solar Cell Photovoltaic

P-N junction converts light energy into electrical energy (photoelectric effect). Used in calculators, satellites, rooftop panels, spacecraft power systems.

⚑ Semiconductor Traps
  • Semiconductor resistance decreases with temperature — opposite of metals
  • Silicon is more widely used than Germanium in modern electronics (higher operating temperature, more abundant)
  • N-type semiconductor has extra electrons as majority carriers — it is NOT negatively charged overall (it is neutral)
  • P-type semiconductor has holes as majority carriers — it is NOT positively charged overall (neutral)
  • A diode in reverse bias does not conduct (except at breakdown voltage in Zener diode)
  • Transistor was invented in 1947 — it replaced the bulky, power-hungry vacuum tubes and enabled miniaturisation of electronics
Section 8 — LASER, Special Relativity & Nuclear Technology
🔴
LASER, Special Relativity & Nuclear Applications
Modern technology rooted in 20th-century physics
▾
LASER — Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation

LASER produces a beam of light that is monochromatic (single wavelength/colour), coherent (all photons in phase), and highly collimated (narrow, non-diverging beam). It works by stimulated emission — a photon triggers an excited atom to emit another photon of the same frequency, phase, and direction. Key types include: Ruby laser (first laser, 1960, Theodore Maiman), He-Ne laser (red beam), CO₂ laser (high power, industrial), semiconductor laser diodes (CD/DVD players).

Applications of LASER
  • Medical — eye surgery (LASIK), kidney stone removal (lithotripsy), tumour removal, skin treatments
  • Communications — fibre optic data transmission, long-distance telephony
  • Defence / Military — laser rangefinders, target designators, missile guidance systems, laser weapons (directed-energy weapons)
  • Industry — cutting and welding metals, precision drilling, 3D printing (laser sintering)
  • Consumer — barcode scanners, CD/DVD/Blu-ray players, laser printers
  • Scientific — spectroscopy, holography, laser cooling (trapping atoms)
Special Theory of Relativity — Einstein (1905)
  • Postulate 1: Laws of physics are the same in all inertial (non-accelerating) reference frames
  • Postulate 2: The speed of light in vacuum is constant (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s) for all observers, regardless of the motion of the source or observer
  • Time dilation — a moving clock runs slower; moving objects experience less time (confirmed by atomic clocks on satellites)
  • Length contraction — objects moving at high speed appear shorter in the direction of motion
  • Mass-energy equivalence: E = mc² — matter can be converted to energy and vice versa; the ultimate source of nuclear energy
  • Nothing with mass can reach or exceed the speed of light
Nuclear Technology — India's Programme & Key Facts
  • Homi J. Bhabha — father of India's nuclear programme; founded TIFR (1945) and BARC (1954)
  • BARC (Bhabha Atomic Research Centre, Trombay, Mumbai) — India's premier nuclear research centre
  • Apsara — India's first nuclear reactor (1956, BARC); Asia's first research reactor
  • India's nuclear power plants — use Pressurised Heavy Water Reactors (PHWR); fuel is natural uranium; moderator is heavy water (D₂O)
  • Pokhran-I (Smiling Buddha, 1974) — India's first nuclear test; peaceful nuclear explosion
  • Pokhran-II (Operation Shakti, 1998) — India's second nuclear test; established India as a nuclear weapons state
  • Thorium — India has world's largest thorium reserves (Kerala beach sands); India's three-stage nuclear programme aims to use thorium as fuel in stage 3
⚑ Modern Technology Exam Traps
  • Einstein won his Nobel Prize for the photoelectric effect (1921) — NOT for relativity or E = mc²
  • LASER light is coherent, monochromatic, and collimated — ordinary light is none of these
  • The speed of light (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s) is the universal speed limit — nothing with mass can reach it
  • India's nuclear reactors use heavy water (D₂O) as moderator — not graphite (unlike Russian RBMK reactors like Chernobyl)
  • Homi Bhabha (nuclear) ≠ Vikram Sarabhai (space programme). Sarabhai is the father of India's space programme; Bhabha of nuclear.
